📘 1 · Tintinalli's Emergency Medicine, 9th Ed

Sorting the Benign from the Lethal Headache

"Most headaches in the emergency department are benign, but a small number are the first and only warning of a catastrophe. The clinician's job is not to diagnose migraine — it is to identify the dangerous secondary headache hiding among the primary ones. The history that matters most is not the character of the pain, but its onset: the headache that reaches maximum intensity within seconds — the thunderclap — is subarachnoid haemorrhage until proven otherwise."

Tintinalli's Emergency Medicine, 9th Ed. Headache. McGraw-Hill; 2020.

The dangerous secondary headaches

  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage — thunderclap, "worst headache of my life", ± neck stiffness/LOC
  • Meningitis / encephalitis — fever, neck stiffness, altered mentation, rash
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis — subacute, worse lying flat, seizures, risk factors (pregnancy, OCP, thrombophilia)
  • Space-occupying lesion / raised ICP — progressive, worse in the morning/on Valsalva, papilloedema
  • Giant cell (temporal) arteritis — age >50, jaw claudication, visual loss, tender temporal artery, high ESR/CRP
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma — painful red eye, halos, mid-dilated pupil
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning — headache + multiple household members affected
  • Arterial dissection (carotid/vertebral), pituitary apoplexy, pre-eclampsia/PRES
🚩 Red flags — the "SNNOOP10" way to remember them
  • Systemic symptoms/signs (fever, weight loss) · Neoplasm history · Neurological deficit or altered consciousness
  • Onset sudden/thunderclap · Older age (>50 new headache) · Pattern change / Positional / Precipitated by Valsalva
  • Papilloedema · Progressive · Pregnancy/puerperium · Painful eye · Post-traumatic · immunosuppression (HIV) · analgesic overuse
📗 2 · SAH Pathophysiology (Rosen's / NCS)

Why Aneurysmal SAH Kills in Two Waves

"Aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage kills in two waves. The first is the ictus itself — the sudden rise in intracranial pressure and the risk of early rebleeding, which is lethal and most likely in the first 24–72 hours until the aneurysm is secured. The second wave comes days later: delayed cerebral ischaemia from vasospasm, the reason these patients stay in neurocritical care long after the bleed has stopped."

Rosen's Emergency Medicine, 10th Ed & Neurocritical Care Society guidance on aneurysmal SAH.
🔬 How aneurysmal SAH evolves
TriggerRupture of a cerebral (berry) aneurysm into the subarachnoid space
Sudden ↑ ICP → thunderclap headache, ± transient LOC, meningism

📗 The two waves and the systemic havoc SAH causes
  • Rebleeding is most frequent in the first 24 hours and carries very high mortality — this is why the aneurysm is secured (coiling/clipping) as early as possible, and why blood pressure is controlled before it is secured.
  • Delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI) from vasospasm peaks days 4–14; nimodipine improves neurological outcome (though it does not reliably reverse angiographic spasm). Maintain euvolaemia — old "triple-H" therapy is out; treat DCI with induced hypertension and, if needed, endovascular therapy.
  • Systemic effects: acute hydrocephalus (may need an EVD), seizures, hyponatraemia (cerebral salt wasting or SIADH — do not fluid-restrict CSW), neurogenic pulmonary oedema, and stress ("stunned") cardiomyopathy with troponin rise and ECG changes mimicking ACS.
📗 Grading — communicates severity & prognosis
  • WFNS / Hunt & Hess — clinical grade (conscious level ± deficit); higher grade = worse prognosis.
  • Modified Fisher — the amount and distribution of subarachnoid blood on CT predicts vasospasm/DCI risk.
📋 3 · AHA/ASA 2023 · Ottawa SAH Rule

Diagnosis & Acute Management

Ruling out SAH — CT then LP Ottawa SAH Rule
A non-contrast CT head within 6 hours of headache onset, read by a competent clinician, is >98–99% sensitive for SAH — a normal scan in that window effectively excludes it in the neurologically intact patient.
After 6 hours (or if any doubt): CT then lumbar puncture, looking for xanthochromia (spectrophotometry ideal; RBCs that do not clear tube-to-tube) — perform the LP ≥6–12 h after onset to allow xanthochromia to develop. CT angiography is an alternative pathway in some settings.
The Ottawa SAH Rule identifies alert patients ≥15 y with acute non-traumatic headache who need investigation (age ≥40, neck pain/stiffness, witnessed LOC, exertional onset, thunderclap/instant peak, limited neck flexion) — highly sensitive to select who to work up.
Secure the aneurysm & prevent rebleeding AHA/ASA 2023
Early aneurysm repair (coiling or clipping), ideally within 24–72 h, to prevent rebleeding — endovascular coiling is preferred where the aneurysm is amenable (ISAT).
Until secured, control blood pressure (commonly SBP <~160 mmHg — balance rebleed vs perfusion) with a titratable agent, provide analgesia, and manage acute hydrocephalus with an external ventricular drain if needed. A short course of an antifibrinolytic may be considered pre-securing in selected cases.
Prevent & treat delayed cerebral ischaemia
Oral nimodipine 60 mg every 4 hours for 21 days in all aSAH — improves outcome (give even if it lowers BP; reduce dose/frequency rather than stop, and support BP).
Maintain euvolaemia (not prophylactic hypervolaemia/"triple-H"). Monitor for DCI clinically ± transcranial Doppler/CT perfusion; treat established DCI with induced hypertension and consider endovascular therapy (angioplasty/intra-arterial vasodilators).
🇮🇳 Indian Context

Access to 24/7 neurointervention and neurosurgery is concentrated in tertiary centres — recognise SAH early and transfer promptly, controlling BP and pain en route. Nimodipine is widely available and inexpensive; start it as soon as SAH is confirmed.

Remember the endemic mimics of "headache + fever + neck stiffness": tuberculous and pyogenic meningitis, cerebral malaria, dengue, scrub typhus and neurocysticercosis. A febrile thunderclap still needs SAH excluded, but the differential is broader in India.

🗺 5 · Clinical Flowchart

Acute Severe Headache Pathway

1

Screen for red flags

  • Thunderclap/instant-peak onset, worst-ever, neurological deficit, altered mentation
  • Fever + meningism, papilloedema, age >50 new headache, positional, pregnancy, painful red eye
  • Resuscitate and give empirical antibiotics ± aciclovir immediately if meningitis/encephalitis likely
2

Exclude SAH

  • Non-contrast CT head <6 h from onset → >98–99% sensitive; normal ⇒ SAH effectively excluded (intact patient)
  • >6 h or doubt → CT + LP for xanthochromia (LP ≥6–12 h after onset), or CTA per local pathway
3

SAH confirmed — stabilise & grade

  • Control BP (SBP <~160), analgesia, EVD if hydrocephalus; grade (WFNS/Hunt-Hess, modified Fisher)
  • Urgent neurosurgery/neurointervention referral; start nimodipine
4

Secure the aneurysm

  • Coiling or clipping within 24–72 h (coiling preferred if amenable — ISAT) to prevent rebleeding
5

Neurocritical care — watch for DCI

  • Continue nimodipine 21 days; maintain euvolaemia; monitor for vasospasm/DCI (days 4–14)
  • Treat DCI with induced hypertension ± endovascular therapy; manage hyponatraemia (CSW — don't restrict), seizures, cardiac stunning
⚠️ 6 · Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes in Headache / SAH

❌ Mistake 1 — Treating a thunderclap headache as migraine

A headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds is SAH until excluded — even if it later eases with analgesia. Response to a "migraine cocktail" does not rule out SAH.

❌ Mistake 2 — Trusting a CT done too late without an LP

CT sensitivity for SAH falls after 6 hours. Beyond that window, a normal CT must be followed by LP for xanthochromia (or a defined CTA pathway) — don't discharge on a late-normal CT alone.

❌ Mistake 3 — Doing the LP too early

Xanthochromia takes several hours to develop; an LP done before ~6–12 h after onset may miss it, and a traumatic tap can confuse interpretation. Time the LP and look for a non-clearing RBC count plus xanthochromia.

❌ Mistake 4 — Fluid-restricting hyponatraemia in SAH

Cerebral salt wasting causes hypovolaemic hyponatraemia; restricting fluids worsens hypovolaemia and DCI risk. Maintain euvolaemia and replace sodium (hypertonic saline if needed).

❌ Mistake 5 — Stopping nimodipine because of hypotension

Nimodipine improves outcome and should be continued for 21 days. If it lowers BP, reduce the dose/frequency and support the pressure rather than discontinuing it.

❌ Mistake 6 — Delaying antibiotics/steroids for tests

When bacterial meningitis is likely, give antibiotics (± aciclovir) immediately — do not wait for CT/LP. When giant cell arteritis threatens vision, start high-dose steroids before biopsy.

❌ Mistake 7 — Mistaking SAH cardiac changes for primary ACS

SAH causes neurogenic stress cardiomyopathy with troponin rise, ECG changes and pulmonary oedema. Anticoagulating/thrombolysing this "ACS" would be catastrophic — the brain is the primary problem.

🎓 7 · Exam Pearls — DrNB / MEM / EDIC

Exam Pearls

Q: What is a thunderclap headache, and what must be excluded?
A headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds to a minute. It is subarachnoid haemorrhage until proven otherwise (also consider CVST, dissection, RCVS, pituitary apoplexy).

Q: How sensitive is CT for SAH, and how does timing matter?
Non-contrast CT within 6 hours of onset is >98–99% sensitive; sensitivity declines afterwards, mandating CT + LP (or CTA) beyond that window.

Q: What are you looking for on LP, and when should it be done?
Xanthochromia (and a non-clearing RBC count), best assessed ≥6–12 h after headache onset to allow it to develop.

Q: When and how is the aneurysm secured?
Early (within 24–72 h) by coiling or clipping to prevent rebleeding; endovascular coiling is preferred where feasible (ISAT).

Q: What is the role of nimodipine?
Oral nimodipine 60 mg 4-hourly for 21 days improves neurological outcome in aSAH by reducing delayed cerebral ischaemia (it does not reliably reverse angiographic vasospasm).

Q: How is delayed cerebral ischaemia managed today?
Maintain euvolaemia (not prophylactic triple-H), monitor days 4–14, and treat established DCI with induced hypertension ± endovascular angioplasty/vasodilators.

Q: Why can SAH mimic an MI?
It causes neurogenic stress (stunned) cardiomyopathy with troponin rise, ECG changes and neurogenic pulmonary oedema — the primary pathology is intracranial.

Q: Name the grading scales used in aSAH.
Clinical: WFNS and Hunt & Hess. Radiological (vasospasm risk): modified Fisher scale.
