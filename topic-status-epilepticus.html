Definition & Types

Status Epilepticus (SE) is defined as: (1) a seizure lasting ≥5 minutes, OR (2) two or more discrete seizures without full recovery of consciousness between them.

The older 30-minute definition is obsolete. Seizures lasting >5 minutes are unlikely to stop spontaneously and begin causing neuronal injury — hence immediate treatment.

How Status Epilepticus Works — Pathophysiology

A seizure involves hypersynchronous neuronal firing. Normally, inhibitory mechanisms (GABA) terminate seizures within seconds. In SE, these fail:

The Vicious Cycle

  • Prolonged depolarisation → Ca²⁺ influx → excitotoxicity → neuronal death
  • GABA-A receptor internalisation (downregulation) — this is why benzodiazepines become LESS effective with time → treat early and treat adequately
  • NMDA receptor upregulation → more excitation → harder to stop
  • Systemic consequences: Hypoxia, hyperthermia (temperature rises 1°C every 30 min of SE), hypoglycaemia, lactic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, aspiration

Neuronal Injury Timeline

  • 0–5 min: Compensatory mechanisms active; cerebral blood flow ↑; no neuronal injury
  • 5–30 min: Compensation begins to fail; beginning of excitotoxic injury
  • >30 min: Established SE; neuronal injury; systemic complications; GABA receptors internalised (BZD resistance)
  • >60 min: Severe neuronal death; high morbidity/mortality

🇮🇳 India-Specific — Most Common Causes

Neurocysticercosis (NCC): MOST COMMON cause of new-onset seizures in adults in India (Taenia solium pork tapeworm larvae in brain). CT shows ring-enhancing lesions. Treatment: Albendazole 400 mg BD × 8–30 days + Dexamethasone (to prevent cyst death inflammatory reaction causing worse seizures). Anti-epileptics essential.

Organophosphate SE: Atropine controls muscarinic features; Diazepam (benzodiazepines) is the drug of choice for OP-related seizures. Pralidoxime does NOT stop seizures.

Treatment Algorithm — Time-Based (NCS 2024)

0–5 min — Stabilise

Airway, Breathing, Circulation. O₂ 100% via mask. IV access × 2. Glucose (bedside) → if low give 25g Dextrose IV immediately. Draw blood: glucose, Na, Ca, Mg, LFT, RFT, drug levels, toxicology screen, blood cultures. 12-lead ECG. Positioning: lateral decubitus if no C-spine injury.

5–20 min — First-Line (Benzodiazepines)

Choose ONE route based on access:
IV access available: Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg/dose) — can repeat once after 5 min
No IV: Midazolam 10 mg IM (0.2 mg/kg; max 10 mg) — preferred prehospital (RAMPART trial: IM midazolam = IV lorazepam)
Buccal: Midazolam 10 mg buccal (children, no IV access)
PR: Diazepam 0.5 mg/kg PR (max 20 mg) — less preferred (slow absorption)

20–40 min — Second-Line (If Seizure Persists)

Choose ONE (if BZD failed or insufficient dose given):
Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) over 10 min — preferred (ESETT trial: first-line among 2nd-line agents; safe, no drug interactions)
Sodium Valproate 40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg) over 10 min — avoid in liver disease, pregnancy, mitochondrial disease
Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV at max 150 mg PE/min — cardiovascular monitoring needed; avoid in cardiac arrhythmias

40–60 min — Third-Line (Refractory SE)

Repeat second-line agent OR proceed to anaesthetic agents. Intubate. Start continuous EEG monitoring.

>60 min — Anaesthetic Agents (RSE)

Propofol 1–2 mg/kg IV bolus, then 1–10 mg/kg/hr infusion (titrate to burst suppression on EEG)
Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg bolus, then 0.05–2 mg/kg/hr infusion
Thiopentone/Pentobarbital 3–5 mg/kg bolus, then 1–5 mg/kg/hr infusion (for SRSE)
Ketamine 1.5 mg/kg bolus, then 0.5–5 mg/kg/hr — emerging evidence; NMDA antagonist; may protect against excitotoxicity

Refractory & Super-Refractory SE

Refractory SE (RSE) — After 2 ASMs Failed

RSE = seizures persisting despite adequate doses of 2 antiseizure medications (1 BZD + 1 non-BZD). Requires: ICU admission + intubation + continuous EEG monitoring + anaesthetic agents. Mortality of RSE: 20–40%.

Investigations in RSE — Find the Cause

  • CT head (urgent — exclude structural lesion, herniation)
  • MRI brain (after stabilisation) — encephalitis, cortical dysplasia, PRES
  • LP: CSF WBC, protein, glucose, culture, HSV PCR, autoimmune encephalitis panel
  • EEG: Continuous EEG in all RSE — detect NCSE, guide treatment, assess burst suppression
  • Autoimmune panel: anti-NMDA-R, anti-LGI1, anti-CASPR2, anti-GABA-B, anti-AMPA
  • MRI: FLAIR hyperintensity → encephalitis, stroke, PRES, tumour

EEG-Guided Anaesthesia Target

Goal of anaesthetic agents: achieve burst suppression (brief bursts of activity separated by electrical silence). Maintain for 24–48h, then attempt gradual wean with continuous EEG monitoring.

Empirical Treatment for Unknown Cause

  • IV Thiamine 200 mg before any glucose — if alcoholism or malnutrition suspected
  • Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h — if HSV encephalitis possible (LP/MRI not yet available)
  • Methylprednisolone 1g IV OD × 3–5 days — if autoimmune encephalitis suspected
  • IVIG 2g/kg over 5 days OR Plasma exchange — confirmed autoimmune SE

Indian Context

🇮🇳 Common Presentations & Practical Considerations
  • Drug availability: Lorazepam is not universally available in India — Diazepam 0.15–0.2 mg/kg IV is a reliable alternative (followed by a longer-acting agent). Phenobarbitone (phenobarbital) is widely available and effective as 2nd/3rd line.
  • Levetiracetam vs Phenytoin: Levetiracetam increasingly available; preferred because no cardiac monitoring needed, no drug interactions. Phenytoin still used widely but fosphenytoin is preferred (fewer phlebitis complications).
  • Organophosphate SE: Diazepam is the drug of choice. Use high doses (0.25–0.5 mg/kg) as OP causes cholinergic excess. Don't forget atropine for muscarinic features AND diazepam for central SE component.
  • NCC-associated SE: Start antiseizure drug (levetiracetam or VPA), then treat NCC with albendazole + dexamethasone (reduces cyst inflammation). Don't give albendazole first without steroids — can precipitate severe inflammatory reaction and worsen SE.
  • Thiamine deficiency: Always give thiamine 200 mg IV before dextrose in alcoholic or malnourished patients — prevents Wernicke's encephalopathy.

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — 5 Minutes = Treat

The 30-minute definition is dead. Any seizure ≥5 minutes = Status Epilepticus = start treatment NOW. GABA receptors internalise with time — the longer you wait, the harder it is to stop. Time is neurons.

💡 Pearl 2 — RAMPART: IM Midazolam = IV Lorazepam

RAMPART trial: IM midazolam 10 mg was AS EFFECTIVE as IV lorazepam for out-of-hospital SE — and faster (no IV access needed). Use IM midazolam in ED when IV access is delayed. Anterolateral thigh injection.

💡 Pearl 3 — ESETT: LEV = VPA = FOS

ESETT trial: Levetiracetam, Valproate, and Fosphenytoin are equally effective as 2nd-line agents (~50% success each). Choose based on clinical context: liver disease → LEV; pregnancy → LEV; cardiac → LEV or VPA. All are reasonable first choices.

💡 Pearl 4 — NCSE is Underdiagnosed

20–40% of comatose ICU patients have NCSE on EEG. It looks like delirium, encephalopathy, or "not waking up after seizure." If a patient isn't recovering 30–60 min after a seizure → get EEG. Only EEG can diagnose NCSE.

💡 Pearl 5 — Don't Forget Glucose and Thiamine

Hypoglycaemia causes SE AND prevents response to antiseizure drugs. Check glucose at bedside FIRST. Give 25g Dextrose IV if low. In alcoholics/malnourished: thiamine 200 mg IV BEFORE glucose.

💡 Pearl 6 — Temperature Kills Neurons in SE

Core temperature rises ~1°C every 30 minutes of SE. Hyperthermia directly worsens neuronal injury and makes SE harder to stop. Check temperature, give paracetamol, use cooling measures. This is often overlooked.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Waiting for IV Access Before Treating

IM midazolam 10 mg works as well as IV lorazepam. Don't delay treatment by spending 5 minutes getting IV access. Give IM midazolam NOW, then get access.

❌ Mistake 2 — Underdosing Benzodiazepines

The most common reason BZDs fail SE is underdosing. Lorazepam 1–2 mg is NOT enough — give 4 mg (0.1 mg/kg). Diazepam 5 mg is NOT enough — give 10–20 mg. Give full weight-based doses.

❌ Mistake 3 — Not Starting 2nd-Line Agent After BZD

BZD stops the seizure but doesn't prevent recurrence (short brain duration). ALWAYS start a 2nd-line antiseizure medication (LEV, VPA, or phenytoin) after BZD control, even if the seizure has stopped.

❌ Mistake 4 — Missing NCSE in Comatose Patient

After GCSE, patients should wake up within 20–30 minutes. If still unresponsive after 60 minutes → suspect NCSE or subtle SE. Need EEG to diagnose. Treatment: same as GCSE but no obvious convulsions to guide you.

❌ Mistake 5 — Not Looking for Underlying Cause

SE is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Every patient with SE needs: glucose, Na, Ca, CT head, LP (if CNS infection possible), toxicology screen, EEG. Treating SE without finding the cause = 50% recurrence.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM)

Q: Definition of Status Epilepticus? → Seizure lasting ≥5 minutes OR ≥2 seizures without full recovery between them. (Old 30-minute definition is obsolete.)

Q: First-line treatment of SE? → Benzodiazepine — Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg) if IV access. Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg) if no IV. RAMPART trial confirmed IM midazolam = IV lorazepam.

Q: ESETT trial — what did it show? → Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg, Valproate 40 mg/kg, and Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg are equally effective for established SE (~47–50% success each). No agent was superior.

Q: EEG target in refractory SE on anaesthetic agents? → Burst suppression — brief bursts of electrical activity separated by suppression. Maintain for 24–48h, then attempt wean. Continuous EEG monitoring mandatory in RSE.

Q: Most common cause of new-onset seizures/SE in adults in India? → Neurocysticercosis (NCC) — Taenia solium larvae in brain. CT: ring-enhancing lesions. Treatment: Albendazole + Dexamethasone (reduce inflammation) + antiseizure drugs.
