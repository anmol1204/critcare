Initial Ventilator Settings

Initial Settings — Volume Control AC (Most Common)
  • Mode: Volume Control AC (VC-CMV)
  • Tidal Volume (Vt): 6–8 ml/kg Predicted Body Weight (PBW) — reduce to 6 ml/kg in ARDS
  • Respiratory Rate (RR): 12–16/min; adjust to achieve pH 7.35–7.45
  • FiO₂: Start at 1.0 (100%); titrate down to achieve SpO₂ 92–96% (88–95% in ARDS)
  • PEEP: 5 cmH₂O initial; adjust per oxygenation and ventilator graphics
  • Flow rate: 60 L/min initial (adjust for I:E ratio)
  • I:E ratio: 1:2 (prolong expiration to 1:3–1:4 in obstructive disease)
  • Trigger sensitivity: −1 to −2 cmH₂O (pressure); 1–2 L/min (flow); avoid auto-PEEP

Predicted Body Weight (PBW) — ALWAYS Use This for Vt

  • Male: PBW (kg) = 50 + 2.3 × [Height (inches) − 60]
  • Female: PBW (kg) = 45.5 + 2.3 × [Height (inches) − 60]
  • Quick estimate: Male ≈ Height (cm) − 100; Female ≈ Height (cm) − 105

Troubleshooting — High Airway Pressures

High peak pressure (Ppeak) with normal Pplat → increased airway resistance (secretions, bronchospasm, ETT kink). High Ppeak AND high Pplat → reduced compliance (pneumothorax, ARDS, oedema, main-stem intubation).

ProblemCheckAction

Dyssynchrony / fighting ventilatorPain; agitation; auto-PEEP; wrong mode/settingsAnalgesia first; assess need for sedation; check settings; switch to pressure support if awake

Sudden Haemodynamic Collapse on MV — DOPE Mnemonic

Displacement of ETT (main-stem or accidental extubation) | Obstruction (secretions, tension pneumothorax) | Pneumothorax (tension) | Equipment failure (vent malfunction, disconnection). Disconnect from ventilator and manually bag ventilate first — this immediately diagnoses and treats equipment failure and differentiates from tension pneumothorax.

Preventing Ventilator-Induced Lung Injury (VILI)

  • Low Vt (6 ml/kg PBW): Prevents volutrauma — the cornerstone of lung-protective ventilation
  • Pplat ≤ 30 cmH₂O: Prevents barotrauma
  • Driving pressure < 14 cmH₂O: Best predictor of ARDS mortality — minimise above all other parameters
  • Adequate PEEP: Prevents atelectrauma (repetitive collapse/reopening)
  • Avoid unnecessary NMB: P-SILI (Patient Self-Inflicted Lung Injury) occurs with strong, uncontrolled spontaneous efforts — monitor with oesophageal pressure if available
  • Optimal FiO₂: Minimum FiO₂ to achieve target SpO₂; high FiO₂ causes absorptive atelectasis and oxygen toxicity
  • Permissive hypercapnia: Accept PaCO₂ up to 55–60 mmHg (pH ≥7.25) to achieve lung-protective Vt

Weaning Protocol

Daily Readiness Assessment

CriterionTarget
OxygenationSpO₂ ≥ 92% on FiO₂ ≤ 0.5 and PEEP ≤ 8 cmH₂O; P/F ≥ 150
HaemodynamicsStable — no escalating vasopressors in last 24h
ConsciousnessAwake, following commands (RASS −1 to +1)
Respiratory effortCapable of spontaneous breathing
Cause of intubationResolving or resolved
Secretion managementEffective cough or can manage secretions

Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)

  1. Screen with SAT first (see Sedation topic) — pass SAT before SBT
  2. SBT method: Pressure Support 5–7 cmH₂O + PEEP 5 cmH₂O; OR T-piece trial for 30–120 minutes
  3. SBT success: SpO₂ ≥92%; RR <35/min; HR stable (±20% baseline); no distress; accessory muscle use minimal
  4. RSB Index (RSBI): RR ÷ Vt (in litres) — RSBI <105 predicts successful extubation (Yang & Tobin criteria)
  5. SBT failure: Desaturation, RR >35, marked distress, arrhythmia → restart MV, increase support, investigate cause
🇮🇳 Weaning Challenges in Indian ICUs
  • High prevalence of malnutrition — nutritional supplementation is critical for respiratory muscle recovery
  • ICU-acquired weakness (ICUAW): early physiotherapy; neuromuscular assessment before extubation
  • Tracheostomy threshold: lower threshold for tracheostomy in patients with GBS, bulbar palsy, poor cough, or chronic lung disease when >7–10 days MV anticipated

Extubation

Pre-Extubation Checklist

  • Passed SBT of ≥30–120 minutes
  • Adequate consciousness (GCS ≥8, follows commands)
  • Strong cough (ask patient to cough — audible and forceful)
  • Secretion management — not suctioning >q2h for secretions
  • Cuff leak test: deflate cuff, patient can phonate or air leaks — confirms glottic opening (especially important if intubated >72h or history of airway trauma)
  • Upper airway assessment — no significant stridor

Post-Extubation Management

  • Nebulised adrenaline: 1 mg in 5 ml saline if post-extubation stridor
  • IV dexamethasone: 0.1 mg/kg q6h for 24h if laryngeal oedema risk (prolonged intubation, trauma)
  • HFNO: Apply post-extubation in patients at high risk of failure — reduces re-intubation rate (OPTIFLOW studies)
  • NIV: For COPD post-extubation, hypoxaemic patients, obesity — reduces re-intubation
  • Nil by mouth for 30–60 min; speech therapy assessment before oral intake

NIV Failure — When to Intubate

Intubate without delay if: SpO₂ <88% despite NIV; RR >35/min; haemodynamic instability; worsening GCS; patient unable to tolerate mask; no improvement in 1–2h of NIV. Early intubation when NIV failing is safer than delayed intubation in a deteriorating patient.
