📘 1 · Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed

Washington Manual Summary

"Mechanical ventilation is a life-sustaining intervention, not a therapeutic modality. The goal is to support gas exchange while the underlying cause of respiratory failure is treated. The ventilator should be set to minimize harm — not to normalize blood gases."

Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed. (Kollef & Witt, eds.) Wolters Kluwer 2023.

📗 2 · Marino's The ICU Book, 5th Edition (2025)

Marino Physiology — How Ventilators Work

"Only one of the 174 different modes of ventilation has shown evidence of improved clinical outcomes, and only because it limits the lung damage produced by positive pressure ventilation. What this means is that mechanical ventilation is much more complicated than it needs to be, and that 'less is better' with this form of support."

Marino PL. The ICU Book, 5th Ed. Chapter 27: Conventional Mechanical Ventilation, p.481. Wolters Kluwer; 2025.

Step 1 — Volume Control vs Pressure Control (Marino, p.481–484)

📗 Marino Ch.27, p.481–484

Volume Control (VCV): Delivers a PRESELECTED tidal volume at constant flow rate. Airway pressure varies with lung mechanics. Peak pressure = pressure to overcome airway resistance (Pres) + elastic recoil (Pel). Plateau pressure = Palv = Pel ONLY (no flow during inspiratory hold). Major advantage: Guarantees Vt regardless of lung mechanics — essential for lung-protective ventilation.

Pressure Control (PCV): Delivers a preselected inspiratory pressure with decelerating flow. Vt varies with compliance. Major advantage: Better patient comfort, less dyssynchrony. Major disadvantage: Vt changes with compliance — risk of overdistension if compliance improves suddenly.

Marino (p.484): "The risk of alveolar rupture is negligible if the alveolar pressure (plateau pressure) is ≤30 cm H₂O" — this is the basis of lung-protective ventilation.

Step 2 — The Plateau Pressure (Marino Fig.27.2)

📗 Marino Ch.27, p.483 — Inflation Hold Manoeuvre

During a 0.5-second inspiratory hold (no flow): pressure drops from Peak (Ppeak) to Plateau (Pplat). This is the key measurement:

  • Ppeak − Pplat = Pres (airway resistance pressure) — elevated in bronchospasm, ETT secretions, kinked tube
  • Pplat = alveolar pressure (Palv) — elevated in ↓ compliance (ARDS, pulmonary oedema, pneumothorax, abdominal hypertension)
  • High Ppeak + normal Pplat = airway resistance problem (suction, bronchodilators)
  • High Ppeak + high Pplat = lung compliance problem (reduce Vt, treat cause)

Step 3 — Dynamic Hyperinflation and Auto-PEEP

📗 Marino Ch.23, p.416–418 (applies to all obstructive disease on MV)

In patients with severe airflow obstruction (asthma, COPD), exhalation is prolonged and may not complete before the next breath. Trapped gas creates intrinsic PEEP (auto-PEEP). This increases intrathoracic pressure → impedes venous return → hypotension; AND makes patient work harder to trigger each breath.

Marino: "If you want to feel what it is like to breathe in a hyperinflated state, take a deep breath, and try breathing from there."

Detection: Expiratory flow waveform not reaching zero before next breath. Measurement: Expiratory hold manoeuvre. Treatment: Increase expiratory time (↓ RR, ↑ inspiratory flow rate, ↓ I:E ratio to 1:3 or 1:4).

📋 3 · Guidelines & Evidence

Guideline Recommendations

ARDSNet (2000) — Lung-Protective Ventilation Strong · High certainty
Tidal Volume 6 ml/kg PBW vs 12 ml/kg: absolute mortality reduction 8.8% (31% vs 39.8%). Pplat ≤30 cmH₂O. This remains the most impactful ventilation trial ever. All patients on MV should be considered for lung-protective settings.
Amato (NEJM 2015) — Driving Pressure Strong
Driving Pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP) is the single best predictor of ARDS mortality. ΔP >15 cmH₂O independently associated with death. Optimise PEEP and Vt to minimise ΔP below 14 cmH₂O.
Yang & Tobin (NEJM 1991) — Weaning/SBT Prediction Strong
RSBI (Rapid Shallow Breathing Index) = RR ÷ Vt (litres). RSBI <105 predicts successful SBT/extubation. RSBI >105 = high risk of failure. Measure at start of SBT on minimal support (PS 5 + PEEP 5).
ACCP/ERS/ESICM — Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT) Strong
Daily SBT (paired with SAT): PS 5–7 + PEEP 5 cmH₂O × 30–120 min. Success: SpO₂ ≥92%, RR <35, HR stable, no respiratory distress. Failure: extubate → NIV or HFNO post-extubation. SBT superior to SIMV weaning (Brochard 1994).
💊 4 · Initial Settings & Key Calculations

Ventilator Settings & Calculations

Initial Settings — Volume Control AC (VCV-AC) — Standard Approach
  • Mode: Volume Control AC (guarantees Vt regardless of compliance)
  • Tidal Volume: 6–8 ml/kg PBW (start 8, reduce to 6 in ARDS)
  • Respiratory Rate: 12–16/min; adjust to pH 7.35–7.45
  • FiO₂: Start 1.0; titrate down to SpO₂ target
  • PEEP: 5 cmH₂O initial (higher in ARDS per PEEP/FiO₂ table)
  • Flow Rate: 60 L/min (increase to 80–100 in obstructive disease)
  • I:E ratio: 1:2 standard; 1:3 to 1:4 in COPD/Asthma
  • Trigger: Pressure −1 to −2 cmH₂O OR flow 1–2 L/min

IBW and Tidal Volume — Must Know

DOPE Mnemonic — Sudden Deterioration on Ventilator

Emergency — If Patient Suddenly Deteriorates on MV: DOPE
  • Displacement — ETT displaced (main-stem intubation or accidental extubation) → auscultate, confirm position
  • Obstruction — secretions blocking ETT → suction; bronchospasm → bronchodilators
  • Pneumothorax — tension (tracheal deviation, unilateral absent breath sounds, haemodynamic collapse) → NEEDLE DECOMPRESSION 2nd ICS MCL
  • Equipment failure — circuit disconnection, ventilator malfunction → DISCONNECT & MANUALLY BAG VENTILATE (bag-valve-mask)
🗺 5 · Clinical Flowchart

Mechanical Ventilation — Step by Step

1

Intubation Decision & RSI

GCS ≤8 OR P/F <150 on HFNO after 1h OR haemodynamic collapse. RSI: Ketamine 1.5–2 mg/kg IV (preferred in haemodynamic instability; bronchodilator) + Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg IV (reverse with sugammadex). Confirm ETT with ETCO₂ + CXR.

2

Set Initial VCV-AC Settings

Calculate PBW. Set Vt 8 ml/kg PBW (reduce to 6 in ARDS). RR 12–16/min. FiO₂ 1.0 (reduce ASAP). PEEP 5 cmH₂O. Flow 60 L/min. I:E 1:2 standard (1:3–1:4 in obstructive disease). Trigger −1 cmH₂O or 1–2 L/min flow.

3

First ABG at 30–60 min — Check Pplat

Inspiratory hold 0.5s → Pplat. If Pplat >30: reduce Vt by 1 ml/kg q2h (min 4 ml/kg). Increase RR to compensate. Calculate ΔP = Pplat − PEEP. Target ΔP <14. Adjust FiO₂ to SpO₂ 88–95%. If auto-PEEP: increase expiratory time.

4

ARDS: Lung-Protective Protocol (ARDSNet)

  • 6 ml/kg PBW (reduce from 8 over 2–4h)
  • Pplat ≤30, ΔP <14
  • PEEP per FiO₂/PEEP table
  • If P/F <150 → PRONE ≥16h
  • SpO₂ 88–95%; permissive hypercapnia pH ≥7.25
5

Weaning Assessment (Daily)

Screen: P/F ≥150, PEEP ≤8, FiO₂ ≤0.5, haemodynamically stable, awake + follows commands, adequate cough. Pass SAT first. Then SBT: PS 5 + PEEP 5 × 30–120 min. RSBI <105 = likely extubation success.

6

Extubation & Post-Extubation Care

Cuff leak test if intubated >72h. Extubate: have dexamethasone 0.1 mg/kg q6h × 24h ready for stridor. HFNO post-extubation reduces re-intubation in high-risk patients (Hernandez 2016). NIV for COPD post-extubation (ERS/ATS).

⚠️ 6 · Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Using Actual Body Weight for Vt in Obese Patients

A 100 kg woman at 163 cm has PBW ~58 kg. Vt at actual weight (100 kg): 600 ml. Vt at PBW (58 kg): 348 ml. Using actual weight = 72% overdistension = volutrauma = worsens respiratory failure. ALWAYS calculate PBW before connecting patient to ventilator.

❌ Mistake 2 — Not Checking Pplat — Only Watching Peak Pressure

Peak pressure ≠ alveolar pressure. In bronchospasm: Ppeak can be 50 with Pplat 20 (safe alveoli). In ARDS: Ppeak 30 with Pplat 28 (dangerous alveoli). Marino (p.484): "The risk of alveolar rupture is a function of the alveolar pressure (plateau pressure)." Check Pplat with every Vt change.

❌ Mistake 3 — High RR in COPD/Asthma (Causes Auto-PEEP)

Fast RR = shorter expiratory time = incomplete exhalation = auto-PEEP = dynamic hyperinflation = haemodynamic collapse. In obstructive disease: use RR 10–14/min, I:E 1:3–1:4, high flow rates (60–100 L/min) to maximise expiratory time. If sudden hypotension: disconnect from ventilator, allow passive decompression.

❌ Mistake 4 — Setting FiO₂ 1.0 Indefinitely

O₂ toxicity from sustained high FiO₂: lung injury identical to ARDS. Marino (Ch.24, p.431): critically ill patients are deficient in protective antioxidants. Titrate FiO₂ down to achieve SpO₂ 88–95% as fast as possible. FiO₂ >0.60 for >24–48h = toxicity risk.

❌ Mistake 5 — Not Doing Daily SAT + SBT

Kress (NEJM 2000): daily sedation vacation reduced ICU stay and mechanical ventilation duration. The combined SAT+SBT protocol (Girard, NEJM 2008): reduced 1-year mortality. Every ventilated patient deserves a daily extubation readiness assessment. Document why if not done.

🎓 7 · Exam Pearls — DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM

Exam Pearls

Q: Marino 5th Ed — what did he say about 174 modes of ventilation?
"Only one of the 174 different modes of ventilation has shown evidence of improved clinical outcomes, and only because it limits the lung damage produced by positive pressure ventilation." (Ch.27, p.481). The one mode: Volume Control at 6 ml/kg PBW (ARDSNet). Less is better.

Q: How do you measure Pplat and what does it represent?
0.5-second inspiratory hold with NO active breathing by patient. Pressure settles to Pplat = alveolar pressure (Palv) = elastic recoil pressure. Target ≤30 cmH₂O. Ppeak − Pplat = airway resistance pressure (Pres) per Marino Equation 27.3.

Q: IBW calculation for a 170 cm male?
Height = 170 cm ÷ 2.54 = 66.9 inches. PBW = 50 + 2.3 × (66.9 − 60) = 50 + 15.9 = 65.9 kg. Vt at 6 ml/kg = 395 ml. Quick bedside: 170 − 100 = 70 kg (overestimates slightly but useful).

Q: RSBI — what is it, how to calculate, threshold?
Rapid Shallow Breathing Index = RR ÷ Vt (in litres). Measured at start of SBT on PS 5 + PEEP 5. RSBI <105 = likely successful extubation (Yang & Tobin, NEJM 1991). RSBI >105 = high failure risk. Most accurate predictor of SBT outcome.

Q: What happens haemodynamically with auto-PEEP?
Auto-PEEP (intrinsic PEEP) in obstructive disease: ↑ intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return → ↓ cardiac output → hypotension. Can mimic tension pneumothorax. Diagnosis: disconnect from ventilator briefly → if BP recovers, auto-PEEP was the cause. Fix: ↓ RR, ↑ expiratory time.

Q: DOPE mnemonic — sudden deterioration on ventilator?
D = Displacement (ETT wrong position) | O = Obstruction (secretions, bronchospasm) | P = Pneumothorax (tension) | E = Equipment failure (disconnection, malfunction). First action: disconnect and manually bag ventilate. This simultaneously diagnoses equipment failure AND provides immediate rescue breathing.
