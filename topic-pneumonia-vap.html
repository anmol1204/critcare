Pathological stateRespiratory failure ± septic shock (ARDS if severe)

Route of Infection

  • Micro-aspiration: Dominant mechanism in both CAP and VAP. Colonised oropharyngeal secretions bypass lower airway defences → alveolar infection
  • Macro-aspiration: Reduced consciousness (stroke, sedation, seizures, anaesthesia), dysphagia, GORD → large volume aspiration → aspiration pneumonitis/pneumonia
  • Haematogenous: Bacteraemia → seeding of lungs (less common; suggests source elsewhere)
  • Inhalation: Legionella (aerosolised water), TB, viruses, Aspergillus (immunocompromised)

VAP — Specific Mechanism

ETT bypasses all upper airway defences (cough, mucociliary clearance, IgA, lysozyme). Subglottic secretions pool above the ETT cuff → leak past the cuff → micro-aspiration into lungs → infection. The ETT also provides a biofilm surface for bacteria to colonise. Every day of intubation = 1–3% cumulative VAP risk.

Host Defence Failure in ICU

  • Sedation → ↓ cough, ↓ mucociliary clearance
  • Malnutrition → ↓ immune function
  • Prior antibiotics → colonisation with MDR organisms
  • Hyperglycaemia → impaired neutrophil function

CPIS ≥6 = VAP probable. But IDSA 2016: do NOT rely on CPIS alone — use clinical + radiological + microbiological criteria together.

VAP Diagnosis — IDSA/ATS 2016

  • New/progressive pulmonary infiltrate on CXR (in a ventilated patient ≥48h)
  • PLUS at least TWO of: Fever (>38°C) | Leukocytosis/leukopenia | Purulent tracheal secretions
  • Microbiological confirmation: BAL (≥10⁴ CFU/ml) or protected specimen brush (≥10³ CFU/ml) or endotracheal aspirate (≥10⁵ or ≥10⁶ CFU/ml)
  • Blood cultures (positive in only 15% of VAP)
  • Procalcitonin: useful for de-escalation (rising PCT = not responding to treatment)

Empirical Antibiotic Regimens for HAP/VAP

IDSA/ATS 2016 — Key Principle: Cover MDR Risk Factors

The choice of empirical antibiotic depends on MDR risk factors: prior IV antibiotics in last 90 days | hospitalisation ≥5 days | high unit MDR prevalence | septic shock | ARDS | acute renal replacement therapy | shock before VAP onset.

VAP Prevention Bundle (ISCCM / CDC / IHI)

VAP Bundle — All 5 Elements Together Reduce VAP by 60–70%
  • 1. Head-of-Bed Elevation: 30–45° at all times (unless contraindicated — spinal injury, haemodynamic instability) — prevents aspiration of gastric contents
  • 2. Daily Sedation Vacation + Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SAT + SBT): Reduces ventilator days → reduces VAP risk
  • 3. Oral Decontamination: Chlorhexidine 0.12% oral rinse BD — reduces oropharyngeal colonisation → reduces VAP rate
  • 4. Subglottic Suction: Continuous aspiration of subglottic secretions (CASS) — ETT with subglottic suction port reduces early-onset VAP (NNT = 7)
  • 5. DVT & Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis: Reduces complication burden; H₂ blockers preferred over PPI (PPIs raise gastric pH → increased colonisation; however difference in VAP rates disputed)

Additional Prevention Strategies

  • Avoid unnecessary reintubation — failed extubation ↑ VAP risk 8-fold
  • Ventilator circuit changes: Only change when visibly soiled (NOT on a schedule)
  • Avoid routine tracheal suction — suction only when clinically indicated
  • Avoid gastric overdistension: Keep tube feed residuals <500 ml; prokinetics (metoclopramide 10 mg IV TDS) if high residuals
  • Hand hygiene: Most important infection control measure
  • Minimize PPIs: Consider ranitidine instead (less gastric pH elevation)

Duration & De-escalation

IDSA/ATS 2016 — 7 Days is Adequate for Most HAP/VAP

A fixed 7-day course is adequate for most patients with HAP/VAP who are clinically responding (Strong recommendation). Do NOT extend automatically to 14 days. Longer courses increase C. difficile, AMR selection, and adverse effects without benefit (CONSORT-VAP trial).

De-escalation Protocol

  • Review cultures at 48–72h — narrow to organism-directed therapy
  • Procalcitonin-guided de-escalation: PCT <0.5 ng/ml OR >80% decrease from peak → safe to stop (PRORATA trial)
  • Clinical response parameters: Fever resolution, ↓ WBC trend, improving oxygenation (↓ FiO₂ requirement), reducing secretions
  • ASSESS on day 3 and day 7 — do NOT continue antibiotics automatically without reassessment

Indian Context — MDR Nightmare

🇮🇳 Indian ICU — MDR Reality
  • India has among world's highest rates of CRAB (Carbapenem-Resistant A. baumannii), CRPA (Carbapenem-Resistant Pseudomonas), CRKP (Carbapenem-Resistant Klebsiella), and MRSA in ICUs
  • CRAB (XDR Acinetobacter) is near-universally resistant to all agents except colistin, tigecycline, and newer agents (cefiderocol, sulbactam-durlobactam)
  • Colistin is a last resort — nephrotoxic; use with meropenem synergistically. Inhaled colistin for VAP in addition to systemic
  • Ceftazidime-avibactam (CAZ-AVI): available in India; effective against KPC-producing Klebsiella and some CRPA; very expensive
  • Know YOUR ICU's antibiogram — empirical choices should be guided by local resistance data, not international guidelines

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — HCAP is Dead

IDSA/ATS 2016 abolished the HCAP (Healthcare-Associated Pneumonia) category. It led to overuse of broad-spectrum antibiotics without improving outcomes. Now use only CAP, HAP, or VAP. Risk-stratify within HAP/VAP by MDR risk factors.

💡 Pearl 2 — 7 Days, Not 14

IDSA/ATS 2016 Strong recommendation: 7 days for HAP/VAP who are clinically responding. 14-day courses = more AMR selection, more C. difficile, more adverse effects, no better outcomes. Stop at day 7 in responders.

💡 Pearl 3 — Subglottic Suction ETT

Use ETTs with subglottic suction ports (Hi-Lo Evac, Mallinckrodt) in patients expected to be ventilated >48h. Continuous aspiration of subglottic secretions reduces VAP rate by ~50% (NNT=7). Should be standard of care.

💡 Pearl 4 — Linezolid for MRSA-VAP?

IDSA 2016: Either vancomycin OR linezolid for MRSA-VAP. ATTAIN trial showed linezolid superior for clinical cure in MRSA-VAP (likely due to better lung penetration). Linezolid 600 mg BD is a reasonable choice for MRSA-VAP, especially when vancomycin is nephrotoxic.

💡 Pearl 5 — Blood Culture in HAP/VAP

Blood cultures are positive in only 15% of VAP. But they're important — bacteraemia from a different source (e.g., PICC line, abdominal) may mimic VAP. Always draw 2 blood culture sets BEFORE antibiotics in HAP/VAP.

💡 Pearl 6 — India: Know Your Antibiogram

International guidelines assume a certain resistance landscape. Indian ICUs have MUCH higher XDR prevalence. Your unit's antibiogram (sensitivity patterns of isolates over last 3–6 months) should guide empirical choices more than IDSA guidelines.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Using HCAP Classification

HCAP was abolished in IDSA/ATS 2016. It led to unnecessary broad-spectrum antibiotic use in patients who had CAP. A nursing home patient with CAP should be treated as CAP, with risk stratification for MDR only if risk factors are present.

❌ Mistake 2 — Extending Antibiotics to 14 Days Routinely

IDSA/ATS 2016 Strong: 7 days is adequate. Longer courses increase C. difficile infection, AMR selection, adverse effects (nephrotoxicity, hearing loss with aminoglycosides). Stop at day 7 if clinically improving.

❌ Mistake 3 — Not Changing ETT Position for CXR Evaluation

Supine AP CXR in ICU is notoriously unreliable for HAP/VAP infiltrates — atelectasis, effusion, and cardiomegaly can mask or mimic infiltrates. Always correlate CXR with clinical findings and ideally get an upright or at least semi-upright film.

❌ Mistake 4 — Treating Tracheal Colonisation as VAP

A positive tracheal culture ALONE is NOT VAP. All intubated patients have tracheal colonisation within 48h. Treat VAP only when: new infiltrate on CXR + clinical signs (fever, WBC change, purulent secretions, worsening oxygenation) + positive culture at diagnostic threshold.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM)

Q: IDSA/ATS 2016 duration of antibiotics for VAP? → 7 days (Strong recommendation) for clinically responding patients. NOT 14 days routinely.

Q: VAP diagnosis criteria? → New/progressive infiltrate on CXR + ≥2 of: Fever >38°C, Leukocytosis/leukopenia, Purulent secretions + Microbiological confirmation (BAL ≥10⁴ or ETA ≥10⁵–10⁶ CFU/ml)

Q: Which antibiotic for MRSA-VAP? → Vancomycin 25 mg/kg IV (AUC-guided) OR Linezolid 600 mg BD IV. Both are IDSA-recommended. Linezolid may have better lung penetration.

Q: VAP bundle — 5 elements? → (1) HOB 30–45° (2) Daily SAT+SBT (3) Chlorhexidine oral decontamination (4) Subglottic suction (5) DVT+SUP prophylaxis

Q: CURB-65 score — components? → Confusion | Urea >7 mmol/L | RR ≥30 | SBP <90 or DBP <60 | Age ≥65. Score ≥3: hospital admission ± ICU.
