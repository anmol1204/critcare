📘 1 · Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed

Overview — The Spectrum of Acute PE

"Acute pulmonary embolism is the third most common cause of cardiovascular death after myocardial infarction and stroke. The clinical spectrum ranges from asymptomatic incidental emboli to sudden cardiac death from massive obstruction. The single most important determinant of early mortality is not the anatomical clot burden but the haemodynamic consequence — right ventricular dysfunction. Management is therefore stratified by risk, not by the size of the clot on imaging."

Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed. Wolters Kluwer 2023. Chapter: Venous Thromboembolism.

Why RV Function Drives Everything

The thin-walled right ventricle is exquisitely sensitive to acute increases in afterload. When >30–50% of the pulmonary vascular bed is obstructed, pulmonary artery pressure rises abruptly. The unconditioned RV cannot generate a mean PA pressure above ~40 mmHg acutely — beyond this it dilates, fails, and the resulting spiral of RV ischaemia → reduced LV preload → systemic hypotension → reduced coronary perfusion → worsening RV ischaemia is the lethal pathway in massive PE.

Classification by Haemodynamic Severity

🔴 High-risk (Massive)

SBP <90 mmHg for >15 min, or drop ≥40 mmHg, or need for vasopressors / cardiac arrest. Mortality >15% (up to 50% if arrest). Thrombolysis indicated.

🟠 Intermediate (Submassive)

Normotensive BUT RV dysfunction (echo/CT) AND/OR raised troponin/BNP. Intermediate-high = both positive. Mortality 3–15%. Monitor; rescue lysis if deteriorates.

🟢 Low-risk

Normotensive, no RV dysfunction, normal biomarkers, sPESI = 0. Mortality <1%. Anticoagulation; consider early discharge / outpatient.

📗 2 · Marino's The ICU Book, 5th Edition (2025)

Marino Physiology — Dead Space, Hypoxaemia & the ECG

🔬 How PE causes obstructive shock
TriggerVenous thrombus (DVT) embolises to the pulmonary arteries
Mechanical obstruction + vasoactive mediators → ↑ pulmonary vascular resistance
Gas exchange: alveolar dead space + low V̇/Q̇ unitshypoxaemia with hypocapnia; A–a gradient widens
Haemodynamics: ↑ RV afterloadRV dilatation & strain (S1Q3T3, TWI V1–V4)
RV failure → septal bowing → ↓ LV preload → ↓ cardiac output
Pathological stateObstructive shock → haemodynamic collapse

"Pulmonary embolism produces a region of lung that is ventilated but not perfused — the definition of alveolar dead space. Yet the hallmark of acute PE is hypoxaemia, which seems paradoxical for a dead-space disease. The explanation lies in the redistribution of blood flow and the opening of low V̇/Q̇ units, intracardiac shunting through a patent foramen ovale, and a fall in mixed venous oxygen saturation from the low cardiac output state."

Marino PL. The ICU Book, 5th Ed. Venous Thromboembolism. Wolters Kluwer; 2025.
📗 Marino — Why the A–a Gradient Widens

Acute PE classically produces hypoxaemia with a widened A–a gradient and a respiratory alkalosis (tachypnoea-driven hypocapnia). Mechanisms of hypoxaemia:

  • V̇/Q̇ mismatch — overperfusion of non-embolised lung creates low V̇/Q̇ units
  • Low mixed venous O₂ — the low cardiac output extracts more O₂ peripherally, so blood returning to the lung is more desaturated
  • Right-to-left shunt — raised RA pressure can open a PFO (present in ~25% of adults)

Pitfall: A normal PaO₂ does NOT exclude PE — up to 40% of patients with proven PE have a normal PaO₂, and ~20% have a normal A–a gradient. Never rule out PE on blood gas alone.

📗 Marino — ECG Signs of RV Strain

The ECG is neither sensitive nor specific but offers prognostic clues. Sinus tachycardia is the most common finding. Signs of acute RV strain (correlate with severity):

  • S1Q3T3 — classic but present in only ~20%; deep S in I, Q wave + inverted T in III
  • T-wave inversion V1–V4 — the most common "strain" sign; reversible with treatment
  • New incomplete/complete RBBB
  • Right axis deviation, P pulmonale
  • Atrial fibrillation — new AF in ~5–10%
📗 Marino — The "Reverse" of the D-dimer Logic

D-dimer has high sensitivity but poor specificity. Its value is in ruling OUT PE in low/intermediate pre-test probability — a normal D-dimer with a low Wells score essentially excludes PE (negative predictive value >99%). It has NO role in high pre-test probability (go straight to CTPA) and is frequently falsely positive in sepsis, malignancy, pregnancy, post-op, and the elderly. Use age-adjusted D-dimer (age × 10 µg/L for age >50) to improve specificity.

📋 3 · Diagnosis & Risk Stratification

From Suspicion to Stratified Diagnosis

Step 1 — Assess Haemodynamic Stability FIRST
If the patient is shocked / hypotensive with suspected PE → do NOT wait for CTPA if too unstable to travel. Bedside echocardiography showing RV dilatation/dysfunction in a shocked patient is sufficient to justify reperfusion (ESC 2019). If stabilised, confirm with CTPA. ESC 2019 Class I
Step 2 — Pre-Test Probability (Wells Score)
Clinical signs of DVT +3 · PE most likely diagnosis +3 · HR >100 +1.5 · Immobilisation/surgery in 4 wks +1.5 · Previous DVT/PE +1.5 · Haemoptysis +1 · Malignancy +1
Two-level: ≤4 = PE unlikely (do D-dimer); >4 = PE likely (do CTPA).
PERC rule (in low-risk ED patients): if all 8 criteria negative, PE excluded without D-dimer.
Step 3 — Confirm: CTPA (first-line)
CT Pulmonary Angiography is the diagnostic gold standard. V̇/Q̇ scan if contrast contraindicated (renal failure, contrast allergy) or in pregnancy (lower breast radiation, esp. with normal CXR). Bilateral leg compression ultrasound — a proximal DVT in a patient with symptoms suggestive of PE confirms VTE and may avoid CTPA.
Step 4 — Prognosticate: sPESI + RV Assessment
Simplified PESI (sPESI) — 1 point each: Age >80 · Cancer · Chronic cardiopulmonary disease · HR ≥110 · SBP <100 · SpO₂ <90%.
sPESI = 0 → low-risk (30-day mortality ~1%). sPESI ≥1 → assess RV: echo (RV/LV ratio >1.0, McConnell's sign, TAPSE <16 mm) + troponin + BNP.
Intermediate-HIGH = RV dysfunction AND positive troponin → highest risk of decompensation; admit to monitored bed.
McConnell's Sign

Echocardiographic finding relatively specific for acute PE: akinesia of the RV mid-free wall with preserved motion of the RV apex ("apical sparing"). Suggests acute (rather than chronic) RV pressure overload. Also look for a dilated RV (RV/LV ratio >1), septal flattening (D-sign), dilated IVC, and a tricuspid regurgitation jet allowing PASP estimation.

💊 4 · ESC 2019 Treatment — Reperfusion & Anticoagulation

Evidence-Based Management by Risk Category

High-risk (Massive) PE — Systemic Thrombolysis
Alteplase (tPA) 100 mg IV over 2 hours is the standard regimen ESC 2019 Class I. Accelerated 0.6 mg/kg over 15 min (max 50 mg) in cardiac arrest / peri-arrest.
Start/continue unfractionated heparin (easier to reverse, titratable) around thrombolysis. Hold the UFH infusion during the tPA bolus, restart when aPTT <2× normal.
If thrombolysis contraindicated or failed → surgical embolectomy or catheter-directed therapy.
Intermediate-high Risk — Monitor, Don't Routinely Lyse
Anticoagulate and admit to a monitored bed. Do NOT give routine systemic thrombolysis ESC 2019 — PEITHO trial: full-dose lysis in submassive PE reduced decompensation but DOUBLED major bleeding and tripled haemorrhagic stroke, with no mortality benefit.
Rescue reperfusion if haemodynamic deterioration occurs.
Catheter-directed therapy (reduced-dose CDT / mechanical thrombectomy) increasingly used as a middle path — consider via a PE Response Team (PERT).
Low / Intermediate-low Risk — Anticoagulation
DOAC first-line (apixaban or rivaroxaban — no bridging needed) ESC 2019 Class I, CHEST 2021.
Apixaban 10 mg BD × 7 days → 5 mg BD. Rivaroxaban 15 mg BD × 21 days → 20 mg OD.
Dabigatran / edoxaban require ≥5 days of initial parenteral heparin first.
LMWH preferred in cancer-associated VTE (or a DOAC if no GI/GU tumour), pregnancy (DOACs contraindicated), and severe renal failure (use UFH if CrCl <15–30).
🇮🇳 Indian ICU Context

Tenecteplase (TNK) is widely available and cheaper than alteplase in India; though off-label for PE, it is frequently used (single weight-based bolus). Alteplase remains the guideline-endorsed agent.

Streptokinase (250,000 IU loading over 30 min → 100,000 IU/h × 12–24h) is a low-cost option still used in resource-limited settings, though more allergenic and less used globally.

Catheter-directed therapy and ECMO availability is limited to tertiary centres — early transfer/PERT activation matters. VA-ECMO can bridge a peri-arrest massive PE to embolectomy or lysis.

⚠️ Absolute Contraindications to Thrombolysis

Prior intracranial haemorrhage · Known structural cerebrovascular lesion / intracranial malignancy · Ischaemic stroke within 3 months · Active bleeding · Recent brain/spinal surgery · Recent significant closed head trauma · Bleeding diathesis. In life-threatening massive PE with cardiac arrest, contraindications become relative — the alternative is death.

🗂 6 · Clinical Flowchart — Suspected Acute PE

Step-by-Step Management Algorithm

1

Is the patient haemodynamically unstable?

  • YES (SBP <90 / shock / arrest): Bedside echo. RV dysfunction + high suspicion → treat as high-risk. CTPA only if can be stabilised to travel.
  • NO: Proceed to risk-based pathway (step 2).
  • Resuscitate: cautious fluids (250–500 mL — over-filling worsens RV failure), noradrenaline first-line, add dobutamine for low output, O₂.
2

Stable: Wells score → D-dimer or CTPA

  • PE unlikely (Wells ≤4): age-adjusted D-dimer → if negative, PE excluded.
  • PE likely (Wells >4) or D-dimer positive: CTPA (V̇/Q̇ if contrast contraindicated).
  • Start empirical anticoagulation while awaiting imaging if delay expected and bleeding risk low.
3

PE confirmed: Risk-stratify

  • sPESI + RV assessment (echo/CT RV/LV ratio) + troponin + BNP.
  • Intermediate-high (RV dysfunction + raised troponin): monitored bed, watch for deterioration.
  • Low-risk (sPESI 0, normal biomarkers): consider early discharge / outpatient DOAC (Hestia criteria).
4

Treat & plan duration

  • Reperfusion for high-risk; anticoagulation for the rest.
  • Provoked by major transient risk factor (surgery): 3 months. Unprovoked / persistent factor / recurrent: extended/indefinite, reassess bleeding risk annually.
  • Consider thrombophilia workup and occult malignancy screen in unprovoked VTE; IVC filter ONLY if anticoagulation absolutely contraindicated.
🛡 7 · DVT Prophylaxis in the ICU

VTE Prophylaxis — Every ICU Patient, Every Day

Critically ill patients are at high VTE risk (immobility, sepsis, central lines, vasopressors). Prophylaxis should be a daily checklist item.

Pharmacological Prophylaxis (first-line)
LMWH: Enoxaparin 40 mg SC OD (reduce to 20–30 mg if CrCl <30) — preferred CHEST 2021
UFH: 5000 U SC BD–TDS — for severe renal failure (CrCl <30) or imminent procedures (shorter half-life)
Dose adjustment / anti-Xa monitoring in extremes of weight.
Mechanical Prophylaxis
Intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) when pharmacological prophylaxis is contraindicated (active bleeding, severe thrombocytopenia <50, high bleeding risk). Combine IPC + pharmacological in the highest-risk patients (major trauma, post-neurosurgery once safe). Graduated compression stockings alone are inferior and avoided in peripheral arterial disease.
When to Hold Prophylaxis

Platelets <50 ×10⁹/L · Active bleeding · INR >1.5 (untreated) · Within 24h of high-bleeding-risk surgery · Epidural catheter (time around insertion/removal). Use IPC in the interim and reassess daily — prophylaxis is too often forgotten when temporarily held.

❌ 8 · Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes in PE Management

❌ Mistake 1 — Aggressive Fluid Loading in RV Failure

The instinct to give large fluid boluses to a hypotensive PE patient is harmful. An already-dilated, failing RV is volume-overloaded; further filling worsens RV distension, increases wall tension, bows the septum into the LV (reducing LV filling), and drops cardiac output further. Give a cautious 250–500 mL challenge at most; reach for noradrenaline early.

❌ Mistake 2 — Ruling Out PE on a Normal PaO₂ / SpO₂

Up to 40% of patients with proven PE have a normal PaO₂ and ~20% a normal A–a gradient. A normal SpO₂ provides false reassurance. Risk-stratify with a validated score and D-dimer/imaging — never the blood gas alone.

❌ Mistake 3 — Routine Full-Dose Thrombolysis for Submassive PE

PEITHO showed full-dose lysis in intermediate-risk PE doubles major bleeding and triples haemorrhagic stroke with no mortality benefit. Reserve systemic lysis for high-risk (haemodynamically unstable) PE or as rescue therapy on deterioration; consider catheter-directed therapy as a middle path.

❌ Mistake 4 — Using a DOAC in Antiphospholipid Syndrome or Pregnancy

DOACs are inferior to warfarin in triple-positive antiphospholipid syndrome (higher arterial thrombosis) and are contraindicated in pregnancy. Use LMWH (pregnancy) or warfarin (APS). DOACs are also avoided in severe renal failure and at extremes of body weight without monitoring.

❌ Mistake 5 — Forgetting Age-Adjusted D-dimer

A fixed D-dimer cut-off (500 µg/L) is frequently exceeded in older patients without PE, driving unnecessary CTPAs. Use the age-adjusted cut-off (age × 10 µg/L above 50 years) to safely increase specificity in the low/intermediate probability group.

❌ Mistake 6 — Reflex IVC Filter Placement

IVC filters do not replace anticoagulation and carry their own thrombotic complications. They are indicated only when anticoagulation is absolutely contraindicated (e.g. active major bleeding) in a patient with acute proximal VTE. Retrieve the filter once anticoagulation can be safely started.
