Definitions — Sepsis-3 (2016)

Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. It is operationalised as an acute change in SOFA score ≥ 2 points attributable to infection, associated with a hospital mortality >10%.

Septic Shock is a subset of sepsis in which underlying circulatory, cellular and metabolic abnormalities are profound enough to substantially increase mortality. Clinical criteria: vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥ 65 mmHg AND serum lactate > 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Hospital mortality exceeds 40%.

SIRS-based definitions are obsolete

The Sepsis-3 consensus (2016) replaced SIRS-based definitions. SIRS criteria are non-specific and not validated for organ dysfunction. The SOFA score is now the standard for identifying sepsis-related organ dysfunction.

Pathophysiology

Dysregulated Immune Response

Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) activate pattern-recognition receptors (Toll-like receptors, NOD-like receptors), triggering a cytokine storm — TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8. This leads to:

  • Systemic endothelial activation and increased vascular permeability
  • Dysregulated coagulation (DIC in severe cases)
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction — cytopathic hypoxia
  • Microcirculatory failure despite adequate macrocirculatory flow

Haemodynamic Phases

  • Hyperdynamic (warm shock): High cardiac output, low SVR, warm extremities, bounding pulse — classic early distributive physiology
  • Hypodynamic (cold shock): Sepsis-induced cardiomyopathy (30–50%), decreased CO, peripheral vasoconstriction — late or severe disease

Organ Dysfunction Cascade

The SOFA score captures dysfunction across 6 organ systems. Renal: afferent arteriolar vasodilation reduces GFR. Pulmonary: ARDS develops in ~40%. Hepatic: cholestasis, coagulopathy. Neurological: septic encephalopathy via microglial activation. Adrenal: relative adrenal insufficiency (CIRCI) in refractory shock.

Sepsis = SOFA ≥ 2 from baseline in a patient with suspected/confirmed infection.

Quick SOFA (qSOFA) — Bedside Screening Only

Score 1 point each for: RR ≥ 22/min, Altered mentation (GCS <15), SBP ≤ 100 mmHg. qSOFA ≥ 2 → suspect sepsis, confirm with full SOFA. Not a diagnostic criterion — a triage tool.

Lactate

Serum lactate ≥ 2 mmol/L indicates cellular hypoperfusion. Lactate > 4 mmol/L = high-risk population regardless of blood pressure. Serial lactate measurement guides resuscitation — target clearance ≥ 10% per 2 hours (SSC 2021, strong recommendation).

Hour-1 Bundle (SSC 2021)

Complete all 5 within 1 hour of sepsis recognition
  • 1. Measure lactate — re-measure if initial > 2 mmol/L to assess clearance
  • 2. Blood cultures — minimum 2 sets (aerobic + anaerobic) before antibiotics; do NOT delay antibiotics
  • 3. Broad-spectrum antibiotics — within 1 hour for septic shock; within 3 hours for sepsis without shock
  • 4. Crystalloid 30 ml/kg IV — if hypotensive (SBP <90 or MAP <65) or lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L
  • 5. Vasopressors — if MAP < 65 mmHg during or after fluid resuscitation; target MAP ≥ 65 mmHg
🇮🇳 ISCCM 2020 — Indian Context

The ISCCM Sepsis Guidelines 2020 endorse the SSC bundle with these modifications for resource-limited settings:

  • Blood cultures before antibiotics — if unavailable, do not delay antibiotics
  • Procalcitonin guidance for de-escalation where available
  • Generic antibiotics acceptable when branded unavailable
  • Peripheral vasopressors acceptable for 6–12 hours while central access is secured

Fluid Resuscitation

Initial Resuscitation

Give 30 ml/kg IV crystalloid within first 3 hours. Reassess after each bolus with dynamic parameters. Do not continue fluid blindly — reassess with PLR, PPV, or IVC collapsibility after initial bolus.

Fluid Choice

  • Balanced crystalloids (Ringer's Lactate, PlasmaLyte) — preferred over 0.9% NaCl to reduce hyperchloraemic acidosis and AKI risk (SMART trial, SALT-ED trial)
  • 0.9% Normal saline — acceptable if balanced crystalloids unavailable
  • Albumin — consider in patients requiring large volumes of crystalloid; may reduce noradrenaline requirements (SAFE trial). SSC 2021: weak recommendation.
  • Hydroxyethyl starches (HES) — CONTRAINDICATED in sepsis. Associated with increased AKI and mortality (6S trial, CHEST trial)
  • Gelatin — insufficient evidence; use with caution
Fluid Stewardship — Avoid Fluid Overload

Fluid overload (>10% body weight gain) independently worsens outcome. After initial resuscitation, use dynamic parameters to guide further fluid administration. Target neutral to negative fluid balance from 24–48 hours. Judicious diuresis to avoid cumulative positive balance.

MAP target: 65–70 mmHg for most patients. Higher target (80–85 mmHg) only for known hypertensives with early AKI who tolerate higher MAP (SEPSISPAM trial) — no overall mortality benefit but reduced RRT requirement in hypertensives.

Avoid Dopamine

SOAP II (2010): Dopamine vs noradrenaline — more arrhythmias with dopamine and higher mortality in cardiogenic shock subgroup. Do not use dopamine as first-line vasopressor in septic shock.

Antimicrobial Therapy

Timing

Every hour delay in antibiotic administration in septic shock is associated with ~7% increase in mortality. Administer within 1 hour of septic shock recognition and within 3 hours of sepsis recognition.

De-escalation & Duration

  • Review at 48–72 hours: narrow spectrum based on culture/sensitivity (SSC 2021, strong recommendation)
  • Procalcitonin-guided de-escalation reduces duration safely (PRORATA, SAPS trials)
  • Duration: 7 days is adequate for most infections with good source control and clinical response
  • Shorter courses (5 days) acceptable for uncomplicated pyelonephritis/pneumonia with good response

Source Control

Identify and control the infectious source as early as clinically feasible — drain abscess, remove infected devices, debride necrotic tissue. Delay in source control is an independent predictor of mortality. Reassess every 12 hours if not improving.

Adjunctive Therapies

Corticosteroids

Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day (50 mg IV q6h or 200 mg/24h continuous infusion) is recommended in septic shock not responding to adequate fluid resuscitation and vasopressors (SSC 2021, weak recommendation). 2B

  • APROCCHSS (2018): Hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone → reduced 90-day mortality in septic shock
  • ADRENAL (2018): Hydrocortisone → faster shock reversal, no difference in 90-day mortality
  • Do NOT use ACTH stimulation test to guide steroid use
  • Wean hydrocortisone when vasopressor requirements are decreasing
  • Add Fludrocortisone 50 mcg PO/NG daily if using hydrocortisone (APROCCHSS protocol)

Glycaemic Control

Target blood glucose 140–180 mg/dL (7.8–10 mmol/L) using insulin infusion protocol. Avoid hypoglycaemia (<80 mg/dL) — independently associated with mortality. Tight control 80–110 mg/dL shown to cause harm (NICE-SUGAR, 2009). 1A

Blood Products

  • RBC: Restrictive threshold — transfuse when Hb <7 g/dL (Hb <9 only for acute coronary ischaemia). TRISS trial: no harm from restrictive strategy. 1B
  • FFP: Only for active bleeding or invasive procedures with coagulopathy. Not for prophylactic correction of INR.
  • Platelets: <10,000/µL prophylactically; <20,000/µL if active bleeding risk; <50,000/µL for procedures

Vitamin C

High-dose IV Vitamin C (1.5 g q6h) is NOT recommended as routine therapy. CITRIS-ALI (2019) showed no benefit. VITAMINS trial (2020): Vitamin C + hydrocortisone + thiamine did not improve vasopressor-free days. (SSC 2021: weak recommendation against)

DVT & Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis

  • DVT: LMWH preferred; mechanical prophylaxis if anticoagulation contraindicated
  • Stress ulcer: Pantoprazole 40 mg IV OD for high-risk (MV >48h, coagulopathy, prior GI bleed, steroids). Avoid routine use in low-risk.

Indian-Specific Considerations

🇮🇳 Indian Society of Critical Care Medicine (ISCCM) 2020
  • Antimicrobial resistance: India has one of the world's highest rates of ESBL and carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae. Empirical carbapenem use may be needed earlier in hospitalised patients.
  • Malaria & dengue: Always consider as cause of sepsis-like presentation in India — do rapid antigen test and NS1/IgM dengue in appropriate epidemiological settings.
  • Leptospirosis: Consider in monsoon season with fever, jaundice, and AKI — add Doxycycline 100 mg BD or Penicillin G.
  • Enteric fever (typhoid): Salmonella typhi/paratyphi can present as septic shock — add Ceftriaxone 2g IV OD empirically if suspected.
  • Peripheral vasopressors: In resource-limited settings, peripheral line for vasopressors (wrist/antecubital) acceptable for up to 12 hours with frequent site inspection (infiltration risk).
  • Central venous access: Internal jugular vein preferred over subclavian (pneumothorax risk in coagulopathic patients). Ultrasound guidance strongly recommended.
