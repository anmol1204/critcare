How Sepsis Works — The Pathophysiology

Understanding the mechanism is the foundation of everything. If you know why the body does what it does in sepsis, every treatment decision becomes logical.

The Trigger — Pattern Recognition

Infection releases Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns (PAMPs) — components of bacterial cell walls (LPS, peptidoglycan, flagellin) and fungal walls (β-glucan). Tissue damage releases Damage-Associated Molecular Patterns (DAMPs) — HMGB-1, heat shock proteins, mitochondrial DNA. Both bind to Toll-like receptors (TLRs) on macrophages, monocytes, and dendritic cells → activates NF-κB transcription factor → cytokine storm.

The Storm — Cytokine Release

TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-12, and IFN-γ are released. This leads to:

  • Endothelial activation: Upregulation of adhesion molecules → neutrophil extravasation → capillary leak → third-spacing of fluid
  • Vasodilation: Macrophage release of iNOS → nitric oxide → profound vasodilation → ↓ SVR (the dominant haemodynamic lesion)
  • Microvascular dysfunction: Microthrombi + vasodilation = cellular hypoxia despite normal macrocirculation
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction: Direct inhibition of oxidative phosphorylation → cytopathic hypoxia — cells cannot use oxygen even when delivered

Organ Failure Cascade

  • Kidney: Afferent arteriolar dilation → ↓ GFR → AKI (most common organ failure in sepsis)
  • Lung: ARDS in ~40% — neutrophil-mediated alveolar damage, capillary leak
  • Liver: Kupffer cell activation → cholestasis, ↑ LFTs, synthetic failure (coagulopathy)
  • Brain: Septic encephalopathy — microglial activation, BBB disruption, neurotransmitter changes (↑ glutamate, ↓ GABA)
  • Adrenals: Relative adrenal insufficiency (CIRCI) in 30–50% of septic shock — baseline cortisol <15 or poor response to ACTH
  • Coagulation: TF expression on macrophages → widespread microvascular coagulation → DIC in severe cases

Definitions — Sepsis-3 (2016, retained in SSC 2026)

Sepsis: Life-threatening acute organ dysfunction due to infection. Operationalised as: suspected/confirmed infection + acute SOFA score increase ≥2 from baseline. Hospital mortality: ~10–15%.

Septic Shock: Subset of sepsis with circulatory and cellular/metabolic dysfunction. Clinical criteria: vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg AND lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Hospital mortality: >40%.

SSC 2026 — New Sepsis Terminology

The 2026 guidelines introduce standardised language for clinical uncertainty: Definite sepsis (confirmed infection + organ dysfunction), Probable (likely infection + OD), Possible (uncertain infection + OD), Unlikely. This language is used in all 2026 recommendations.

Screening — SSC 2026 Updated

SSC 2026 Statement 4 — Strong Recommendation

For acutely ill patients in hospital, use NEWS, NEWS2, MEWS, or SIRS over qSOFA as a single screening tool. (Strong recommendation, moderate certainty evidence)

Why not qSOFA? Four systematic reviews showed that Early Warning Scores (NEWS/MEWS/SIRS) are more sensitive for sepsis diagnosis than qSOFA. qSOFA was designed as a prognostic tool, not a screening tool.

qSOFA (for Prognostication only — NOT screening)

Score 1 each: RR ≥22/min | Altered mentation (GCS <15) | SBP ≤100 mmHg. qSOFA ≥2 predicts ICU admission and death — but misses many early sepsis cases when used as a screen.

Lactate — The Critical Triage Tool

  • Lactate ≥2 mmol/L = hypoperfusion → treat as sepsis-spectrum even if BP normal (occult shock)
  • Lactate ≥4 mmol/L = high-risk; give 30 ml/kg fluid regardless of BP
  • Serial lactate: clearance ≥10% per 2 hours = adequate resuscitation (SSC 2026 retained)

Early Management — What to Do in the First Hour

SSC 2026 — Within 1 Hour of Recognition (Septic Shock)
  • 1. Blood cultures × 2 sets (aerobic + anaerobic, different sites) — BEFORE antibiotics; do not delay antibiotics for cultures
  • 2. Lactate measurement — remeasure if initial >2 mmol/L at 2 hours
  • 3. Antibiotics immediately — within 1 hour of recognising septic shock (strong rec, very low certainty)
  • 4. IV crystalloid 30 ml/kg — if lactate ≥4 OR MAP <65 or SBP <90 (conditional rec)
  • 5. Vasopressors — if MAP <65 despite fluid; start peripherally (see below)

Fluid Resuscitation — SSC 2026

Initial Resuscitation

Give at least 30 ml/kg IV crystalloid in first 3 hours. New 2026 remark: In patients with BMI >30, calculate 30 ml/kg based on adjusted or ideal body weight, not actual body weight. Use dynamic measures to reassess after each bolus.

Deresuscitation — New in SSC 2026 New

SSC 2026 Statement 89 — After Acute Resuscitation Phase

For adults with septic shock after the acute resuscitation phase, we suggest active fluid removal (diuretics; if insufficient → ultrafiltration or extracorporeal fluid removal) over not using active fluid removal. (Conditional recommendation, very low certainty)

Why: Fluid overload causes tissue oedema, impaired O₂ delivery, and worsens outcomes. Active deresuscitation in the recovery phase reduces mechanical ventilation duration and improves outcomes.

Vasopressors — SSC 2026 Updated

When to Start — SSC 2026

Start vasopressors if MAP <65 mmHg despite initial fluid resuscitation. New 2026: Start vasopressors peripherally initially (rather than waiting for central access) — conditional recommendation. Transition to central line when feasible.

SSC 2026 MAP Targets — CHANGED from 2021
  • All adults with septic shock: Initial MAP target 65 mmHg (Strong rec, moderate certainty). New remark: maintaining exactly 65 is not feasible — a range within ±5 mmHg (i.e., 60–70) is acceptable.
  • Adults ≥65 years (NEW): Initial MAP target 60–65 mmHg (Conditional rec, low certainty). Meta-analysis: lower target associated with reduced mortality in elderly.

Blood Pressure Monitoring — Changed in SSC 2026 Changed

SSC 2026: For adults with septic shock, we suggest using either invasive OR noninvasive blood pressure monitoring (conditional, very low certainty). EVERDAC trial: noninvasive strategy noninferior to arterial line for 28-day mortality.

Use invasive (arterial line) when: Intermediate-to-high dose vasopressors; escalating vasopressors; multiple vasopressors; frequent ABGs; inconsistent NIBP readings.

Antimicrobials — SSC 2026

Key New/Upgraded Recommendations

🆕 Prolonged Beta-Lactam Infusion — Upgraded to STRONG (SSC 2026 Statement 33)

For adults with sepsis/septic shock, we RECOMMEND prolonged infusion of beta-lactams for maintenance (after loading dose) over bolus administration. (Strong recommendation, moderate certainty — UPGRADED from conditional)

Why: Beta-lactams are time-dependent antibiotics. Prolonged infusion maintains drug levels above MIC for longer → better bacterial kill. Give loading dose first, then switch to 3–4h extended infusion.

🆕 De-escalation — Upgraded to STRONG (SSC 2026 Statement 36)

We RECOMMEND de-escalation of antimicrobial therapy over no de-escalation when confirmed microbiological diagnosis and susceptibility profile is available. (Strong recommendation, very low certainty)

No Routine Anaerobic Coverage — New SSC 2026 New

SSC 2026 Statement 28 — New Conditional Recommendation

For adults with sepsis/septic shock WITHOUT risk factors for anaerobic infection, we suggest using an empiric antibiotic regimen WITHOUT anaerobic coverage.

Risk factors FOR anaerobic coverage: Intra-abdominal source, deep gynecological/obstetric infection, necrotizing soft-tissue infection, head and neck infection, CNS abscess or empyema.

Exception: Agents that happen to have anaerobic activity (pip-taz, carbapenems) can still be used if needed for MDR coverage.

No Routine Empiric Antifungal — Changed SSC 2026 Changed

SSC 2026: We suggest against empiric antifungal therapy. (Conditional, low certainty — changed from prior dual recommendation). Consider case-by-case in: immunocompromised, prolonged antibiotics, prolonged hospitalisation, intra-abdominal source.

Antibiotic Duration

  • Most infections: 7 days adequate with good source control and clinical response
  • PCT-guided de-escalation safe and reduces duration (SSC 2026 retained)
  • S. aureus bacteraemia: minimum 14 days IV antibiotics
  • Candida bloodstream: 14 days from last positive culture

Adjunctive Therapies — SSC 2026

Corticosteroids — Downgraded Certainty

SSC 2026: We suggest using IV corticosteroids in septic shock. (Conditional recommendation, low certainty — downgraded from moderate). Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day (50 mg IV q6h or 200 mg continuous infusion).

When to give: Not responding adequately to vasopressors AND fluid resuscitation. APROCCHSS trial added fludrocortisone 50 mcg PO/NG OD.

Glycaemic Control

Target blood glucose 140–180 mg/dL using insulin infusion. NICE-SUGAR confirmed: tight control 80–110 mg/dL causes harm. Monitor glucose q1–2h on insulin infusion.

Blood Transfusion

Restrictive strategy: transfuse RBC when Hb <7 g/dL. Higher threshold (Hb <9 g/dL) only for active myocardial ischaemia or severe hypoxaemia.

Selective Digestive Tract Decontamination (SDD) — New 2026 New

SSC 2026 Statement 41: For mechanically ventilated adults with sepsis/septic shock in units with low antimicrobial resistance prevalence, we suggest using SDD. (Conditional, moderate certainty). SDD = topical antibiotics to oropharynx + upper GI + short-course IV antibiotics. Reduces mortality without increasing AMR in low-resistance environments. NOT recommended in Indian ICUs with high MDR prevalence.

DVT & Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis

  • DVT: LMWH preferred over UFH; mechanical (stockings/pneumatic) if anticoagulation contraindicated
  • Stress ulcer: PPI 40 mg IV OD in high-risk patients (MV >48h, coagulopathy, prior GI bleed, steroids). Not for all patients — overuse increases C. difficile risk

Indian Context

🇮🇳 Indian Epidemiology & ISCCM Guidance
  • AMR landscape: India has among the highest ESBL, CRKP, and XDR Acinetobacter rates globally. Empirical carbapenem use may be necessary earlier than in Western guidelines. De-escalation based on culture is critical to preserve last-resort antibiotics.
  • Tropical sepsis: Always consider malaria (rapid antigen test), dengue (NS1 + IgM), scrub typhus (ELISA/Weil-Felix), leptospirosis (monsoon season), enteric fever. These can mimic bacterial septic shock and require specific treatment.
  • Leptospirosis sepsis: Monsoon months; jaundice + AKI + fever = Weil's disease; add Penicillin G 1.5 MU IV q6h or Doxycycline 100 mg BD
  • Peripheral vasopressors: In resource-limited settings, peripheral line (antecubital/wrist) for NE up to 12h is acceptable. SSC 2026 explicitly supports this.
  • SDD not recommended in Indian ICUs due to high AMR background rates — SSC 2026's SDD recommendation applies only to low-AMR environments.
  • Blood culture yield: In India, take cultures BEFORE antibiotics — blood culture positivity drops from 31% to 19% after antibiotics. Use automated BACTEC/BacT/ALERT if available.

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — Sepsis is a Time-Sensitive Emergency

Every hour of delay in antibiotics in septic shock adds ~7% relative mortality increase. Get cultures and give antibiotics simultaneously — don't wait for culture results. A 30-min delay for "one more investigation" costs lives.

💡 Pearl 2 — NEWS2 > qSOFA for Screening (2026)

qSOFA misses ~40% of sepsis at presentation. Use NEWS2 (RR, SpO₂, Systolic BP, Pulse, Consciousness, Temp + O₂) for initial screening. qSOFA ≥2 predicts ICU admission — useful for prognosis, not diagnosis.

💡 Pearl 3 — Age ≥65? Target MAP 60–65 (2026 Change)

NEW in SSC 2026: permissive hypotension (MAP 60–65) in elderly (≥65 yrs) reduces vasopressor exposure and may reduce mortality. You do NOT need to push to MAP 70–75 in every elderly patient.

💡 Pearl 4 — No Anaerobic Coverage Without Risk Factors (2026)

NEW SSC 2026: Lung sepsis, UTI, and most unknown-source sepsis do NOT need metronidazole or anaerobic-specific coverage. Only add if: abdominal, deep gynae, necrotizing soft tissue, CNS abscess, or head/neck source.

💡 Pearl 5 — Extended Infusion Beta-Lactams (Now Strong Rec)

Meropenem loading dose 1g over 30 min → THEN 1g over 3–4h q8h. Piperacillin-Taz: 4.5g load over 30 min → then 4.5g over 4h q6h. This is now a STRONG recommendation in SSC 2026. Sets it up simply: give the bolus, then switch to extended infusion.

💡 Pearl 6 — Deresuscitate After Acute Phase

NEW SSC 2026: Once the patient is haemodynamically stable (off/low vasopressors), actively remove fluid with furosemide. Positive fluid balance >10% body weight = worsened outcomes. Target neutral to negative balance from 24–48h.

💡 Pearl 7 — Albumin is Out (2026 Change)

SSC 2026 updated meta-analysis: albumin has NO effect on mortality. Use crystalloids alone. The only exception: very large crystalloid volumes already given OR cirrhosis. Albumin is expensive and no longer recommended routinely.

💡 Pearl 8 — Lactate Clearance > Lactate Level

A lactate of 3.5 that clears 50% in 2h is better than a lactate of 2 that doesn't change. Serial measurement and clearance trending drives resuscitation decisions, not a single absolute value.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Waiting for Central Line to Start Vasopressors

SSC 2026 explicitly recommends starting vasopressors peripherally. A wrist or antecubital NE infusion is safe for up to 12h. The patient needs MAP now — don't delay for 30 min to insert a CVC.

❌ Mistake 2 — Giving Albumin Routinely

SSC 2026 changed this — crystalloids alone are preferred. Meta-analysis showed NO mortality benefit from albumin. Crystalloid is cheaper, equally effective, and the evidence is now stronger.

❌ Mistake 3 — Using Dopamine as First-Line

SOAP II trial confirmed: dopamine causes significantly more arrhythmias. SSC 2026 maintains strong recommendation for NE over dopamine. If NE is unavailable, dopamine is acceptable — but only then.

❌ Mistake 4 — Not De-escalating Antibiotics

SSC 2026 upgraded de-escalation to a STRONG recommendation. When cultures come back at 48–72h, narrow the spectrum. Broad-spectrum empirics for 14 days = CDiff, AMR, increased mortality. De-escalate.

❌ Mistake 5 — Targeting MAP 70–75 in All Elderly Patients

New SSC 2026: patients ≥65 years can be managed at MAP 60–65. Pushing to 70+ requires more vasopressors → arrhythmias + digital ischaemia. Evidence shows permissive hypotension is safe and may improve outcomes in the elderly.

❌ Mistake 6 — Adding Metronidazole to Every Sepsis Empiric Regimen

New SSC 2026: no anaerobic coverage unless specific risk factors present. Respiratory, urinary, and unknown-source sepsis do NOT need anaerobic coverage. Unnecessary metronidazole adds hepatotoxicity risk and CDiff risk with no benefit.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM / EDIC)

Q: Which screening tool does SSC 2026 recommend over qSOFA? → NEWS, NEWS2, MEWS, or SIRS (strong recommendation, moderate certainty). qSOFA is prognostic only.

Q: MAP target in septic shock for patients ≥65 years? (NEW 2026) → 60–65 mmHg (conditional recommendation, low certainty). For <65 years: ≥65 mmHg.

Q: What changed about albumin in SSC 2026? → Now recommend crystalloids ALONE. Albumin no longer routinely recommended — updated meta-analysis shows no mortality benefit.

Q: Extended infusion of beta-lactams is now what grade in SSC 2026? → Strong recommendation (upgraded from conditional), moderate certainty. Give loading dose first, then extended 3–4h infusion.

Q: When do you NOT need anaerobic antibiotic coverage per SSC 2026? → When there are NO risk factors for anaerobic infection (i.e., non-abdominal, non-gynae, non-necrotizing, non-CNS sepsis).

Q: Is invasive BP monitoring (arterial line) mandatory in septic shock per SSC 2026? → No — CHANGED in 2026. Either invasive OR noninvasive acceptable. Arterial line indicated for: high-dose vasopressors, escalating doses, multiple vasopressors, frequent ABGs, inconsistent NIBP.

Q: What did SSC 2026 say about SDD (selective digestive decontamination)? → Suggested for MV patients with sepsis in LOW-AMR prevalence units (moderate certainty). NOT recommended in high-AMR settings like most Indian ICUs.

Q: Norepinephrine recommendation strength in SSC 2026 vs 2021? → 2021: Strong over vasopressin/angiotensin II. 2026: DOWNGRADED to conditional over vasopressin/angiotensin II. Still strong over dopamine/epinephrine/selepressin.

Q: De-escalation of antibiotics in SSC 2026? → Upgraded to STRONG recommendation when confirmed microbiological diagnosis and susceptibility available.
