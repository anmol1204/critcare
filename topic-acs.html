📘 1 · Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed

Washington Manual Summary

"Every minute of myocardial ischaemia results in approximately 1.7 additional deaths per 1000 treated patients. The most important principle in ACS management is rapid reperfusion — time is muscle, and time is life."

Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed. (Kollef & Witt, eds.) Wolters Kluwer 2023.

📗 2 · Marino's The ICU Book, 5th Edition (2025)

Marino Physiology — ACS

🔬 How acute coronary syndrome develops
TriggerAtheromatous plaque rupture or erosion in a coronary artery
Exposed collagen → platelet adhesion (GPIb–vWF) → activation (ADP, TXA₂, thrombin)
Aggregation (GPIIb/IIIa–fibrinogen) + thrombin→fibrin → coronary thrombus
Partial occlusion → UA / NSTEMIsubendocardial ischaemia
Complete occlusion → STEMItransmural wavefront over 6–12h
Myocyte death from ~20–40 min; reperfusion injury if prolonged
Pathological stateMyocardial infarction → pump failure · arrhythmia · cardiogenic shock

"The central event in the pathogenesis of most cases of ACS is disruption (rupture or erosion) of an atheromatous plaque in a coronary artery. This leads to thrombus formation and, in most cases, partial or complete obstruction of coronary blood flow."

Marino PL. The ICU Book, 5th Ed. Chapter 20: Acute Coronary Syndromes, p.356 (approx). Wolters Kluwer; 2025.
📗 Marino Ch.20 — ACS Pathophysiology

From the saved Marino Ch.20 content:

  • Plaque rupture exposes subendothelial collagen → platelet adhesion (GPIb-vWF) → activation (ADP, TXA₂, thrombin) → aggregation (GPIIb/IIIa-fibrinogen) → thrombus
  • Thrombin generated → fibrin → red clot → coronary occlusion → ischaemia → infarction if prolonged
  • Myocardial cells die within 20–40 minutes of complete ischaemia; wavefront progresses subendocardium → transmural over 6–12 hours
  • Reperfusion injury: paradoxical worsening on reopening blocked artery (reactive oxygen species, Ca²⁺ overload) — irreversible if >4–6h of ischaemia
📗 Marino Ch.20 — Why Oxygen is NOT Routinely Indicated

ACC/AHA 2025 and Marino: supplemental O₂ NOT recommended if SpO₂ ≥90%. High-flow O₂ in normoxic MI may increase infarct size (coronary vasoconstriction, oxidant stress) — Class III: Harm. Give O₂ only if SpO₂ <90%.

📋 3 · ACC/AHA/ACEP 2025 Guidelines

Key Recommendations — ACC/AHA 2025

Rao SV, O'Donoghue ML, Ruel M, et al. ACC/AHA/ACEP/NAEMSP/SCAI 2025 Guideline for Management of Patients with ACS. JACC 2025;85(9):e59–e161.
STEMI — Reperfusion Timing Strong · Class I
Primary PCI: First medical contact (FMC) to balloon (D2B) <90 min if PCI-capable centre | <120 min if transfer needed
Fibrinolysis: FMC to needle <30 min if PCI not available within 120 min and symptoms <12h
ECG within 10 min of FMC — STEMI is an ECG diagnosis, NOT troponin-dependent
NSTEMI — Risk Stratification Strong · Class I
GRACE score for risk stratification: >140 = High risk → invasive within 24h | 109–140 = Intermediate → 72h | <109 = Low → conservative or stress testing
Immediate (<2h): haemodynamic instability, refractory ischaemia, life-threatening arrhythmia, cardiogenic shock
Oxygen — Class III (Harm) Avoid in normoxic MI
Do NOT give supplemental O₂ if SpO₂ ≥90%. High-flow O₂ in normoxic ACS may increase infarct size (AVOID-HF, DETO2X-AMI trials). Titrate O₂ to maintain SpO₂ 90–96% only if hypoxic.

🗺 5 · Clinical Flowchart

ACS Management — Step by Step

1

ECG within 10 Minutes — First Priority

Every patient with chest pain/dyspnoea/syncope: ECG within 10 min. STEMI on ECG → activate cath lab NOW. Do NOT wait for troponin. Troponin takes hours; ECG diagnosis is immediate.

2

STEMI — Reperfusion Decision

PCI available in <120 min → Primary PCI (D2B <90 min). PCI NOT available in 120 min AND symptoms <12h AND no contraindications → Fibrinolysis (Tenecteplase weight-based IV push OR Streptokinase 1.5 MIU in India). Then transfer for angiography within 24h.

3

Dual Antiplatelets + Anticoagulation

Aspirin 300 mg chewed + Ticagrelor 180 mg (preferred) or Clopidogrel 600 mg. UFH or Enoxaparin. Start before PCI/fibrinolysis. Withhold only if absolute contraindication to antiplatelet (active CNS bleed).

4

Inferior STEMI — Right-Sided Leads Mandatory

ST elevation V3R–V4R = RV infarction (30–50% of inferior STEMI). Management: IV fluids (NOT diuretics), AVOID nitrates (precipitate hypotension), inotropes if needed. Missing RV infarction = iatrogenic cardiac arrest.

5

NSTEMI/UA — GRACE Score Risk Stratification

GRACE >140 or haemodynamic instability/refractory ischaemia → angiography <2h (immediate). GRACE 109–140 → angiography <24h. GRACE <109 → conservative or stress testing. Medical: aspirin + ticagrelor + anticoagulation + high-intensity statin.

6

Cardiogenic Shock in ACS

Early revascularisation (SHOCK trial). Noradrenaline (MAP ≥65) + Dobutamine (↑CO). IABP: no mortality benefit (IABP-SHOCK II) — use as bridge only. VA-ECMO for refractory cardiogenic shock. Transfer early to ECMO/surgical centre.

⚠️ 6 · Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Waiting for Troponin to Activate Cath Lab

STEMI is an ECG diagnosis. Do NOT wait for troponin results (takes 1–6h depending on assay). Activate cath lab immediately on ECG criteria. Every minute of delay = 1.7 additional deaths per 1000 treated patients.

❌ Mistake 2 — Not Doing Right-Sided ECG in Inferior STEMI

RV infarction complicates 30–50% of inferior STEMI. Diagnosis: ST elevation V3R–V4R. Nitrates in RV infarction = severe hypotension. Diuretics = worsens RV preload. Treatment: IV fluids. Missing RV infarction = preventable catastrophe.

❌ Mistake 3 — High-Flow O₂ in Normoxic ACS

ACC/AHA 2025: Class III (Harm) — supplemental O₂ NOT recommended if SpO₂ ≥90%. Hyperoxia may cause coronary vasoconstriction and increase infarct size. Titrate to SpO₂ 90–96% only if hypoxic.

❌ Mistake 4 — Nitrates in RV Infarction or With PDE5 Inhibitors

RV infarction = preload-dependent. Nitrates → ↓ venous return → ↓ RV filling → cardiovascular collapse. Also: sildenafil/tadalafil within 24–48h + nitrates = severe hypotension. Always ask about PDE5 inhibitors before giving GTN.

❌ Mistake 5 — Metoprolol in Acute Heart Failure or Cardiogenic Shock

Beta-blockers are contraindicated in acute decompensated heart failure and cardiogenic shock. Start within 24h ONLY if haemodynamically stable. Evidence (COMMIT trial): early IV beta-blocker in Killip class II–IV patients → harm.

🎓 7 · Exam Pearls — DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM

Exam Pearls

Q: ACC/AHA 2025 door-to-balloon time targets?
<90 min if patient presents to PCI-capable centre. <120 min if transfer is required. If PCI not available within 120 min AND symptoms <12h → fibrinolysis (Tenecteplase single IV bolus, weight-based) within 30 min of arrival (door-to-needle).

Q: Why avoid nitrates in inferior STEMI? When?
If right-sided leads (V3R–V4R) show ST elevation = RV infarction = preload-dependent. Nitrates → ↓ venous return → ↓ RV filling → haemodynamic collapse. Also contraindicated if PDE5 inhibitors used within 24–48h (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil).

Q: GRACE score thresholds for angiography timing?
>140 OR haemodynamic instability/refractory ischaemia → Immediate (<2h) | 109–140 → Early (<24h) | <109 → Conservative or 72h. HEART score used for chest pain risk stratification in ED (low ≤3 = 2% MACE; high ≥7 = 65% MACE).

Q: Tenecteplase (TNK) dosing for STEMI?
Weight-based single IV bolus: <60 kg: 30 mg | 60–70 kg: 35 mg | 70–80 kg: 40 mg | 80–90 kg: 45 mg | >90 kg: 50 mg. Give over 10 seconds. Add aspirin 300 mg + clopidogrel 300 mg + enoxaparin or UFH simultaneously.

Q: What is the ACC/AHA 2025 recommendation on oxygen in ACS?
Class III (Harm): supplemental O₂ NOT recommended if SpO₂ ≥90%. Hyperoxia in normoxic MI may increase infarct size. Give O₂ only if SpO₂ <90%; titrate to maintain 90–96%.
