How ACS Works — The Pathophysiology

The Culprit: Coronary Plaque Rupture

ACS is almost always caused by rupture or erosion of a coronary atherosclerotic plaque. Vulnerable plaques have a large lipid core, thin fibrous cap, and high inflammatory cell content (foam cells, macrophages). Triggers include:

  • Shear stress from turbulent blood flow at bifurcations
  • Sympathetic surges (exercise, emotional stress, cocaine use)
  • Inflammation (infection, cold weather — higher AMI incidence in winter)
  • Paradoxically, statin use temporarily increases rupture risk as plaques stabilise (regression)

The Coagulation Cascade After Plaque Rupture

  1. Plaque rupture exposes subendothelial collagen and tissue factor
  2. Platelets adhere (GPIb-vWF), activate (ADP, TXA₂, thrombin), aggregate (GPIIb/IIIa-fibrinogen)
  3. Thrombin generated → fibrin formed → white thrombus → red clot
  4. Thrombus occludes lumen → cessation of coronary flow → ischaemia → infarction if prolonged

Time = Myocardium

Myocardial cells die within 20–40 minutes of complete ischaemia. Wavefront of necrosis progresses from subendocardium → transmural over 6–12 hours. Every minute of delay in STEMI = 1.7 additional lives lost per 1000 patients treated (De Luca, 2004).

The ACS Spectrum — One Mechanism, Different Severity
  • Unstable Angina (UA): Plaque rupture + partial thrombus → reduced flow → ischaemia without infarction. Troponin NEGATIVE.
  • NSTEMI: Partial or transient occlusion → subendocardial infarction. Troponin POSITIVE. No ST elevation.
  • STEMI: Complete occlusion of a major coronary artery → transmural infarction. ST elevation + positive troponin.

STEMI Equivalents (No Obvious ST Elevation)

  • LBBB (new or presumed new) + typical symptoms: Treat as STEMI (Sgarbossa criteria if needed)
  • De Winter T-waves (V1–V6): Upsloping ST depression + peaked symmetrical T-waves = LAD occlusion equivalent
  • aVR ST elevation + diffuse depression: Left main or proximal LAD occlusion — surgical emergency
  • Wellens syndrome: Deeply inverted T-waves V2–V3 in pain-free state = LAD critical stenosis — high risk of anterior STEMI

Diagnosis

High-Sensitivity Troponin (hs-cTnI / hs-cTnT)

  • Detectable within 1–3 hours of symptom onset (vs 4–6h for conventional troponin)
  • 0h/1h algorithm (ESC 2023): hs-cTn at 0h + 1h (or 0h + 2h); RULE IN if either >5× URL; RULE OUT if both <2 ng/L at 0h and rise <4 ng/L at 1h
  • Troponin alone does NOT diagnose ACS — must be in clinical context with symptoms and ECG
  • Elevated troponin without plaque rupture = Type 2 MI (demand ischaemia: tachycardia, sepsis, PE, myocarditis)

Other Investigations

  • 12-lead ECG within 10 min of presentation (ACC/AHA 2025: Class I)
  • Right-sided leads (V3R–V4R) in ALL inferior STEMI — detect RV infarction
  • Posterior leads (V7–V9) if ST depression V1–V3 — detect posterior MI
  • Echo: wall motion abnormalities, LV function, complications (VSD, MR, pericardial effusion)
  • CXR: pulmonary oedema, mediastinal widening (aortic dissection DDx)

STEMI Management — Time-Critical

ACC/AHA 2025 — STEMI Time Targets
  • First Medical Contact (FMC) to ECG: ≤10 minutes
  • FMC to Primary PCI (pPCI) — Door-to-Balloon: ≤90 minutes if PCI centre
  • FMC to pPCI — if transfer needed: ≤120 minutes (if transfer >120 min → fibrinolysis first)
  • FMC to Fibrinolysis: ≤30 minutes (door-to-needle) if pPCI not available in time
  • Symptom onset to Reperfusion: <12 hours (consider up to 24h in selected patients)

Primary PCI (pPCI) — Preferred Strategy

Primary PCI is the preferred reperfusion strategy when available within 120 minutes of FMC. Advantages over fibrinolysis: higher TIMI 3 flow rates (90% vs 60%), lower re-occlusion, lower intracranial haemorrhage risk, better outcomes in cardiogenic shock.

Contraindications to Fibrinolysis

Absolute Contraindications — Do NOT give fibrinolysis
  • Prior intracranial haemorrhage at any time
  • Structural cerebrovascular lesion (AVM, aneurysm)
  • Malignant intracranial neoplasm
  • Ischaemic stroke within 3 months (except acute ischaemic stroke within 4.5h)
  • Suspected aortic dissection
  • Active bleeding or bleeding diathesis (excluding menses)
  • Significant closed head or facial trauma within 3 months
  • Intracranial or intraspinal surgery within 2 months
  • Severe uncontrolled hypertension (SBP >180 or DBP >110 refractory to treatment)

Post-Fibrinolysis Management

  • Transfer to PCI centre for coronary angiography within 3–24h (pharmaco-invasive strategy)
  • Emergency PCI if: failed fibrinolysis (ST elevation persists at 60–90 min, ongoing pain, haemodynamic instability)
  • Anticoagulation: UFH or enoxaparin for minimum 48h post-fibrinolysis

NSTEMI/Unstable Angina Management

Risk Stratification — GRACE Score

GRACE score stratifies NSTEMI/UA into low, intermediate, and high risk to determine timing of invasive strategy. Key components: age, HR, SBP, creatinine, Killip class, cardiac arrest, ST deviation, elevated cardiac markers.

Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)

DAPT — Aspirin + P2Y₁₂ Inhibitor
  • Aspirin 75–100 mg OD — lifelong
  • Ticagrelor 90 mg BD OR Clopidogrel 75 mg OD — minimum 12 months after STEMI/NSTEMI-PCI
  • After bare-metal stent: Minimum 1 month DAPT
  • After drug-eluting stent: Minimum 6–12 months DAPT
  • Can extend to 24–36 months in high ischaemic risk and low bleeding risk patients
  • Reduce to 3–6 months in high bleeding risk patients

Cardiogenic Shock in ACS

Cardiogenic Shock — 30–40% Mortality Despite PCI

Cardiogenic shock complicates 5–10% of STEMI and has mortality 30–40% even with modern PCI. Presentation: SBP <90 mmHg + evidence of hypoperfusion (cold/clammy peripheries, altered mentation, UO <30 ml/hr, elevated lactate).

Management

  • Immediate coronary angiography + PCI — SHOCK trial: early revascularisation reduces 6-month mortality. If multi-vessel CAD: only culprit vessel PCI initially.
  • Vasopressors: Noradrenaline first-line. Dopamine NOT preferred (more arrhythmias). Add dobutamine for low CO. Target MAP ≥65 mmHg.
  • Avoid: Aggressive fluid loading — cardiogenic shock is NOT fluid-responsive; fluids worsen pulmonary oedema
  • Mechanical Circulatory Support:
    • IABP: No mortality benefit (IABP-SHOCK II); use as bridge to ECMO/surgery if needed
    • Impella: Provides 2.5–5.5 L/min support; ISAR-SHOCK and IMPRESS: no clear mortality benefit
    • VA-ECMO: Refractory cardiogenic shock; provides full cardiopulmonary support; refer early to ECMO centre

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — STEMI: Time Is Muscle

Every 30 minutes delay in STEMI reperfusion = 7.5 additional deaths per 1000 patients. Get ECG within 10 min. Activate cath lab immediately. Do NOT wait for troponin results to call pPCI — STEMI is an ECG diagnosis.

💡 Pearl 2 — Right-Sided ECG in ALL Inferior STEMI

RV infarction complicates 30–50% of inferior STEMI. Diagnosis: ST elevation V3R–V4R. Treatment is COMPLETELY different: aggressive fluids (NOT diuretics), avoid nitrates (cause hypotension), avoid morphine. Missing RV infarction = iatrogenic cardiac arrest.

💡 Pearl 3 — No O₂ if SpO₂ Normal

ACC/AHA 2025 and ESC 2023: Supplemental O₂ NOT recommended if SpO₂ ≥90%. High-flow O₂ in normoxic MI may cause coronary vasoconstriction and increase infarct size (AVOID-HF, DETO2X-AMI). Give O₂ only if SpO₂ <90%.

💡 Pearl 4 — Nitrates CONTRAINDICATED in RV Infarction

RV infarction = preload-dependent. Nitrates cause venous dilation → ↓ preload → ↓ RV filling → sudden haemodynamic collapse. Also contraindicated with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) — ask every patient before giving nitrates.

💡 Pearl 5 — India: Streptokinase is Often the Reality

PCI is not universally available in India. Know streptokinase well: 1.5 million IU over 60 min. Check absolute contraindications. Give aspirin 300 mg + clopidogrel 300 mg at same time. Transfer for angiography within 24h after successful fibrinolysis.

💡 Pearl 6 — Wellens Syndrome = Impending STEMI

Deeply inverted (Type A) or biphasic (Type B) T-waves in V2–V3 in a pain-free patient who had recent chest pain = critical LAD stenosis. Do NOT stress test — will precipitate massive anterior MI. Urgent angiography.

💡 Pearl 7 — Loud New Systolic Murmur Post-MI = Emergency

New systolic murmur 3–7 days post-MI = papillary muscle rupture (MR — soft, radiates to axilla) OR ventricular septal defect (VSD — harsh, left sternal edge, step-up in RV O₂ saturation). Both are surgical emergencies.

💡 Pearl 8 — IABP Has No Mortality Benefit

IABP-SHOCK II trial: IABP in cardiogenic shock = no survival benefit vs medical therapy. Do not expect IABP to save a patient. It can be used as a bridge to MCS or surgery. VA-ECMO provides definitive mechanical support for refractory cardiogenic shock.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Not Doing Right-Sided ECG in Inferior STEMI

RV infarction = preload-dependent; giving nitrates → cardiovascular collapse. Missing it = iatrogenic catastrophe. Do right-sided leads (V3R–V4R) in ALL inferior STEMI routinely.

❌ Mistake 2 — Giving O₂ to All MI Patients

ACC/AHA 2025: No supplemental O₂ if SpO₂ ≥90%. High-flow O₂ may increase infarct size (DETO2X-AMI trial). Give O₂ only if hypoxic. This is now a Class III (harm) recommendation in normoxic patients.

❌ Mistake 3 — Giving Nitrates in RV Infarction or After PDE5 Inhibitor

Nitrates → ↓ preload → ↓ RV filling in RV infarction → haemodynamic collapse. Also: sildenafil/tadalafil + nitrates → severe hypotension. ALWAYS ask about PDE5 inhibitors before giving nitrates.

❌ Mistake 4 — Giving Fluids in Cardiogenic Shock

Cardiogenic shock is pump failure — NOT hypovolaemia. Aggressive fluid loading worsens pulmonary oedema and RV afterload. Small cautious fluid challenge (250 ml) is acceptable to exclude pre-renal component, but DO NOT fluid-load a cardiogenic shock patient.

❌ Mistake 5 — Waiting for Troponin to Call STEMI

STEMI is diagnosed on the ECG — not on troponin. Troponin may be negative in the first 1–2 hours. Call the cath lab based on ST elevation in ≥2 contiguous leads. Every minute of delay costs myocardium.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM)

Q: Door-to-Balloon time target in STEMI? → ≤90 minutes if PCI available. ≤120 minutes if transfer needed. ≤30 minutes door-to-needle if fibrinolysis given instead.

Q: When is fibrinolysis preferred over primary PCI in STEMI? → If PCI cannot be performed within 120 minutes of FMC AND symptom onset <12 hours AND no absolute contraindications to fibrinolysis.

Q: Triad of RV infarction? → Hypotension + Elevated JVP + Clear lungs (Kussmaul's triad). Diagnose with right-sided ECG: ST elevation V3R–V4R. Treat with: IV fluids (NOT diuretics), avoid nitrates, inotropes if needed.

Q: New loud systolic murmur on day 4 post-inferior MI? → Papillary muscle rupture → acute severe mitral regurgitation. Posteromedial papillary muscle (single blood supply from RCA) most vulnerable. Emergency surgery ± MCS as bridge.

Q: Wellens syndrome ECG pattern and significance? → Deeply inverted (Type A) or biphasic (Type B) T-waves V2–V3 in pain-free patient who had chest pain = critical LAD stenosis = impending massive anterior STEMI. Urgent angiography. NO exercise testing.

Q: ACC/AHA 2025 recommendation on oxygen in ACS? → Do NOT give supplemental O₂ if SpO₂ ≥90%. High-flow O₂ in normoxic ACS may increase infarct size (Class III — Harm).
