End-Organ Damage = Hypertensive Emergency

  • Hypertensive encephalopathy (PRES — Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome)
  • Intracranial haemorrhage (ICH)
  • Acute ischaemic stroke with very high BP requiring management
  • Aortic dissection (Type A or B)
  • Acute pulmonary oedema / flash pulmonary oedema
  • Acute coronary syndrome (NSTEMI/UA with hypertension)
  • Acute kidney injury (hypertensive nephrosclerosis)
  • Eclampsia / severe pre-eclampsia
  • Hypertensive retinopathy (Grade III–IV — haemorrhages, papilloedema)
  • Microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (TMA with severe hypertension)

Pathophysiology — Why BP Causes Organ Damage

Loss of Cerebral Autoregulation

Normally, cerebral blood flow (CBF) is maintained constant between MAP 60–150 mmHg (autoregulation). In chronic hypertension, this curve shifts right (higher range required). In hypertensive emergency, MAP exceeds the upper limit of autoregulation → pressure-passive flow → breakthrough hyperperfusion → cerebral oedema → hypertensive encephalopathy.

Implication: Rapidly lowering BP to "normal" in a chronically hypertensive patient = CBF falls below their autoregulatory floor → watershed infarction. This is why we lower MAP by only 10–20% in the first hour.

Endothelial Injury

Very high BP → endothelial shear stress → fibrinoid necrosis of arterioles → microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (MAHA) → target organ ischaemia. This produces the "malignant hypertension" picture: very high BP + papilloedema + MAHA + AKI.

Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone Axis

Renal ischaemia from BP crisis → ↑ renin → ↑ angiotensin II → vasoconstriction → further BP elevation → vicious cycle. Also: aldosterone → Na and water retention → volume expansion → more BP elevation.

BP Reduction Targets — Scenario-Specific

General Principle — Do NOT lower to normal in first hour

Rapid normalisation → cerebral and renal hypoperfusion due to shifted autoregulation. Target: reduce MAP by 10–20% in first 1 hour, then gradually to 160/100 over 2–6 hours, then to normal over next 24–48 hours. Exception: aortic dissection (fastest lowering needed).

Specific Scenarios — Detailed Management

Hypertensive Encephalopathy / PRES

Presents as: severe headache, nausea/vomiting, visual disturbances, confusion, seizures in setting of very high BP. MRI: FLAIR hyperintensity posterior parieto-occipital regions (PRES pattern). Treat BP → symptoms/MRI resolve within hours to days.

Management: Labetalol IV or nicardipine infusion. Target MAP reduction 20–25% in first hour. No specific drug for PRES — lowering BP is the treatment.

Aortic Dissection

Aortic Dissection — Fastest BP Lowering Needed

Target: SBP <120 AND HR <60 within 20 minutes. Each additional mmHg of BP propagates the dissection and worsens prognosis. β-blocker FIRST to reduce HR and dP/dt, THEN vasodilator. Giving a vasodilator without a β-blocker causes reflex tachycardia → increases aortic wall stress → propagates dissection.

Protocol: Esmolol 500 mcg/kg bolus → 50–300 mcg/kg/min infusion (to achieve HR <60) → THEN Sodium nitroprusside 0.5–10 mcg/kg/min to achieve SBP <120.

Eclampsia / Pre-eclampsia

  • Magnesium sulphate 4g IV loading over 15–20 min → 1–2g/hr infusion — for seizure prevention AND treatment
  • Antihypertensive if SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥110: Labetalol 20 mg IV → repeat 40 mg → 80 mg (total max 300 mg); OR Hydralazine 5 mg IV q20 min (max 20 mg); OR Nifedipine 10 mg PO (oral — NOT sublingual)
  • Target: SBP 140–155, DBP 90–105 — do NOT lower too aggressively (placental hypoperfusion)
  • Definitive treatment: Delivery of the foetus
  • Monitor: MgSO₄ toxicity (↓DTRs → first sign; respiratory arrest → needs calcium gluconate 1g IV antidote)

Monitoring in Hypertensive Emergency

  • Arterial line: Mandatory for nitroprusside; strongly recommended for all IV antihypertensive infusions (allows beat-to-beat BP monitoring)
  • Target-organ monitoring: Neuro: GCS, NIHSS; Cardiac: ECG, troponin, echo; Renal: hourly UO, creatinine; Ophthalmology: fundoscopy
  • ECG: Continuous monitoring for ischaemia, arrhythmia
  • Urinary catheter: Monitor UO hourly; oliguria suggests renal hypoperfusion (over-treatment) or ongoing hypertensive nephropathy
  • MRI Brain: If encephalopathy/PRES suspected — FLAIR sequence most sensitive

Indian Context

🇮🇳 Indian ED — Practical Considerations
  • Sublingual nifedipine is STILL used in India despite strong evidence of harm. Educate your team to stop this practice entirely — it causes uncontrolled BP falls, strokes, and MI.
  • Hydralazine availability: More widely available in India than nicardipine; useful in eclampsia. Watch for reflex tachycardia — avoid in coronary disease.
  • Nitroprusside: Requires arterial line and careful monitoring; cyanide toxicity with prolonged use or renal failure — limit to 10 mcg/kg/min × 10 min or 0.5 mcg/kg/min × 10h. Use thiosulphate co-infusion with prolonged use.
  • Phaeochromocytoma in India: Consider in young hypertensive with paroxysmal BP, sweating, palpitations, headache. Urine VMA/metanephrines. Give phentolamine or phenoxybenzamine (PO) — NEVER give β-blocker without α-blockade (unopposed α → hypertensive crisis).

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — Urgency vs Emergency: The Organ Makes the Difference

Very high BP without end-organ damage = Hypertensive URGENCY → oral medications, gradual reduction over 24–48h. No IV treatment needed, no ICU admission. BP alone doesn't determine urgency — organs do.

💡 Pearl 2 — 10–20% MAP Reduction in First Hour Only

Chronic hypertension shifts cerebral autoregulation rightward. Dropping BP rapidly to "normal" = CBF falls below autoregulatory floor = stroke. Reduce MAP by 10–20% only in first hour. Then gradually over 24–48h.

💡 Pearl 3 — Aortic Dissection: β-Blocker BEFORE Vasodilator

Vasodilator alone → reflex tachycardia → ↑ aortic wall stress → propagates dissection. Always give β-blocker (esmolol or labetalol) first to achieve HR <60, THEN add vasodilator to achieve SBP <120.

💡 Pearl 4 — ICH: Treat if SBP >150–220 mmHg

INTERACT-2 and ATACH-2 trials support aggressive BP control (SBP <140) in ICH within first 6h. Reduces haematoma expansion. BUT too-aggressive lowering can cause perihematoma ischaemia — watch for neurological worsening.

💡 Pearl 5 — MgSO₄ Toxicity Signs: Loss of DTRs First

MgSO₄ toxicity progression: Loss of patellar reflex (Mg 4–5 mmol/L) → Respiratory paralysis (Mg 6–7 mmol/L) → Cardiac arrest (Mg >12 mmol/L). Check DTRs before each dose. Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1g IV (10 ml of 10%).

💡 Pearl 6 — Phaeochromocytoma: α BEFORE β

Giving a β-blocker in phaeochromocytoma crisis without α-blockade → unopposed α-receptor stimulation → severe hypertensive crisis. ALWAYS start α-blockade (phentolamine IV or phenoxybenzamine PO) first, THEN add β-blocker if needed for tachycardia.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Sublingual Nifedipine

Causes precipitous, uncontrolled BP drop → stroke, MI, death. Cannot be titrated. Absolutely contraindicated in hypertensive emergency/urgency. Use IV labetalol, nicardipine, or oral amlodipine.

❌ Mistake 2 — Lowering BP Too Fast to Normal

Rapid normalisation of BP in chronically hypertensive patient = relative hypotension → watershed stroke, renal failure. Lower MAP by only 10–20% in first hour. Exception: aortic dissection.

❌ Mistake 3 — Giving Vasodilator Without β-Blocker in Aortic Dissection

Vasodilators alone → reflex tachycardia → ↑ dp/dt → propagates dissection → death. Must give β-blocker first to reduce HR <60, then vasodilator.

❌ Mistake 4 — β-Blocker First in Phaeochromocytoma

β-blockade in phaeochromocytoma without α-blockade = unopposed α-stimulation = severe BP surge. ALWAYS establish α-blockade (phentolamine IV) before any β-blocker.

❌ Mistake 5 — Not Checking for End-Organ Damage

BP of 200/120 without any symptoms may be hypertensive urgency (oral treatment only). The same BP with headache + confusion + papilloedema = hypertensive emergency (IV treatment). The target organ assessment determines urgency, not the BP number.

Exam Pearls

Q: Difference between hypertensive emergency and urgency? → Emergency = severely elevated BP WITH acute end-organ damage (encephalopathy, ICH, dissection, ACS, pulmonary oedema, eclampsia, AKI) → IV treatment. Urgency = elevated BP WITHOUT end-organ damage → oral treatment, gradual reduction.

Q: Drug of choice in aortic dissection with hypertension? → β-blocker FIRST (esmolol or labetalol) to achieve HR <60, THEN vasodilator (nitroprusside or nicardipine) to achieve SBP <120 within 20 min. Never give vasodilator alone.

Q: BP target in ischaemic stroke (no tPA)? → Do NOT treat unless SBP >220 or DBP >120. If treating: reduce by only 15–25% in first 24h. Most strokes need permissive hypertension to maintain penumbra perfusion.

Q: MgSO₄ toxicity — first sign and antidote? → First sign = loss of patellar (deep tendon) reflexes (Mg ~4–5 mmol/L). Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1g IV (10 ml of 10% solution). Respiratory arrest occurs at Mg ~6–7 mmol/L.

Q: Why is rapid BP normalisation dangerous in hypertensive emergency? → Chronic hypertension shifts cerebral autoregulation rightward. Rapid normalisation = MAP falls below patient's autoregulatory floor = passive CBF reduction = watershed ischaemia. Lower MAP by only 10–20% in first hour.
