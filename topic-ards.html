📘 1 · Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed

Washington Manual Summary

"ARDS represents the final common pathway of diverse insults to the lung. The mainstay of management is lung-protective ventilation; no pharmacological therapy has been proven to improve mortality. Time, patience, and meticulous supportive care are the cornerstones of ARDS management."

Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed. (Kollef & Witt, eds.) Wolters Kluwer 2023.

Clinical Synopsis

  • Definition (2023 Global): Acute onset (<7 days) + bilateral opacities on CXR/CT + oxygenation impairment (P/F ≤300 on PEEP ≥5) + not fully explained by cardiac failure
  • Expanded 2023: Includes non-intubated patients on HFNO ≥30 L/min or NIV/CPAP ≥5 cmH₂O; SpO₂/FiO₂ ≤315 also acceptable
  • Mortality: Mild ~27%; Moderate ~32%; Severe ~45% (in-hospital)
  • Incidence: ~190,000 cases/year in US; Affects ~10% of all ICU admissions; LUNG SAFE study: 23% of ventilated patients have ARDS
  • COVID-19 ARDS: Marino (p.423) — unique form with often preserved lung compliance despite severe hypoxaemia; studies may not apply to non-COVID ARDS

📗 2 · Marino's The ICU Book, 5th Edition (2025)

Marino Physiology — How ARDS Develops

🔬 How ARDS develops
TriggerInflammatory insult — pneumonia · aspiration · extrapulmonary sepsis · trauma
Circulating neutrophil activation → sequestration in the pulmonary microcirculation
Degranulation → proteolytic enzymes + ROS → alveolar–capillary membrane injury
Protein-rich exudate floods alveolihyaline membranes, surfactant loss → non-cardiogenic oedema
Alveolar collapse & consolidationintrapulmonary shunt
Refractory hypoxaemia + ↓ complianceamplified by P-SILI & O₂ toxicity
Pathological stateDiffuse alveolar damage → refractory respiratory failure ± MODS

"The lung consolidation in ARDS originates with the activation of circulating neutrophils. This leads to neutrophil sequestration in the pulmonary microcirculation, where the neutrophils attach to the vascular endothelium and move between endothelial cells and into the lung parenchyma."

Marino PL. The ICU Book, 5th Ed. Chapter 24: Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome, p.422. Wolters Kluwer; 2025.

Step 1 — Inflammatory Trigger

📗 Marino Ch.24, p.422–423

ARDS is a diffuse inflammatory injury of the lungs — not a primary lung disorder. Circulating neutrophils are activated by the underlying condition (sepsis, pneumonia, aspiration) → neutrophil sequestration in pulmonary microcirculation → degranulation → release of proteolytic enzymes and reactive oxygen species → damage to alveolar-capillary membrane.

Predisposing conditions (Marino Table 24.1): Pneumonia (60%), extrapulmonary sepsis (16%), septic shock (8%), gastric aspiration (14%), blood transfusions (4%), multisystem trauma (4%). The shared feature among all: activation of neutrophils in the inflammatory response.

Step 2 — Patient Self-Induced Lung Injury (P-SILI)

📗 Marino Ch.24, p.429–430 — Critical Concept

P-SILI is lung injury caused by the patient's own vigorous breathing efforts — large negative swings in intrathoracic pressure promote "negative pressure pulmonary oedema" and alveolar overdistension from increased tidal volumes. Leaky pulmonary capillaries in ARDS amplify this effect.

Clinical implication (Marino p.430): "Delays to intubation and mechanical ventilation can have deleterious consequences for patients who continue to have labored breathing." If breathing remains distressed, intubate sooner rather than later. NIV failure (P/F not improving after 1 hour, or Vt ≥9.5 ml/kg PBW) = intubate.

Step 3 — O₂ Toxicity — An Underrecognised Risk

📗 Marino Ch.24, p.431 — "Don't Forget O₂ Toxicity"
  • Critically ill patients are often deficient in protective antioxidants (glutathione) → more susceptible to O₂ toxicity
  • High O₂ accelerates the damaging effects of mechanical ventilation
  • The lung injury in pulmonary O₂ toxicity is identical to ARDS — ARDS that progresses while breathing high FiO₂ could be O₂ toxicity worsening it
  • Limit FiO₂ whenever possible; target SpO₂ 88–95% (not 100%)

Step 4 — Why Prone Positioning Works

📗 Marino Ch.24, p.434

ARDS: dependent (posterior) lung regions are collapsed and consolidated; anterior regions are relatively aerated. Turning patient prone → blood flow shifts to previously aerated anterior regions → V/Q matching improves → oxygenation improves. Also promotes aeration in posterior segments → further V/Q improvement + facilitates CO₂ removal. During spontaneous breathing, prone position reduces respiratory rate and work of breathing.

Marino (p.434): "Prone positioning not only increases blood flow in the anterior region of the lungs, it also promotes aeration in posterior lung segments. Both effects serve to improve arterial oxygenation and facilitate CO₂ removal."

📋 3 · Guideline Recommendations

Latest Guideline Recommendations

2023 Global ARDS Definition (Matthay MA, et al. AJRCCM 2024) | ATS/ESICM/SCCM Guidelines 2017 | ISCCM 2020

A. Diagnosis

2023 Global ARDS Definition — Major Update
New criteria now include non-intubated patients: SpO₂/FiO₂ ≤315 on O₂ ≥0.3 OR HFNO ≥30 L/min OR NIV/CPAP ≥5 cmH₂O. PEEP ≥5 no longer mandatory for non-intubated. Expands ARDS recognition in resource-limited settings. 2023 Major Change

B. Lung-Protective Ventilation

ARDSNet (2000) + ATS/ESICM Guidelines Strong · High certainty
Tidal Volume: 6 ml/kg Predicted Body Weight (PBW). Plateau Pressure: ≤30 cmH₂O. Driving Pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP): <14–15 cmH₂O (best mortality predictor). SpO₂ target: 88–95%. Permissive hypercapnia: pH ≥7.25 acceptable.

C. Prone Positioning

PROSEVA Trial (2013) + ATS/ESICM Guidelines Strong · Moderate certainty
Prone positioning ≥16 consecutive hours/day for severe ARDS (P/F <150 on PEEP ≥5, FiO₂ ≥0.6) reduces 28-day mortality from 32.8% to 16% (NNT = 6). Implement within 36h of diagnosis. Marino (p.434): indicated for mechanically ventilated moderate-severe ARDS (P/F <150) ventilated at 6 ml/kg PBW.

D. HFNO and NIV

FLORALI Trial (2015) + 2023 Global ARDS Definition Conditional
HFNO (40–60 L/min) for mild ARDS (P/F 200–300) in non-intubated patients — reduces intubation rate vs conventional O₂. Monitor ROX index (SpO₂/FiO₂ ÷ RR): <4.88 at 2h = high risk of failure → intubate. Marino (p.430): "The reduction in intubation rate with HFNO is not consistent — intubation should be a serious consideration when the PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio remains critically low after 1 hour of NIV."

E. Corticosteroids

ATS/ESICM Guidelines + Marino Table 24.3 Conditional (selective)
Per Marino (Table 24.3):
• COVID-19 ARDS: Dexamethasone 6 mg IV/PO once daily × 10 days
• Moderate-severe ARDS (<72h, P/F <200): Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day IBW continuous infusion, taper over 14 days
• Persistent ARDS (7–14 days): Methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg/day (fibrinoproliferative phase)
Marino (p.432): "There have been innumerable trials of steroid therapy in ARDS since the disease was first described almost 50 years ago, yet the role continues to be debated. Simply stated, if a drug effect is not convincing after 50 years, it's time to move on."

F. Neuromuscular Blockade

ACURASYS (2010) vs ROSE (2019) Conditional — selective use
Cisatracurium 0.37 mg/kg IV bolus then 0.03–0.06 mg/kg/hr × 48h for severe dyssynchrony or refractory hypoxaemia. ACURASYS: benefit; ROSE: no benefit vs light sedation. Current practice: use selectively, not routinely.

G. Fluid Management

FACTT Trial (2006) Strong · Moderate certainty
Conservative fluid strategy after initial resuscitation: more ventilator-free days, shorter ICU stay, no increase in renal failure. Marino (p.432): "Fluid management should be aimed at avoiding or correcting a positive fluid balance, which can reduce the duration of mechanical ventilation." Target daily fluid balance neutral to negative from 24–48h.
💊 4 · Ventilator Settings & Drug Doses

Ventilator Settings & Drug Doses

IBW and Tidal Volume Calculations

Predicted Body Weight (Devine formula — per ARDSNet):
Male: PBW (kg) = 50 + 2.3 × (Height in inches − 60)
Female: PBW (kg) = 45.5 + 2.3 × (Height in inches − 60)
Quick: Male ≈ Ht(cm) − 100; Female ≈ Ht(cm) − 105

ARDSNet Lung-Protective Ventilator Targets
  • Mode: Volume Control AC (guaranteed Vt)
  • Tidal Volume: Start 8 ml/kg PBW → reduce by 1 ml/kg q2h → target 6 ml/kg PBW
  • Respiratory Rate: 14–35/min (to maintain pH ≥7.30)
  • Plateau Pressure (Pplat): ≤30 cmH₂O (measure by 0.5s inspiratory hold)
  • Driving Pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP): Target <14 cmH₂O
  • FiO₂: Minimum to achieve SpO₂ 88–95%
  • SpO₂ target: 88–95% (not 100%)
  • Permissive hypercapnia: PaCO₂ up to 55–60 mmHg acceptable (pH ≥7.25)

🗺 5 · Clinical Flowchart

ARDS Management — Step by Step

1

Recognition — Suspect ARDS

Bilateral opacities + P/F ≤300 + acute onset <7 days + not explained by cardiac failure. Get ABG, CXR, Echo (exclude cardiogenic). Apply 2023 Global criteria for non-intubated patients on HFNO ≥30 L/min.

2

Airway Decision

Mild (P/F 200–300): trial of HFNO 40–60 L/min. Check ROX index at 2h — if <4.88: prepare for intubation. Moderate-severe (P/F <200): intubate without delay. P-SILI risk with ongoing laboured breathing: Marino: "intubation sooner rather than later."

3

Lung-Protective Ventilation (ARDSNet)

  • Vt 6 ml/kg PBW (start at 8, reduce by 1/q2h)
  • Pplat ≤30 cmH₂O
  • ΔP = Pplat − PEEP: target <14 cmH₂O
  • Set PEEP per FiO₂/PEEP table
  • SpO₂ 88–95%, FiO₂ minimum effective
  • Permissive hypercapnia: pH ≥7.25 acceptable
4

Prone Positioning (Severe ARDS — P/F <150)

Prone ≥16 consecutive hours/day. PROSEVA: mortality 16% vs 32.8% (NNT=6). Implement within 36h of diagnosis. Minimum 4–5 staff. Pre-oxygenate. Continue until P/F consistently >150 off proning.

5

Fluid Management

Conservative strategy after haemodynamic stability: target neutral to negative fluid balance. Judicious furosemide. FACTT trial: conservative = more ventilator-free days. Monitor: daily weights, strict fluid balance charts, ECHO weekly.

6

Adjuncts (Refractory P/F <80 despite prone + LPV)

  • Inhaled nitric oxide or inhaled prostacyclin (bridge to ECMO)
  • Neuromuscular blockade (cisatracurium 48h)
  • VV-ECMO — refer to ECMO-capable centre
  • Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day) for moderate-severe
⚠️ 6 · Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Using Actual Body Weight for Tidal Volume

In obese patients, using actual weight instead of PBW causes dangerous volutrauma. A 90 kg patient at 165 cm has PBW ~63 kg. Vt 6 × 63 = 378 ml — NOT 540 ml. Always calculate PBW before setting Vt. (ARDSNet rule: ALWAYS use PBW)

❌ Mistake 2 — Targeting SpO₂ 98–100% in ARDS

High FiO₂ causes O₂ toxicity identical to ARDS. Marino (p.431): "The lung injury in pulmonary O₂ toxicity is identical to ARDS — cases that progress while on high FiO₂ could be O₂ toxicity worsening it." Target SpO₂ 88–95%. Minimise FiO₂.

❌ Mistake 3 — Aggressive Fluid Resuscitation After Initial Stabilisation

FACTT trial: liberal fluids in ARDS = more ventilator days. After haemodynamic stability, aim for neutral to negative daily fluid balance. Leaky pulmonary capillaries mean every extra litre goes into the lungs.

❌ Mistake 4 — Not Proning Soon Enough

PROSEVA criteria: P/F <150 on PEEP ≥5 + FiO₂ ≥0.6. Implement within 12–36h of diagnosis. Delaying "to see if they improve" misses the critical early treatment window. With NNT=6, proning saves 1 life per 6 patients treated.

❌ Mistake 5 — Delaying Intubation in P-SILI

Marino (p.430): patients breathing with vigorous effort risk P-SILI from large negative intrathoracic pressure swings. If SpO₂ <92% on HFNO after 1h, or breathing remains laboured, intubate sooner. "Delays to intubation can have deleterious consequences." NIV failure Vt >9.5 ml/kg = intubate immediately.

❌ Mistake 6 — Recruitment Manoeuvres in All ARDS

ART trial (2017): Aggressive RM + high PEEP caused harm — increased 28-day mortality vs ARDSNet strategy. Avoid routine sustained inflation recruitment manoeuvres. Use PEEP titration guided by driving pressure instead.

🎓 7 · Exam Pearls — DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM

Exam Pearls

Q: What is the 2023 Global ARDS definition's major new addition?
Includes non-intubated patients (on HFNO ≥30 L/min or NIV/CPAP ≥5 cmH₂O). SpO₂/FiO₂ ≤315 now accepted. Expands ARDS diagnosis beyond mechanically ventilated patients.

Q: P-SILI — what is it and what does Marino say about it?
Patient Self-Induced Lung Injury from vigorous breathing effort: large negative intrathoracic pressure swings → negative pressure pulmonary oedema + alveolar overdistension from large Vt. Marino (Ch.24, p.430): "Delays to intubation and mechanical ventilation can have deleterious consequences." Intubate sooner rather than later.

Q: ARDSNet tidal volume and what weight to use?
6 ml/kg Predicted Body Weight (PBW) — NEVER actual body weight. Male PBW = 50 + 2.3 × (Ht inches − 60). Female = 45.5 + 2.3 × (Ht inches − 60). Using actual weight in obese patient = volutrauma = worsens ARDS.

Q: PROSEVA trial — indications, result, NNT?
Severe ARDS (P/F <150, PEEP ≥5). Prone ≥16h/day. Result: 28-day mortality 16% vs 32.8% in supine. NNT = 6. Currently one of the strongest interventions in critical care medicine. Implement within 36h.

Q: Why is O₂ toxicity important in ARDS? (Marino insight)
Marino (Ch.24, p.431): lung injury from O₂ toxicity is identical to ARDS. ARDS that progresses while breathing high FiO₂ could be O₂ toxicity compounding the injury. Critically ill patients are deficient in protective antioxidants. Minimise FiO₂ — target SpO₂ 88–95%.

Q: Driving pressure — what is it and why does it matter?
ΔP = Pplat − PEEP. Represents stress applied to the aerated "baby lung" in ARDS. Amato (NEJM 2015): driving pressure is the single best predictor of ARDS mortality. Target <14–15 cmH₂O. Reduce Vt or optimise PEEP to minimise ΔP.

Q: Corticosteroid recommendation in non-COVID ARDS per Marino 5th Ed?
Moderate-severe (P/F <200): Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day IBW by continuous infusion in first 72h; 2 mg/kg/day for persistent ARDS at 7–14 days (fibrinoproliferative phase). Slow taper over 14 days. Marino (p.432) notes the long debate and inconsistent results.
