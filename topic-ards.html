Definition & Staging

2023 Global ARDS Definition — Key Update

The 2023 Global Definition expands ARDS beyond intubated patients to include those on high-flow nasal oxygen (HFNO ≥30 L/min) and non-invasive ventilation (CPAP/NIV ≥5 cmH₂O), improving recognition in resource-limited settings where early intubation may not be feasible.

2023 Global ARDS Diagnostic Criteria (all must be met within 1 week of clinical insult)

  • Acute onset: Within 1 week of known clinical insult or new/worsening respiratory symptoms
  • Bilateral opacities: On CXR or CT — not fully explained by effusions, lobar collapse, or nodules
  • Not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload
  • Oxygenation impairment: SpO₂/FiO₂ ≤315 on O₂ ≥0.21 (non-intubated) OR PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤300 on PEEP ≥5 cmH₂O (intubated)

Pathophysiology

Diffuse Alveolar Damage (DAD)

ARDS results from direct (pulmonary) or indirect (extrapulmonary) injury to the alveolar-capillary membrane:

  • Direct causes: Pneumonia (bacterial, viral, COVID-19), aspiration, pulmonary contusion, inhalation injury
  • Indirect causes: Sepsis (most common — 40% of ARDS), pancreatitis, massive transfusion (TRALI), fat embolism, major surgery

Phases of ARDS

  • Exudative (Days 1–7): Alveolar flooding with protein-rich oedema, hyaline membrane formation, neutrophil infiltration, alveolar collapse. CT: dependent consolidation + anterior ground-glass opacities = "baby lung" (only 20–30% aerated)
  • Proliferative (Days 7–14): Type II pneumocyte proliferation, fibroblast ingrowth, early fibrosis
  • Fibrotic (beyond 2 weeks): Established fibrosis — poor prognosis if reached

Ventilator-Induced Lung Injury (VILI) — The Core Problem

The main goal of ventilatory management is preventing VILI through 4 mechanisms:

  • Volutrauma: Overdistension from high tidal volumes
  • Barotrauma: High pressures (Pplat >30 cmH₂O)
  • Atelectrauma: Repetitive opening and closing of collapsed alveoli
  • Biotrauma: Mechanical stretch triggers cytokine release, worsening systemic inflammation

Driving pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP) is the single best predictor of ARDS mortality. ΔP >15 cmH₂O independently predicts death — minimise it above all other targets.

Diagnosis & Differential Diagnosis

Investigations

  • ABG: P/F ratio, pH, PaCO₂
  • CXR: bilateral infiltrates (less sensitive — misses ~30%)
  • CT chest (if doubt): consolidation pattern, barotrauma, alternative diagnosis
  • Echo: exclude cardiogenic oedema (LVEDP, wall motion abnormality)
  • BNP/NT-proBNP: elevated in cardiac pulmonary oedema
  • BAL: if immunocompromised — PCP, CMV, fungal causes

Lung-Protective Ventilation (ARDSNet Protocol)

ARDSNet Targets — Apply to ALL Ventilated ARDS Patients
  • Tidal Volume: 6 ml/kg Predicted Body Weight (PBW) — start at 8 ml/kg, reduce by 1 ml/kg q2h to reach 6 ml/kg
  • Plateau Pressure (Pplat): ≤ 30 cmH₂O (measure by 0.5s inspiratory hold)
  • Driving Pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP): Target < 14–15 cmH₂O
  • Respiratory Rate: 14–35/min; adjust to maintain pH ≥ 7.30
  • Permissive Hypercapnia: pH ≥ 7.25; PaCO₂ up to 50–60 mmHg acceptable
  • SpO₂ target: 88–95%; PaO₂ 55–80 mmHg
  • FiO₂: Titrate to minimum to achieve SpO₂ target
  • I:E ratio: 1:1 to 1:3 (start 1:2)

Predicted Body Weight Calculation

  • Males: PBW (kg) = 50 + 2.3 × [height (inches) − 60]
  • Females: PBW (kg) = 45.5 + 2.3 × [height (inches) − 60]
  • Approximate: Males PBW ≈ Height (cm) − 100; Females ≈ Height (cm) − 105
Never base Vt on actual body weight

In obese patients, using actual weight will cause dangerous overdistension. Always use predicted body weight based on height and sex. A 90 kg patient who is 165 cm tall has PBW of ~63 kg (female) — Vt = 6 × 63 = 378 ml, NOT 540 ml.

PEEP Strategy

PEEP prevents alveolar derecruitment (atelectrauma) and improves oxygenation by keeping collapsed alveoli open. However, too-high PEEP causes haemodynamic compromise and overdistension.

Higher PEEP (Moderate-Severe ARDS)

For P/F <200, consider higher PEEP titration guided by driving pressure. If higher PEEP reduces ΔP → lung is recruitable. If ΔP increases with higher PEEP → over-distension, reduce PEEP.

Recruitment Manoeuvres — Use with Caution

ART trial (2017) showed that aggressive recruitment manoeuvres (RM) + high PEEP caused harm — increased 28-day mortality vs ARDSNet strategy. Use RMs only as rescue for refractory hypoxaemia, not routinely. Brief sustained inflation (40 cmH₂O × 40 sec) has mixed evidence.

Prone Positioning

PROSEVA (2013) — Most Impactful ARDS Trial

Prone ≥16 hours/day for severe ARDS (P/F <150) reduced 28-day mortality from 32.8% to 16% (NNT = 6). This is the strongest intervention in ARDS management. Implement early — within 12–36 hours of moderate-severe ARDS diagnosis.

Indications (SSC/ESICM 2017)

  • Severe ARDS: P/F <150 on PEEP ≥5, FiO₂ ≥0.6
  • After ≥12–24 hours of optimised supine ventilation
  • Minimum session duration: ≥16 consecutive hours

Prone Positioning Protocol

  1. Pre-prone preparation: Minimum 4–5 staff; pre-oxygenate; pause enteral feeds 30–60 min; empty bladder; secure all lines/ETT; ocular protection
  2. Sedation: Deep sedation (RASS −3 to −4) + ensure NMB if dyssynchrony
  3. Turn manoeuvre: Log-roll in one smooth coordinated movement; one person dedicated to head/ETT
  4. Positioning: Arms in swimmer's position or alongside body; head rotation every 2h; padded pressure points
  5. Monitoring: Check ETT position; monitor SpO₂, BP, HR throughout
  6. Resupinate: After ≥16h or if deterioration (SpO₂ <85% for >5 min despite interventions)

Contraindications to Prone Positioning

  • Absolute: Spinal instability, elevated ICP, open abdomen, anterior chest wound
  • Relative: Facial fractures, pregnancy (>20 weeks), haemodynamic instability, uncontrolled arrhythmia, recent sternotomy

Adjunctive Therapies

Neuromuscular Blockade (NMB)

Cisatracurium 0.37 mg/kg bolus then 0.03–0.06 mg/kg/hr infusion for 48h in severe ARDS (P/F <150).

  • ACURASYS (2010): Improved 90-day survival, less barotrauma
  • ROSE (2019): No difference vs light sedation strategy — NMB not routinely required
  • Current practice: Use NMB selectively for severe ventilator dyssynchrony, refractory hypoxaemia despite prone, or when driving pressure cannot be controlled otherwise. Monitor with train-of-four. 2B

High-Flow Nasal Oxygen (HFNO)

  • For mild ARDS (P/F 200–300) in non-intubated patients: HFNO at 40–60 L/min
  • FLORALI trial (2015): HFNO superior to conventional O₂ and face-mask NIV in hypoxaemic respiratory failure
  • ROX Index = SpO₂/FiO₂ ÷ RR — if <4.88 at 2h or 6h → high risk of failure → intubate early
  • Do not delay intubation with HFNO in rapidly worsening patients

Inhaled Pulmonary Vasodilators

  • Inhaled Nitric Oxide (iNO): Improves V/Q matching and oxygenation acutely — no survival benefit (Cochrane 2016). Use as bridge to ECMO or as rescue in refractory hypoxaemia (P/F <80). 2B
  • Inhaled Prostacyclin (Epoprostenol/Iloprost): Similar profile to iNO; cheaper, easier to administer. Use as alternative rescue measure.

Venovenous ECMO (VV-ECMO)

  • For reversible severe ARDS: P/F <80 on optimal ventilatory settings despite prone positioning
  • EOLIA (2018): VV-ECMO did not meet primary endpoint (28-day mortality) due to crossover bias; post-hoc analysis shows benefit
  • Refer early to ECMO-capable centre. VV-ECMO is best initiated before respiratory failure worsens to the point of haemodynamic compromise
  • Centres in India: AIIMS Delhi, PGI Chandigarh, CMC Vellore, SGPGIMS Lucknow

Corticosteroids in ARDS

  • Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (or dexamethasone 20 mg/day × 5 days then 10 mg × 5 days): may reduce duration of MV in moderate-severe ARDS (DEXA-ARDS trial 2020)
  • Do not start after day 14 of ARDS — worsens outcome (LaSRS trial)
  • Best evidence for early moderate-severe ARDS (P/F <200). 2B

Fluid Management in ARDS

After haemodynamic stabilisation, use a conservative fluid strategy. FACTT trial (2006): Conservative fluid strategy improved ventilator-free days and ICU-free days without increasing non-pulmonary organ failure.

  • Target neutral to negative fluid balance from 24–48h after resuscitation
  • Judicious furosemide to achieve target — 20–40 mg IV BD if tolerated haemodynamically
  • Daily weights; strict fluid balance charting
  • Avoid fluid creep from infusions/medications — use concentrated drips

Weaning from Mechanical Ventilation

ARDS patients often have prolonged MV dependency. Systematic approach:

Criteria for SBT Trial

  • P/F ≥ 150 (some suggest ≥ 200) on PEEP ≤ 8 cmH₂O and FiO₂ ≤ 0.5
  • Patient awake and following commands (RASS −1 to +1)
  • Haemodynamically stable (no or low vasopressors)
  • No new deterioration in last 24h
  • Adequate cough/airway protection

SBT Method

Pressure Support 5–7 cmH₂O + PEEP 5 cmH₂O × 30–120 minutes. Successful if: SpO₂ ≥92%, RR <35, no significant distress, HR/BP stable. RSB index (RR/Vt) <105 predicts success.

Indian Context

🇮🇳 ISCCM Perspective
  • COVID-19 ARDS: ISCCM published specific COVID ARDS management guidelines (2021) with emphasis on HFNO before intubation in suitable patients, awake proning, and dexamethasone
  • Scrub typhus ARDS: Emerging cause in India (Tamil Nadu, Himalayan foothills, Northeast) — consider doxycycline 100 mg BD in addition to ventilatory management
  • Prone positioning without ICU beds: Awake prone positioning (12–16h/day) for spontaneously breathing COVID/non-COVID ARDS patients on HFNO is feasible and improves oxygenation
  • VV-ECMO availability: Limited to tertiary centres; early transfer referral is critical
  • Ventilator shortages: ISCCM guidelines include ventilator-sharing protocols for resource-limited situations (published 2020)
