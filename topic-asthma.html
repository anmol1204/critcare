📘 1 · Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed

Washington Manual Summary

"Status asthmaticus is one of the most reversible causes of acute respiratory failure. With aggressive bronchodilator therapy and careful ventilator management, the majority of patients can be successfully managed in the ICU and extubated within 24–72 hours."

Washington Manual of Critical Care, 4th Ed. (Kollef & Witt, eds.) Wolters Kluwer 2023.

Clinical Synopsis

  • Definition: Acute exacerbation of asthma not responding to standard initial treatment; requiring escalating therapy or mechanical ventilation
  • Mortality: Need for mechanical ventilation is the strongest risk factor for death (Marino Ch.23, p.405)
  • Severity: Mild (PEFR ≥70% predicted) | Moderate (40–69%) | Severe (<40%) — Marino (p.406)
  • Rising PaCO₂ = danger: Normal PaCO₂ in severe asthma means the patient is TIRING (losing their compensatory hyperventilation) — impending respiratory arrest

📗 2 · Marino's The ICU Book, 5th Edition (2025)

Marino Physiology — Asthma in the ICU

"For patients with asthma, the need for mechanical ventilation has been identified as the strongest risk factor for death."

Marino PL. The ICU Book, 5th Ed. Chapter 23: Asthma and COPD in the ICU, p.405. Wolters Kluwer; 2025.

Drug Delivery — MDI vs Nebuliser (Marino Table 23.1)

📗 Marino Ch.23, p.407 — Actual Lung Deposition Data

Marino Table 23.1 (actual data):

SiteNebuliser (2.5 mg)MDI (200 mcg)MDI + Spacer (200 mcg)
Exhaled20%1%1%
Apparatus66%10%78%
Oropharynx2%80%1%
Lungs12%9%20%

MDI with spacer delivers DOUBLE the lung deposition of either MDI alone or nebuliser. "Bronchodilator treatments with nebulizers and MDIs produce equivalent results despite a marked difference in drug dosage." (Marino, p.408). Spacers are recommended for all bronchodilator treatments using MDIs.

Auto-PEEP (Dynamic Hyperinflation) — The Ventilated Asthmatic

📗 Marino Ch.23, p.416–419 — Critical ICU Concept

In ventilated asthmatics: severe bronchospasm → prolonged exhalation → gas trapping → auto-PEEP (intrinsic PEEP). High intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return → haemodynamic collapse. Marino: "If you want to feel what it is like to breathe in a hyperinflated state, take a deep breath, and try breathing from there."

Prevention during MV: (1) Avoid inflation volumes >6–8 ml/kg; (2) Maximise expiratory time — slow rate + ↑ inspiratory flow rate so inflation occupies only 1/3 of respiratory cycle; (3) Monitor intrinsic PEEP with expiratory hold.

Marino's A Final Word (Ch.23, p.419): "Give bronchodilators and corticosteroids to all patients... If the condition is severe, use noninvasive ventilation if possible, and conventional mechanical ventilation if necessary. That's about it."

📋 3 · GINA 2026 Guidelines

GINA 2026 — Key Recommendations

Global Initiative for Asthma (GINA). Global Strategy for Asthma Management and Prevention. Updated May 2026. www.ginasthma.org
GINA 2026 — Oxygen Targets Changed from previous guidance
Do NOT give supplemental O₂ unless SpO₂ <92%. If giving O₂, target SpO₂ 92–95% in adults and adolescents (NOT 100%). High-concentration O₂ worsens outcomes (Haldane effect → V/Q mismatch). This is Evidence A. Major 2026 Change
GINA 2026 — SABA Dosing Conservative — Revised
More conservative SABA dosing recommended due to evidence of SABA toxicity: lactic acidosis → compensatory hyperventilation → misinterpreted as worsening → more SABA → vicious cycle. Shake pMDI before EACH actuation. Consider ICS-formoterol as alternative for mild exacerbations in ED. Revised 2026
GINA 2026 — Anaphylaxis + Asthma New 2026
If features of anaphylaxis as well as asthma: give Adrenaline IM FIRST, then bronchodilators. Epinephrine can be given intranasally in children. This is now explicit in all 4 new GINA 2026 flowcharts.
GINA 2026 — New Standardised Flowcharts New 2026
4 new standardised flowcharts for acute asthma in primary care and ED (adults/adolescents/children). Includes criteria for mild/moderate/severe/life-threatening assessment. Validated clinical score (PRAM/PAS) recommended for children <18 years.

🗺 5 · Clinical Flowchart

Acute Severe Asthma — Step by Step

1

Anaphylaxis? — Check First (GINA 2026)

If urticaria/angioedema/haemodynamic compromise alongside asthma → Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM anterolateral thigh FIRST, then bronchodilators. Missing anaphylaxis = potentially fatal error.

2

O₂ — Controlled (GINA 2026 Change)

Give O₂ ONLY if SpO₂ <92%. Target 92–95% (NOT 100%). High-concentration O₂ worsens V/Q mismatch (Haldane effect) and increases risk of hypercapnia. Use Venturi mask 24–28% or titrated nasal prongs.

3

Bronchodilators — Salbutamol + Ipratropium

Salbutamol 4–8 puffs pMDI+spacer q20 min × 3 OR 2.5–5 mg neb q20 min × 3. Shake pMDI before each puff (GINA 2026). Add Ipratropium 0.5 mg neb × 3 in ED → STOP after initial phase (no benefit in hospitalised patients).

4

Corticosteroids + MgSO₄

Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV q6h OR Prednisolone 40–50 mg PO OD × 5–7 days. MgSO₄ 2g IV over 20 min if no response to initial salbutamol — Evidence A: reduces hospitalisations.

5

Intubation — When to Intubate

Indications: GCS falling, PaCO₂ rising (normal CO₂ in severe asthma = DANGER), SpO₂ <85% on max O₂, exhaustion. RSI: Ketamine 1.5–2 mg/kg + Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg. AVOID morphine + suxamethonium (histamine release).

6

Ventilating the Asthmatic

Mode: VCV. Vt 6–8 ml/kg PBW. RR 10–14/min (LOW — allow expiratory time). I:E 1:3 to 1:5. PEEP: minimal (3–5). Flow 60–100 L/min. Accept PaCO₂ up to 80 mmHg (pH ≥7.15). Monitor auto-PEEP. If sudden haemodynamic collapse: DISCONNECT from ventilator 30 sec.

⚠️ 6 · Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Giving 100% O₂ Routinely

GINA 2026: Do NOT give O₂ unless SpO₂ <92%. Target 92–95%, NOT 100%. High-concentration O₂ worsens V/Q mismatch, increases risk of hypercapnia (Haldane effect), and associated with worse outcomes. This was changed in GINA 2026.

❌ Mistake 2 — Missing Anaphylaxis Component

GINA 2026 now explicitly states: anaphylaxis + asthma → Adrenaline IM FIRST. Missing the anaphylaxis component and only giving bronchodilators can be fatal. Check for urticaria, angioedema, or haemodynamic compromise in every severe asthma presentation.

❌ Mistake 3 — Using Morphine or Suxamethonium for RSI

Both cause histamine release → severe bronchospasm → catastrophic worsening. Use ketamine (bronchodilator) + rocuronium for RSI in status asthmaticus. Ketamine is the drug of choice.

❌ Mistake 4 — High RR on the Ventilator

Marino (Ch.23, p.418): high RR + auto-PEEP in asthma = ↑ intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return → haemodynamic collapse. Use RR 10–14/min, I:E 1:3–1:5, high inspiratory flow rate (60–100 L/min) to maximise expiratory time. If sudden BP collapse: disconnect from ventilator.

❌ Mistake 5 — Normal CO₂ as Reassurance

Severe asthma patients hyperventilate → PaCO₂ should be LOW (25–35). A "normal" PaCO₂ of 40 means they are TIRING — impending respiratory arrest. Rising PaCO₂ toward normal = intubate urgently.

🎓 7 · Exam Pearls — DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM

Exam Pearls

Q: GINA 2026 O₂ target in acute asthma?
Do NOT give O₂ unless SpO₂ <92%. Target 92–95% (NOT 100%). High-concentration O₂ worsens V/Q mismatch (Haldane effect). This is a major GINA 2026 change (Evidence A). Controlled O₂ associated with lower mortality.

Q: MDI vs nebuliser — Marino's data?
Marino Table 23.1 (Ch.23, p.407): Nebuliser delivers 12% to lungs; MDI alone 9%; MDI + spacer 20%. MDI+spacer delivers double the lung deposition. Clinically equivalent if correct technique used. Spacers recommended for all MDI bronchodilator treatments.

Q: Drug of choice for RSI in status asthmaticus?
Ketamine 1.5–2 mg/kg IV — bronchodilator (releases catecholamines), preserves BP, analgesic. Muscle relaxant: Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg IV. AVOID morphine (histamine release) and suxamethonium (histamine → bronchospasm).

Q: When do you intubate a patient with acute severe asthma?
Indications: Falling GCS, rising PaCO₂ (toward or above normal), SpO₂ <85% on max O₂, exhaustion, silent chest. Normal PaCO₂ in severe asthma = DANGER SIGN (patient losing their hyperventilatory compensation). Do not wait for arrest.

Q: Marino's A Final Word on asthma/COPD management?
"Give bronchodilators and corticosteroids to all patients, plus antibiotics for patients with severe COPD. If the condition is severe or progresses despite the above measures, use noninvasive ventilation if possible, and conventional mechanical ventilation if necessary. That's about it." (Marino Ch.23, p.419)
