How Asthma Works — The Pathophysiology

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterised by variable airflow obstruction. Three pathological processes occur simultaneously:

1. Airway Inflammation (the core lesion)

Allergens, viral infections, or irritants trigger a Th2-driven immune response. Mast cells, eosinophils, and CD4+ T-helper cells release histamine, leukotrienes (LTC4, LTD4, LTE4), prostaglandins, and cytokines (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13). This causes:

  • Mucosal oedema — swelling of airway walls narrows lumen
  • Mucus hypersecretion — plugs narrow airways ("mucus plugging" in fatal asthma)
  • Epithelial damage — denuded mucosa exposes sensory nerve endings → hyperresponsiveness

2. Bronchospasm (the acute trigger)

Smooth muscle contraction (mediated by acetylcholine, histamine, leukotrienes) narrows the airway lumen. This is the reversible component — responds to bronchodilators. In severe/status asthmaticus, smooth muscle may be in sustained spasm despite maximal treatment.

3. Airway Remodelling (in chronic disease)

Repeated inflammation causes structural changes: sub-epithelial fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy, angiogenesis. This leads to fixed airflow obstruction that does not fully reverse.

The Physiological Cascade in Status Asthmaticus

  • Severe bronchospasm → markedly ↑ airway resistance → ↑ work of breathing
  • Air trapping → hyperinflation → auto-PEEP → haemodynamic compromise (↓ venous return)
  • Hypoxaemia → hypocapnia early (↑ RR, hyperventilation), then hypercapnia late (respiratory muscle fatigue)
  • Rising PaCO₂ in a patient who was hyperventilating = RED FLAG — impending respiratory arrest
  • Lactic acidosis from beta-agonist overuse (β₂ stimulation → glycolysis → lactic acid) — may mimic worsening asthma → vicious cycle of over-treatment

Emergency Department Management (GINA 2026)

GINA 2026 — Standardised ED Management Algorithm
  • Step 1 — Anaphylaxis? If features of anaphylaxis present (urticaria, angioedema, haemodynamic compromise): Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM FIRST, then bronchodilators
  • Step 2 — Assess severity (table above). Measure SpO₂, PEF if possible, RR, HR, consciousness
  • Step 3 — Start treatment simultaneously with assessment: SABA + ipratropium + systemic corticosteroids if moderate/severe
  • Step 4 — Oxygen ONLY if SpO₂ <92%; target 92–95% (NOT 100%)
  • Step 5 — Reassess after 20 min: PEF, SpO₂, symptoms. If improving → continue; if not → escalate
  • Step 6 — Spirometry/PEF before discharge — must measure, even after clinical improvement

Oxygen Therapy — GINA 2026 Changed

GINA 2026 — New Oxygen Guidance (Major Change)

Do NOT give supplemental oxygen routinely in asthma. Give oxygen only if SpO₂ <92%. If giving oxygen, target SpO₂ 92–95% in adults and adolescents (NOT 100%).

Why: High-concentration O₂ causes V/Q mismatch (Haldane effect) → worsens hypercapnia. Hyperoxia also abolishes hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction → increases dead space. Controlled O₂ is associated with lower mortality and better outcomes than high-concentration oxygen (Evidence A).

In children 5 years and younger: target SpO₂ ≥92% if oxygen is given.

Drug Doses in Acute Asthma (GINA 2026)

Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (SABA)

GINA 2026 — Conservative SABA Dosing (Changed)

GINA 2026 reduces SABA doses due to evidence of SABA toxicity: lactic acidosis, hypokalaemia, tachycardia. Lactic acidosis from SABA causes compensatory hyperventilation that can be misinterpreted as worsening asthma → further SABA administration → vicious cycle.

ICS-Formoterol in Acute Asthma — New GINA 2026 New

For adults/adolescents with a mild exacerbation presenting in primary care or ED: Budesonide-formoterol (160/4.5 mcg or 200/6 mcg metered dose) is now an option as an anti-inflammatory reliever, as alternative or addition to SABA. Maximum 12 inhalations in 24h (combined as-needed + maintenance doses). This provides opportunity to start MART (maintenance-and-reliever therapy) before discharge to prevent future exacerbations.

Magnesium Sulphate — Rescue Therapy

IV Magnesium — When SABA + Ipratropium + Steroids Fail

Dose: MgSO₄ 2g IV over 20 minutes (adults). Evidence A: reduces hospitalisations in severe asthma not responding to initial treatment. Mechanism: smooth muscle relaxation (competes with Ca²⁺). Safe even with hypokalaemia from SABA. Check Mg levels — if low, replace as well.

ICU Admission & Intubation

Indications for ICU Admission

  • Life-threatening features (any one) — see severity table
  • No improvement or deterioration after 1 hour of maximal treatment
  • PaCO₂ rising toward normal despite aggressive treatment
  • Requiring IV bronchodilators or magnesium
  • Concerns about fatigue or ability to maintain airway

When to Intubate

Intubate Early — Don't Wait for Cardiac Arrest
  • Respiratory arrest or peri-arrest
  • Altered consciousness (GCS <13, confusion, agitation from hypercapnia)
  • Rising PaCO₂ despite maximal medical therapy
  • SpO₂ <85% on FiO₂ 1.0 (no response to O₂)
  • Exhaustion — patient cannot maintain respiratory effort

Ventilating the Asthmatic Patient — Special Considerations

Asthma Ventilation is Different from ARDS

The primary problem is severe airflow obstruction (↑ resistance) NOT reduced compliance. Auto-PEEP and gas trapping are the main threats — not derecruitment. High PEEP and high RR are DANGEROUS in asthma.

Ventilator Strategy for Status Asthmaticus
  • Mode: Volume Control AC (guaranteed Vt)
  • Tidal Volume: 6–8 ml/kg IBW (lung-protective)
  • Rate: 10–14/min (LOW — allow adequate expiratory time)
  • I:E ratio: 1:3 to 1:5 (prolonged expiration — CRITICAL)
  • Flow rate: 60–100 L/min (fast inspiration to allow more expiratory time)
  • PEEP: Minimal (3–5 cmH₂O) — dynamic hyperinflation makes auto-PEEP; extrinsic PEEP worsens this
  • FiO₂: Titrate to SpO₂ 92–95%
  • Accept: Permissive hypercapnia (PaCO₂ up to 80–100 mmHg; pH ≥7.15) — safe in asthma

Auto-PEEP (Dynamic Hyperinflation) — The Major Danger

Incomplete expiration → gas trapping → lungs over-inflate → ↑ intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return → hypotension. If sudden haemodynamic collapse on ventilator:

  1. Disconnect from ventilator immediately for 30–60 seconds (allow passive decompression)
  2. If BP recovers → dynamic hyperinflation was the cause
  3. Measure auto-PEEP with expiratory hold manoeuvre
  4. Reduce RR, prolong expiratory time, reduce Vt

Mucus Plugging

A major cause of refractory status asthmaticus. Consider bronchoscopy for mucus plug clearance in ventilated patients who are not improving despite maximal bronchodilator therapy. Warm humidified gases help mobilise secretions.

Discharge Planning (GINA 2026)

  • Spirometry or PEF before discharge — document baseline
  • Initiate or step up ICS-containing treatment: preferred = MART with ICS-formoterol at Step 4
  • SABA-only treatment is NO LONGER recommended — every patient needs ICS
  • Prescribe prednisolone 40–50 mg/day × 5–7 days (or dexamethasone 0.3–0.6 mg/kg × 1–2 days)
  • Written Asthma Action Plan — mandatory before discharge
  • Even one exacerbation = "red flag" → review inhaler technique, adherence, triggers
  • Follow-up within 1–2 weeks (preferably before stopping OCS)

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — Normal CO₂ = Danger in Asthma

A patient with severe asthma SHOULD be hyperventilating → low PaCO₂. A "normal" CO₂ of 40 mmHg in a severe asthma attack means they're tiring — impending respiratory failure. Rising CO₂ = emergency.

💡 Pearl 2 — O₂ Target Changed in 2026

GINA 2026: Don't give O₂ unless SpO₂ <92%. Target 92–95%, NOT 100%. High-concentration O₂ worsens V/Q mismatch and may worsen hypercapnia (Haldane effect).

💡 Pearl 3 — SABA Causes Lactic Acidosis

High-dose SABA (β₂ stimulation) → glycolysis → lactate production → lactic acidosis → compensatory hyperventilation → misinterpreted as worsening asthma → more SABA. GINA 2026 now recommends conservative SABA dosing.

💡 Pearl 4 — Magnesium is the Rescue Drug

When SABA + ipratropium + steroids aren't working after 1 hour → MgSO₄ 2g IV over 20 min. Evidence A for reducing hospitalisations. Safe, cheap, available everywhere in India.

💡 Pearl 5 — Ketamine for Induction in Status Asthmaticus

Ketamine is the drug of choice for RSI in severe asthma. Releases catecholamines → bronchodilation. Also provides analgesia and sedation. Avoid morphine (histamine release) and suxamethonium (histamine release → worsening bronchospasm).

💡 Pearl 6 — Disconnect if Haemodynamic Collapse on Vent

Sudden hypotension in ventilated asthmatic = dynamic hyperinflation (auto-PEEP) or tension pneumothorax. First step: disconnect from ventilator. If BP recovers in 30–60 sec = auto-PEEP. Reduce rate, prolong expiratory time.

💡 Pearl 7 — Don't Forget Anaphylaxis

GINA 2026 now explicitly states: if features of anaphylaxis accompany asthma exacerbation → Adrenaline IM FIRST, then bronchodilators. Food allergy + asthma = high-risk combination for fatal attacks.

💡 Pearl 8 — Shake Salbutamol pMDI Each Time

GINA 2026 specifically highlights: shake the salbutamol inhaler immediately before EACH actuation. New inhaler without shaking delivers ultra-high doses; near-empty inhaler without shaking delivers very low doses. This simple step is often missed.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Giving 100% O₂ Routinely

GINA 2026 changed this. High-concentration O₂ worsens outcomes. Only give O₂ if SpO₂ <92%, and target 92–95%. Controlled O₂ reduces mortality (Evidence A).

❌ Mistake 2 — Using Morphine for Sedation/Intubation

Morphine causes histamine release from mast cells → worsens bronchospasm. Also suxamethonium causes histamine release. Use ketamine + rocuronium for RSI in status asthmaticus.

❌ Mistake 3 — High PEEP and High RR on the Ventilator

Asthma is a high-resistance disease (not low compliance like ARDS). High RR → less expiratory time → more air trapping → auto-PEEP → haemodynamic collapse. Use RR 10–14/min, prolonged I:E (1:3 to 1:5), minimal extrinsic PEEP.

❌ Mistake 4 — Reassured by Normal SpO₂

In severe asthma, patients can maintain SpO₂ by extreme hyperventilation (tachypnoea + ↑ effort). Normal SpO₂ with RR >30 and accessory muscle use = still severe. Watch PaCO₂ — normal CO₂ in this context = exhaustion and failure.

❌ Mistake 5 — Not Starting ICS at Discharge

Every patient who presents with an asthma exacerbation needs ICS-containing treatment started before or at discharge. SABA-only treatment is no longer recommended. Missed opportunity = higher risk of future attacks.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM)

Q: GINA 2026 oxygen target in acute asthma? → Do NOT give O₂ unless SpO₂ <92%. If giving O₂, target 92–95% (NOT 100%). High-concentration O₂ associated with worse outcomes (Haldane effect, V/Q mismatch worsening).

Q: Most dangerous sign in ventilated asthmatic? → Sudden haemodynamic collapse = auto-PEEP / dynamic hyperinflation. First action: disconnect from ventilator. If BP recovers → dynamic hyperinflation confirmed.

Q: Why is normal PaCO₂ dangerous in acute severe asthma? → Severe asthma → hypoxic drive → hyperventilation → PaCO₂ falls. A "normal" PaCO₂ (35–45) in severe asthma means the patient is no longer hyperventilating → respiratory muscle fatigue → impending arrest.

Q: Drug of choice for RSI in status asthmaticus? → Ketamine (bronchodilator, releases catecholamines) + Rocuronium. Avoid morphine (histamine → bronchospasm) and suxamethonium (histamine release).

Q: What is the new GINA 2026 recommendation for ICS-formoterol in acute asthma? → ICS-formoterol (budesonide-formoterol) is now an option for mild exacerbations in primary care and ED as alternative to SABA. Max 12 inhalations/24h (combined as-needed + maintenance doses).

Q: Rescue therapy for severe asthma not responding to SABA + ipratropium + steroids? → MgSO₄ 2g IV over 20 min. Evidence A: reduces hospitalisations in severe asthma.

Q: Ventilator I:E ratio in status asthmaticus? → 1:3 to 1:5 (prolonged expiratory time). This is opposite to ARDS (1:2). Low RR (10–14/min) to allow complete exhalation and prevent gas trapping.
