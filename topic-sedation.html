ABCDEF Bundle

The ABCDEF bundle is an evidence-based, interprofessional framework for optimising outcomes in ICU patients. Implementing all components together is more effective than individual elements.

Analgesia First (eCASH)

eCASH: early Comfort using Analgesia, minimal Sedatives, maximal Humane care. The modern paradigm: treat pain before adding sedation.

Principle: Comfort > Sedation
  • Start with IV opioid analgesia (fentanyl or morphine infusion) — most agitation in ICU is pain-driven
  • Target CPOT 0–2 (pain absent or mild) before assessing need for sedation
  • Add sedation only if still agitated/distressed after adequate analgesia
  • Target lightest effective sedation: RASS −1 to 0 for most ventilated patients
  • Perform daily SAT (spontaneous awakening trial) — pause all sedation and reassess

Target: RASS −1 to 0 (most patients). RASS −2 to −3 only for ARDS/high vent support/raised ICP/active seizures.

CPOT — Critical-Care Pain Observation Tool

4 domains: facial expression, body movements, muscle tension, compliance with ventilator. Score 0–8. CPOT ≥ 3 indicates significant pain — increase analgesia.

Propofol Infusion Syndrome (PRIS)

Life-threatening: metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac failure, renal failure, hypertriglyceridaemia. Risk factors: dose >4mg/kg/hr, duration >48h, critical illness, steroids, catecholamines. Monitor: triglycerides, CK, lactate. Stop propofol and switch if suspected.

Delirium in the ICU

ICU delirium affects 60–80% of ventilated patients. Associated with increased mortality, prolonged MV, cognitive impairment, and longer LOS.

CAM-ICU (assess twice daily)

Four features — positive if Feature 1 AND 2 AND (3 OR 4):

  • Feature 1: Acute change or fluctuating course in mental status
  • Feature 2: Inattention (letters test: squeeze hand on 'A' in "SAVEAHAART" — >2 errors = positive)
  • Feature 3: Altered level of consciousness (RASS ≠ 0)
  • Feature 4: Disorganised thinking (4 simple yes/no questions; squeeze fingers command — >1 error = positive)

Non-pharmacological prevention (first line)

  • Orientation: daily reorientation, clock/calendar visible, familiar objects
  • Sleep hygiene: cluster care, reduce noise at night, ear plugs, eye masks
  • Early mobilisation: PT/OT from ICU day 1
  • Remove physical restraints where safe
  • Hearing aids, glasses — ensure patients have their aids
  • Minimise anticholinergic drugs, benzodiazepines, opioid dose
  • Treat reversible causes: pain, constipation, urinary retention, hypoxia, metabolic abnormalities

Pharmacological management

No drug has proven mortality benefit for ICU delirium (MIND-USA, HOPE-ICU trials).

  • Agitated delirium: Haloperidol 2.5–5mg IV q8–12h (monitor QTc <500ms); quetiapine 25–50mg PO/NGT q12h as alternative
  • Hypoactive delirium: No drug proven — focus on non-pharm; quetiapine may help
  • Dexmedetomidine: Reduces delirium duration in some studies; consider as sedative in agitated delirium

Special Situations

ARDS / high ventilator support

Deeper sedation (RASS −2 to −3) required to achieve ventilator synchrony and allow proning. Propofol or midazolam + fentanyl. Add NMB if severe dyssynchrony persists.

Status epilepticus in ICU

Refractory status: propofol, midazolam, or ketamine infusion as anaesthetic agents. Titrate to EEG burst suppression. Keep on continuous EEG monitoring.

Liver failure

Avoid benzodiazepines (hepatic encephalopathy, prolonged effect). Use fentanyl + dexmedetomidine. Propofol acceptable at low doses if no severe haemodynamic compromise.

Alcohol withdrawal

CIWA-Ar monitoring. Benzodiazepines are first-line (diazepam or chlordiazepoxide). IV thiamine before glucose. Propofol or phenobarbital for severe/refractory withdrawal.
