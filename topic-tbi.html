BTF 2023 — Key Changes in TBI Management

Brain Trauma Foundation Prehospital Guidelines 3rd Edition (2023)

The BTF 2023 update changes blood pressure thresholds for TBI. The old SBP >90 mmHg threshold is replaced with higher, more protective targets based on newer evidence from the Arizona EPIC initiative and large prehospital databases.

How TBI Works — Pathophysiology

Primary Brain Injury (Immediate — Unchangeable)

Direct mechanical injury at moment of trauma: axonal shearing (diffuse axonal injury — DAI), contusions, haematomas, lacerations. Occurs in microseconds. No medical intervention can reverse it. All ICU care focuses on preventing secondary brain injury.

Secondary Brain Injury (Hours to Days — Preventable)

The secondary injury cascade is triggered by the primary injury and evolves over hours to days. Every element is a potential treatment target:

  • Cerebral oedema: Vasogenic (BBB disruption → protein leak) + cytotoxic (cellular swelling from Na-K ATPase failure) → ↑ intracranial volume
  • Raised ICP: ↑ intracranial volume → ↑ ICP → ↓ CPP (CPP = MAP − ICP) → ↓ CBF → ischaemia → more oedema → vicious cycle
  • Excitotoxicity: Glutamate flood → NMDA receptor activation → Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial dysfunction → cell death
  • Inflammation: Microglial activation → cytokines → BBB breakdown → oedema amplification
  • Ischaemia: Haematoma compression, ↑ICP, hypotension, hypoxia, vasospasm — all reduce O₂ delivery to already-injured brain
  • Seizures: ↑ metabolic demand, excitotoxicity, cortical spreading depolarisations

The Monro-Kellie Doctrine

The skull is a rigid box. Total intracranial volume = Brain (80%) + CSF (10%) + Blood (10%) = CONSTANT. Any increase in one component must be compensated by decrease in another. Once compensatory mechanisms are exhausted → small increases in volume cause exponential rises in ICP → brainstem compression → death.

The 4 Absolute Killers in TBI — Prevent These
  • Hypotension: SBP <110 mmHg → ↓ CPP → ischaemia → secondary injury (BTF 2023: keep SBP ≥110)
  • Hypoxia: SpO₂ <90% → ↓ O₂ delivery to injured brain → ischaemia (even brief episodes are harmful)
  • Hyperventilation (prophylactic): ↓ PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → ↓ CBF → ischaemia
  • Hyperthermia: ↑ metabolic demand → ↑ ICP → worsens injury (aggressive fever management)

Primary Survey — ABCDE in TBI

In TBI, ABCDE must occur simultaneously. A is addressed while B and C are managed. The brain cannot tolerate ANY period of hypoxia or hypotension.

StepActionTBI-Specific Considerations
A — AirwayEnsure patent airway; C-spine protectionGCS ≤8: intubate. Use RSI. Assume C-spine injury in all high-velocity injuries. Manual inline stabilisation during intubation.
B — BreathingO₂, ventilationSpO₂ ≥90% mandatory (BTF: monitor continuously; correct immediately). EtCO₂ 35–45 mmHg — no prophylactic hyperventilation.
C — CirculationBP, haemorrhage controlSBP ≥110 mmHg (BTF 2023). Treat hypotension aggressively with fluids (isotonic). Control external haemorrhage. Cushing response (↑BP + ↓HR + irregular breathing) = imminent herniation.
D — DisabilityGCS, pupils, limb movementsGCS: open eyes/verbal/motor. Pupils: unilateral fixed dilated = uncal herniation (surgical emergency). Bilateral fixed dilated = bilateral herniation or death.
E — Exposure/EnvironmentTemp, spine, other injuriesPrevent hypothermia AND hyperthermia. Full spine clearance. Secondary survey for other injuries.

Blood Pressure & Oxygenation Targets — BTF 2023

BTF 2023 — Updated BP Targets (Strong Recommendation)
  • Adults: SBP ≥110 mmHg (optimal targets may be even higher — evidence evolving)
  • Children >6 years and adolescents: SBP ≥100 mmHg
  • Children 1–5 years: SBP ≥90 mmHg
  • Children 1–12 months: SBP ≥84 mmHg
  • Adults (Weak recommendation): SBP ≥110 provides better outcomes; avoid the threshold zone even below 110

Fluid choice for resuscitation: 0.9% Normal Saline (isotonic) — NOT hypotonic fluids (D5W, Ringer's Lactate in some formulations). Hypotonic fluids lower plasma osmolality → free water shifts into brain → worsens cerebral oedema.

Vasopressors: If BP unresponsive to fluids → noradrenaline. Maintain CPP ≥60 mmHg (ICP + MAP).

Oxygenation Targets

  • SpO₂ ≥90% (Strong recommendation — BTF 2023)
  • PaO₂ ≥60 mmHg
  • Monitor continuously with pulse oximetry — even brief desaturation episodes are profoundly harmful
  • Supplemental O₂ freely — hyperoxia does not worsen outcomes in TBI (unlike asthma/COPD)

ICP Management

ICP Monitoring — BTF Guidelines

ICP monitoring recommended for all severe TBI (GCS ≤8) with abnormal CT scan. Normal ICP: 7–15 mmHg. Treat ICP >22 mmHg (BTF threshold). Target CPP 60–70 mmHg.

Tier 1 — First-Line ICP Management

  • Head of bed 30° elevation; maintain neutral head position (no neck rotation)
  • Optimise analgesia and sedation (pain/agitation = ↑ ICP)
  • Normocapnia: PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg; EtCO₂ 35–40 mmHg
  • Normothermia: Treat fever aggressively (paracetamol + cooling)
  • Normoglycaemia: Target 140–180 mg/dL (hypoglycaemia severely harmful)
  • CSF drainage via EVD (if in situ)

Tier 2 — Osmotherapy (Second-Line)

Tier 3 — Refractory Elevated ICP

  • Barbiturate coma: Thiopentone or pentobarbital — burst suppression on EEG; only when all other measures failed; monitor cardiac function (profound myocardial depression)
  • Decompressive craniectomy: For refractory ICP >25 mmHg unresponsive to medical therapy — DECRA trial (modest ICP reduction, no mortality benefit); RESCUEicp trial (survival benefit but more vegetative state)
  • Hypothermia: 35–36°C — may reduce ICP but no overall mortality benefit (Eurotherm3235 trial); rewarming complications; use selectively

Specific Interventions

Seizure Prophylaxis

Levetiracetam 1000 mg BD × 7 days — first-line for post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis in severe TBI (GCS ≤8). Prevents early post-traumatic seizures (within 7 days) — NOT late seizures. Levetiracetam preferred over phenytoin (no cognitive impairment, no drug interactions).

DVT Prophylaxis

  • Mechanical: Graduated compression stockings + intermittent pneumatic compression from day 1
  • Pharmacological: Start LMWH after 24–48h IF intracranial haemorrhage is stable on repeat CT and no active bleeding

Nutrition

Early enteral nutrition within 24–48h. TBI patients are hypercatabolic. Target 25–30 kcal/kg/day. Avoid prolonged NPO — gut feeds maintain gut integrity and reduce infection risk.

Steroids

Steroids are CONTRAINDICATED in TBI

CRASH trial (2004, n=10,008): Methylprednisolone in TBI → INCREASED 14-day mortality. Strong evidence of harm. Do NOT give steroids for TBI under any circumstances.

Hyperventilation — Only as Rescue

Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35 mmHg) for imminent herniation (dilating pupil, Cushing's triad) as a BRIDGE to definitive treatment (mannitol, surgery, EVD). NOT for prophylaxis. Duration: minutes to hours only.

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — BTF 2023: SBP ≥110 mmHg Not ≥90

The old SBP >90 threshold is obsolete. BTF 2023 Strong recommendation: SBP ≥110 mmHg in adults. Evidence shows worse outcomes even with SBP 90–110. Keep the injured brain well-perfused.

💡 Pearl 2 — Steroids Kill in TBI

CRASH trial: methylprednisolone in TBI → increased mortality. Steroids are ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED in TBI. If you see a patient getting steroids for TBI, stop it immediately.

💡 Pearl 3 — Hyperventilation = Temporary Bridge Only

Prophylactic hyperventilation (PaCO₂ <35) causes cerebral vasoconstriction → ischaemia → worsens TBI. Use hyperventilation ONLY as emergency bridge to buy time for definitive treatment (EVD, mannitol, OR) when there's imminent herniation.

💡 Pearl 4 — Hypotonic Fluids are Contraindicated in TBI

Ringer's Lactate (slightly hypotonic) and D5W: AVOID in TBI. Hypotonic fluids lower plasma osmolality → free water shifts into brain → worsens cerebral oedema. Use 0.9% Normal Saline or hypertonic saline for resuscitation.

💡 Pearl 5 — Cushing's Triad = Surgical Emergency

Hypertension + Bradycardia + Irregular respirations = Cushing's triad = brainstem compression from raised ICP. This is a pre-terminal finding. Give mannitol 1 g/kg IV NOW and call neurosurgeon IMMEDIATELY. Brief hyperventilation as bridge.

💡 Pearl 6 — Levetiracetam Not Phenytoin

Levetiracetam 1000 mg BD × 7 days for post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis. Both work equally for early seizures, but levetiracetam has no cognitive side effects, no drug interactions, and no need for monitoring levels.

💡 Pearl 7 — "Talk and Die" — Lucid Interval in EDH

Extradural haematoma classically presents with lucid interval — initial concussion, brief LOC, then recovery (because EDH expands slowly at first), then rapid deterioration as haematoma grows and herniates. Young patients with temporal skull fracture: always rule out EDH even if GCS 15.

💡 Pearl 8 — CPP = MAP − ICP

Cerebral Perfusion Pressure = MAP − ICP. Target CPP 60–70 mmHg. If ICP = 20 and MAP = 80, CPP = 60 (adequate). If MAP drops to 70, CPP = 50 (inadequate — injury). Two levers: lower ICP or raise MAP.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — Giving Steroids for TBI

CRASH trial proved methylprednisolone increases mortality in TBI. Steroids are absolutely contraindicated. Never give dexamethasone or methylprednisolone for head injury.

❌ Mistake 2 — SBP Target of 90 mmHg

BTF 2023 updated this to SBP ≥110 mmHg in adults. The zone 90–110 is no longer considered adequate. Studies show worse neurological outcomes even when SBP stays above 90 but below 110.

❌ Mistake 3 — Prophylactic Hyperventilation

Routine hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) causes cerebral vasoconstriction → ischaemia. Only use for imminent herniation as a bridge to definitive treatment. Target normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35–45) in ICU.

❌ Mistake 4 — Using Hypotonic Fluids

Ringer's Lactate (slightly hypotonic at 254 mOsm/L vs plasma 285) lowers plasma osmolality → free water enters brain → worsens oedema. Use 0.9% NaCl or hypertonic saline for TBI resuscitation. KDIGO 2026 also recommends 0.9% NaCl for TBI specifically.

❌ Mistake 5 — Phenytoin for Seizure Prophylaxis

Levetiracetam is preferred over phenytoin for post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis. Phenytoin has cardiovascular side effects (hypotension with IV loading), drug interactions, cognitive impairment, and requires level monitoring. Same efficacy, worse side effect profile.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM)

Q: BTF 2023 SBP target in adult TBI? → SBP ≥110 mmHg (Strong recommendation). This is updated from the old ≥90 mmHg threshold. Optimal targets may be even higher.

Q: Steroid use in TBI? → ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED. CRASH trial: methylprednisolone → increased 14-day mortality in TBI. Never give steroids for head injury.

Q: What is CPP and target in severe TBI? → CPP = MAP − ICP. Target 60–70 mmHg. Treat ICP >22 mmHg and/or CPP <60 mmHg with escalating interventions (CSF drainage, osmotherapy, sedation, surgical).

Q: Drug of choice for post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis? → Levetiracetam 1000 mg BD × 7 days (prevents early post-traumatic seizures only — not late). Preferred over phenytoin.

Q: CT findings in EDH vs SDH? → EDH: Biconvex (lens-shaped), hyperdense, does NOT cross suture lines (bounded by dura attachment). SDH: Concave (crescent-shaped), crosses suture lines (no dural attachment).

Q: Why avoid Ringer's Lactate in TBI? → Slightly hypotonic (254 mOsm/L) → lowers plasma osmolality → free water shifts into brain → worsens cerebral oedema. Use 0.9% NaCl or hypertonic saline for TBI resuscitation.

Q: Cushing's triad significance? → Hypertension + Bradycardia + Irregular respirations = brainstem compression from severely raised ICP = imminent death. Emergency: Mannitol 1 g/kg IV + brief hyperventilation + call neurosurgeon immediately.
