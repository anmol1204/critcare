AKI Staging — KDIGO 2012

StageSerum Creatinine CriteriaUrine Output CriteriaApproximate Mortality
Stage 1× 1.5–1.9 baseline OR rise ≥ 0.3 mg/dL within 48h< 0.5 ml/kg/h for 6–12h~10–15%
Stage 2× 2.0–2.9 baseline< 0.5 ml/kg/h for ≥12h~20–30%
Stage 3× 3.0 baseline OR ≥ 4.0 mg/dL OR initiation of RRT OR eGFR <35 (age <18)< 0.3 ml/kg/h for ≥24h OR anuria ≥12h~40–60%

Use whichever criterion (creatinine OR urine output) gives the higher stage. Baseline creatinine: Lowest value within 3 months prior. If unavailable, estimate from MDRD assuming eGFR = 75 ml/min/1.73m².

First Step: Check the Urinary Catheter

Before any workup, confirm the catheter is patent and correctly positioned. Kinked, blocked, or incorrectly placed catheters cause iatrogenic post-renal AKI. Flush with 10 ml sterile water if doubt.

Nephrotoxic Drugs to Stop/Adjust in AKI

  • NSAIDs and COX-2 inhibitors
  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs (unless strongly indicated)
  • Aminoglycosides — switch to alternative if feasible
  • IV contrast — use iso-osmolar; pre-hydrate; avoid if AKI stage ≥2
  • Metformin — stop (lactic acidosis risk)
  • Spironolactone, eplerenone — hyperkalaemia risk
  • Amphotericin B — use liposomal form in AKI

Prevention of AKI in ICU

  • Haemodynamic optimisation: Maintain MAP ≥ 65 mmHg; avoid hypovolaemia and excessive positive fluid balance
  • Balanced crystalloids: Prefer Ringer's Lactate over 0.9% NaCl (SMART, SALT-ED trials)
  • Avoid nephrotoxins: Check all drug doses and interactions; avoid unnecessary contrast
  • Contrast nephropathy prophylaxis: IV hydration with 0.9% NaCl or sodium bicarbonate 1–1.5 ml/kg/h × 3–12h pre/post procedure; N-acetylcysteine 1200 mg PO BD before and after (weak evidence)
  • Glycaemic control: Target 140–180 mg/dL
  • Aminoglycoside dosing: Once-daily dosing reduces nephrotoxicity; monitor trough levels; avoid in existing AKI
  • Vancomycin: Monitor AUC/MIC (target AUC 400–600 mg·h/L); avoid concomitant piperacillin-tazobactam (↑ nephrotoxicity)

Conservative Management of AKI

  • Fluid balance: Avoid both hypovolaemia (worsens AKI) and fluid overload (>10% weight gain worsens outcome)
  • Diuretics: Furosemide for fluid overload management; does NOT prevent AKI or accelerate renal recovery (FACTT-AKI) — use only to manage volume
  • Nutritional support: Do NOT restrict protein in AKI — standard 0.8–1.2 g/kg/day; higher 1.5–2 g/kg/day on CRRT (amino acid losses)
  • Potassium management: K <5.5 mEq/L: dietary restriction, calcium resonium 15g PO TDS; K 5.5–6.5: add insulin-dextrose; K >6.5 or ECG changes: urgent RRT
  • Bicarbonate: Sodium bicarbonate infusion for severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.2) if RRT not immediately available — 1–2 mEq/kg slow IV infusion
  • Medication review: Dose-adjust all renally-cleared drugs (see drug dosing section)

Indications for Renal Replacement Therapy

Absolute Indications — Start Immediately (AEIOU Mnemonic)
  • Acidosis — metabolic acidosis pH <7.1 refractory to bicarbonate therapy
  • Electrolytes — hyperkalaemia K >6.5 mEq/L with ECG changes, or K >7.0 regardless
  • Ingestion — dialysable toxins (methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, salicylates, metformin)
  • Overload — pulmonary oedema refractory to diuretics
  • Uraemia — uraemic encephalopathy, uraemic pericarditis, uraemic bleeding, urea >150–200 mg/dL (25–33 mmol/L)

Timing of RRT — Standard Strategy (KDIGO / STARRT-AKI)

STARRT-AKI (2020) — Key Evidence

Accelerated RRT initiation (within 12h of AKI stage 2–3) vs standard strategy (based on absolute indications) showed no difference in 90-day mortality. The standard (indication-based) strategy is preferred — it avoids unnecessary RRT in patients who may recover. Do NOT initiate RRT prophylactically.

Standard strategy: Initiate RRT when at least ONE absolute indication (AEIOU) is present, or when AKI is Stage 3 with clinical deterioration despite conservative management.

RENAL Trial (2009) — Higher Dose Not Better

RENAL trial: Higher effluent dose (40 ml/kg/hr) vs standard (25 ml/kg/hr) — NO difference in 90-day mortality. Do not prescribe doses above 25 ml/kg/hr routinely. Higher doses increase cost without benefit.

Citrate Accumulation — How to Detect

Suspect if: Total Ca²⁺ / Ionised Ca²⁺ ratio > 2.5; unexplained metabolic alkalosis (pH >7.50); hypocalcaemia despite calcium replacement; worsening liver function. Action: Reduce citrate rate or switch to heparin anticoagulation.

Renal Recovery & Discontinuation of RRT

  • Attempt IHD or SLED weaning trial when patient is haemodynamically stable
  • Signs of recovery: improving urine output (>400 ml/day off RRT), falling creatinine, improving electrolytes
  • Trial off RRT: stop CRRT for 24–48h; if creatinine stable or improving and UO adequate → discontinue
  • If no renal recovery at 3 months → refer to nephrology for CKD management and potential long-term dialysis planning
  • Risk factors for non-recovery: older age, CKD at baseline, prolonged AKI, diabetic nephropathy, severe ATN
