How COPD Works — The Pathophysiology

COPD is a heterogeneous lung condition characterised by chronic respiratory symptoms (dyspnoea, cough, sputum) and persistent, progressive airflow limitation. Two overlapping mechanisms:

1. Small Airway Disease (the dominant mechanism)

Cigarette smoke, biomass fuel combustion (common in India — chulhas, crop burning), and occupational dusts trigger neutrophilic inflammation in small airways (<2 mm). This causes:

  • Goblet cell hyperplasia → mucus hypersecretion → airway plugging
  • Peribronchial fibrosis → fixed airway narrowing
  • Loss of elastic lung recoil (emphysema) → dynamic airway collapse on expiration
  • Airflow limitation that is NOT fully reversible (unlike asthma)

2. Emphysema (parenchymal destruction)

Protease-antiprotease imbalance (neutrophil elastase overwhelms α1-antitrypsin) → alveolar wall destruction → ↑ compliance → loss of elastic recoil → dynamic airway collapse → air trapping → hyperinflation. Hyperinflation pushes diaphragm flat → mechanical disadvantage → increased work of breathing.

Pathophysiology of Type 2 Respiratory Failure in COPD

  • Airflow obstruction → ↑ work of breathing → respiratory muscle fatigue
  • V/Q mismatch → hypoxaemia (poorly ventilated lung areas still perfused)
  • Hypercapnia: CO₂ retention from alveolar hypoventilation + V/Q mismatch
  • Chronic CO₂ retention → renal compensation (↑ HCO₃) → chronic respiratory acidosis compensated metabolically
  • Acute on chronic: During exacerbation → further ↑ airway resistance → acute hypercapnia on background of chronic compensated hypercapnia → dangerous acidaemia
Hypoxic Drive — The Myth

The "hypoxic drive" rationale for withholding O₂ in COPD is largely outdated. The real reason for controlled O₂ is the Haldane effect (O₂ displaces CO₂ from haemoglobin → apparent rise in PaCO₂) and abolition of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (↑ V/Q mismatch). Controlled O₂ to SpO₂ 88–92% is safe and necessary — withholding O₂ entirely is dangerous and wrong.

Exacerbation — Definitions (GOLD 2026)

An exacerbation of COPD is an acute event characterised by a worsening of the patient's respiratory symptoms that is beyond normal day-to-day variation and leads to a change in medication.

Causes of Exacerbation

  • Infection (80%): Viral (rhinovirus, influenza, RSV — 40%); bacterial (H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae, M. catarrhalis, Pseudomonas in severe COPD — 40%)
  • Environmental: Air pollution (PM2.5 — important in India), temperature changes, biomass smoke
  • Non-adherence to maintenance medications
  • Comorbidities: PE (5–10% of exacerbations), pneumothorax, cardiac failure

Score 1 point each for: Extended MRC dyspnoea score ≥5b | Eosinopenia (<0.05×10⁹/L) | Consolidation on CXR | Atrial Fibrillation | pH <7.30. Score 0–1: low risk (1.4% mortality); 2: medium (9.2%); 3–6: high (29% mortality)

ED and ICU Treatment

GOLD 2026 — Exacerbation Management Algorithm
  • 1. Controlled Oxygen — SpO₂ target 88–92% (NOT 100%) — see below
  • 2. Short-acting bronchodilators — SABA ± SAMA (ipratropium) via pMDI+spacer or nebuliser
  • 3. Systemic corticosteroids — Prednisolone 40 mg OD × 5 days (GOLD recommendation)
  • 4. Antibiotics — if purulent sputum or radiological evidence of pneumonia, or on MV
  • 5. NIV — if pH <7.35 and PaCO₂ >45 with adequate consciousness (FIRST-LINE over intubation)
  • 6. Invasive ventilation — if NIV fails or contraindicated

Controlled Oxygen Therapy — The Critical Distinction

Never Give 100% O₂ in COPD Exacerbation

Target SpO₂ 88–92% in COPD with known or suspected hypercapnia. High-concentration O₂ causes: (1) Haldane effect — O₂ displaces CO₂ from Hb → apparent ↑ PaCO₂; (2) Abolished hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction → worsened V/Q mismatch → ↑ dead space → ↑ CO₂ retention; (3) Blunted respiratory drive. Use 24% or 28% Venturi mask (reliable FiO₂) or 2–4 L/min nasal prongs.

NIV in COPD Exacerbation — First-Line

GOLD 2026 — NIV Indications (Evidence A)

NIV should be the first mode of ventilatory support in COPD exacerbation with:

  • pH <7.35 AND PaCO₂ >45 mmHg (respiratory acidosis)
  • OR respiratory rate >25/min despite maximal medical treatment
  • OR SpO₂ <88% despite controlled O₂

NIV reduces: mortality, intubation rate, length of hospital stay, treatment failure vs medical therapy alone (Evidence A).

NIV Response Assessment

  • Check ABG at 1–2h after initiation
  • Success: pH improving, PaCO₂ falling, RR falling, SpO₂ target reached, patient tolerating mask
  • Failure criteria: pH worsening after 1–2h, altered consciousness, unable to tolerate mask, copious secretions, haemodynamic instability → intubate without delay

Contraindications to NIV in COPD

  • Respiratory arrest or impending arrest
  • Severe haemodynamic instability (cardiogenic shock)
  • Inability to protect airway (excessive secretions, GCS <8)
  • Facial trauma preventing mask fit
  • Uncooperative patient who cannot tolerate mask

Antibiotic Indications in COPD Exacerbation (GOLD 2026)

  • Purulent (green/yellow) sputum — antibiotics reduce treatment failure and mortality
  • On mechanical ventilation (NIV or invasive) — always give antibiotics
  • Radiological pneumonia — treat as CAP
  • Clear/white sputum without other indicators — antibiotics not routinely needed
  • PCT <0.1 ng/ml — antibiotics unlikely to benefit

Invasive Mechanical Ventilation in COPD

Indications for Intubation

  • NIV failure (pH worsening after 1–2h, clinical deterioration)
  • NIV contraindicated
  • Respiratory arrest
  • GCS <8 or inability to protect airway

Ventilator Settings for COPD

Ventilator Strategy — Prioritise Expiration
  • Mode: Volume Control AC (Volume Assist-Control)
  • Tidal Volume: 6–8 ml/kg IBW
  • Rate: 10–14/min (low — allow complete expiration)
  • I:E ratio: 1:3 to 1:4 (prolonged expiratory time)
  • PEEP: 3–8 cmH₂O (set at ~80% of auto-PEEP level to counterbalance intrinsic PEEP)
  • FiO₂: Target SpO₂ 88–92%
  • Accept: Permissive hypercapnia — allow PaCO₂ to rise gradually toward patient's chronic baseline (NOT normal range)
  • Key goal: Correct acidaemia (pH), NOT normalise PaCO₂

Important: Target Chronic Baseline, Not Normal CO₂

COPD patients are chronically hypercapnic with renal compensation (elevated HCO₃⁻). Their baseline PaCO₂ may be 50–60 mmHg with pH 7.40. If you ventilate them to PaCO₂ 40 mmHg (overventilate), alkalosis develops → renal HCO₃ excretion → post-extubation hypercapnia and weaning difficulty. Target their chronic baseline CO₂, not 40 mmHg.

Clinical Pearls

💡 Pearl 1 — NIV Before Intubation in COPD

NIV should be tried FIRST in COPD with pH <7.35 and PaCO₂ >45. Evidence A: NIV reduces mortality, intubation rate, and ICU LOS. If you intubate a COPD patient who could have had NIV first, you may be creating a ventilator-dependent patient.

💡 Pearl 2 — Control O₂, Don't Withhold It

Don't withhold oxygen from a hypoxic COPD patient — that's dangerous. Titrate it carefully. Use Venturi mask (24% or 28%), target SpO₂ 88–92%. Check ABG 30–60 min after starting O₂ to detect CO₂ rise.

💡 Pearl 3 — 5 Days of Steroids is Enough

GOLD 2026 confirms: Prednisolone 40 mg × 5 days = equivalent to longer courses for COPD exacerbation. Reduces treatment failure, hospitalisation, and LOS. Shorter courses = fewer side effects (hyperglycaemia, myopathy).

💡 Pearl 4 — Set PEEP at 80% of Auto-PEEP

Patients with severe COPD on MV have significant intrinsic auto-PEEP due to dynamic hyperinflation. Adding extrinsic PEEP at 80% of auto-PEEP level counterbalances intrinsic PEEP, reducing patient effort to trigger breaths. Measure auto-PEEP with expiratory hold manoeuvre.

💡 Pearl 5 — India: Biomass = COPD

In India, 40–50% of COPD in women is due to biomass fuel exposure (chulhas, wood fires). Ask about cooking fuel type. These patients often present younger, with severe disease, and may not smoke. Standard GOLD ABE grouping still applies.

💡 Pearl 6 — Rule Out PE in Exacerbation

PE causes 5–10% of COPD exacerbations. Suspect if: minimal purulent sputum, disproportionate dyspnoea, RV strain on ECG, elevated D-dimer, no infection trigger. CT-PA if clinical probability is intermediate-high.

💡 Pearl 7 — GOLD 2026: 1 Moderate Exacerbation = Escalate

New in GOLD 2026: even ONE moderate exacerbation in the past year is enough to classify as GOLD E and escalate to triple therapy (LAMA + LABA + ICS) if eosinophils ≥300. Previous threshold was ≥2 moderate exacerbations.

💡 Pearl 8 — Don't Over-Ventilate COPD

COPD patients' normal PaCO₂ may be 55–65 mmHg. Ventilating them to PaCO₂ 40 creates metabolic alkalosis + renal HCO₃ excretion. On extubation attempt, they will have high CO₂ drive without the buffer → fail immediately. Target their chronic baseline CO₂.

Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake 1 — 100% O₂ via Non-Rebreather Mask

High-concentration O₂ in COPD causes CO₂ retention (Haldane effect + V/Q mismatch). Always use controlled O₂ with Venturi mask, target SpO₂ 88–92%. Check ABG 30–60 min after any O₂ change.

❌ Mistake 2 — Intubating Before Trying NIV

NIV has Evidence A for COPD exacerbation with respiratory acidosis. Always try NIV first unless contraindicated. Even partial responders benefit. Intubation in COPD often leads to prolonged ventilator dependence.

❌ Mistake 3 — Targeting Normal CO₂ on Ventilator

Ventilating a chronically hypercapnic COPD patient to PaCO₂ 40 mmHg creates alkalosis + renal bicarbonate wasting. On extubation, CO₂ rises but there's no buffer → failed extubation, ventilator dependence. Target the patient's chronic CO₂ baseline.

❌ Mistake 4 — Giving Antibiotics for Every Exacerbation

GOLD 2026: antibiotics only if purulent sputum, on MV, or radiological pneumonia. Clear sputum exacerbations do NOT benefit from antibiotics. Overuse drives AMR — major problem in Indian ICUs.

❌ Mistake 5 — Not Escalating Treatment After 1 Exacerbation

GOLD 2026 changed the threshold: ONE moderate exacerbation in the past year = consider stepping up to LAMA + LABA + ICS (if eosinophils ≥300). Previous approach waited for 2 exacerbations. Earlier escalation prevents future events.

Exam Pearls (DrNB / PDCC / IFCCM)

Q: Oxygen target in COPD exacerbation? → SpO₂ 88–92%. Use Venturi mask (24% or 28%). Check ABG 30–60 min after initiation. Avoid high-concentration O₂ (worsens hypercapnia via Haldane effect).

Q: First-line ventilatory support in COPD with pH 7.28 and PaCO₂ 70 mmHg? → NIV (BiPAP), IPAP 10–16 cmH₂O, EPAP 4–6 cmH₂O. Check ABG at 1–2h. If pH improving → continue NIV. Evidence A for reducing mortality and intubation rate.

Q: Duration of steroids in COPD exacerbation per GOLD 2026? → Prednisolone 40 mg OD × 5 days. 5 days = equivalent to longer courses. Reduces treatment failure and LOS.

Q: GOLD 2026 change regarding exacerbation threshold? → ONE moderate exacerbation now triggers treatment escalation (previously ≥2). Goal is achieving "low disease activity state" with no exacerbations.

Q: Why do you NOT target PaCO₂ 40 mmHg when ventilating COPD? → COPD patients are chronically hypercapnic with compensatory elevated HCO₃⁻. Ventilating to PaCO₂ 40 creates alkalosis → renal HCO₃ excretion → on extubation, CO₂ rises with no buffer → failed weaning.

Q: PEEP setting in ventilated COPD with auto-PEEP? → Set extrinsic PEEP at ~80% of measured auto-PEEP to counterbalance intrinsic PEEP, ease triggering, and reduce work of breathing.
