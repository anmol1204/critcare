Hyponatraemia (Na <135 mEq/L)

Most dangerous pitfall

Correction rate matters as much as the target. Overcorrect and you risk osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS/CPM). Undercorrect symptomatic acute hyponatraemia and risk herniation.

Acute symptomatic (<48h, seizures/coma/herniation)

  • 3% NaCl 150 ml IV over 20 minutes — repeat up to 3 times until symptoms resolve
  • Target: raise Na by 4–6 mEq/L in first hour to resolve symptoms
  • Then slow down — do not exceed 10–12 mEq/L rise in 24h total

Chronic hyponatraemia (or unknown duration)

  • Correct no faster than 8–10 mEq/L per 24h, max 18 mEq/L per 48h
  • Hypovolaemic: isotonic saline + treat underlying cause
  • Euvolaemic (SIADH): fluid restriction (800–1000 ml/day); consider 3% NaCl if symptomatic
  • Hypervolaemic (HF, cirrhosis): fluid restriction, treat underlying; consider tolvaptan (V2 antagonist) with caution (hepatotoxicity in cirrhosis)
SIADH Diagnostic Criteria
  • Plasma osmolality <275 mOsm/kg
  • Urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg
  • Urine Na >30 mEq/L (on normal salt intake)
  • Euvolaemic
  • Normal thyroid, adrenal function

Hypernatraemia (Na >145 mEq/L)

Almost always due to free water deficit. Common in ICU: insensible losses, diabetes insipidus, insufficient enteral water.

Management

  • Calculate free water deficit: FWD (L) = 0.6 × weight × [(Na/140) − 1]
  • Replace with D5W or 0.45% NaCl IV, or free water via NGT
  • Correct no faster than 10–12 mEq/L per 24h (risk of cerebral oedema)
  • Central DI: DDAVP 1–4 mcg SC/IV BD; treat underlying cause

Hypokalaemia (K <3.5 mEq/L)

Mild3.0–3.5Oral/NGTKCl 40–80 mEq/day in divided doses

Always correct Magnesium first

Hypokalaemia is refractory to K+ replacement if Mg2+ is low. Magnesium is essential for intracellular K+ retention. Check and correct Mg before/alongside K+.

Maximum peripheral IV KCl: 40 mEq/hr (highly irritant — use central line for higher rates). Always on cardiac monitor during IV replacement. Continuous infusion preferred over boluses.

Hyperkalaemia (K >5.5 mEq/L)

Stepwise management
  • 1. Stabilise the myocardium (if ECG changes): Calcium gluconate 10ml of 10% IV over 10 min — repeatable. Effect within 1–3 min, lasts 30–60 min.
  • 2. Shift K+ into cells: Insulin 10U + 50ml 50% dextrose IV; Salbutamol nebuliser 10–20mg; NaHCO3 50–100 mEq if metabolic acidosis present
  • 3. Remove K+ from body: Furosemide IV (if urine output); Calcium resonium 15–30g 4-hourly PO/PR; Patiromer/ZS-9 if available; Dialysis (CRRT/IHD) if refractory or anuric
  • 4. Stop causative drugs (ACEi, ARBs, K-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, heparin)

ECG changes in hyperkalaemia (progressive)

Peaked T waves → widened QRS → PR prolongation → loss of P waves → sine wave pattern → VF/asystole. Any ECG change → treat aggressively.

Hypomagnesaemia (Mg <0.75 mmol/L)

Extremely common in ICU (30–60% prevalence). Causes: diarrhoea, aminoglycosides, amphotericin, diuretics, PPI use, sepsis, malnutrition.

Clinical effects

  • Refractory hypokalaemia and hypocalcaemia
  • Cardiac arrhythmias (QTc prolongation, TdP, VF)
  • Neuromuscular: tremor, tetany, seizures
  • Respiratory muscle weakness

Replacement

  • Asymptomatic mild: oral magnesium oxide/hydroxide 400–800 mg/day (poorly absorbed — causes diarrhoea)
  • Symptomatic or Mg <0.5 mmol/L: MgSO4 1–2g (8–16 mEq) IV over 15–60 min; may repeat
  • Torsades de pointes: MgSO4 2g IV push over 2 minutes
  • Ongoing losses: MgSO4 4–8g/day IV infusion; maintain via NGT if possible

Hypophosphataemia (PO4 <0.8 mmol/L)

Severe hypophosphataemia (<0.3 mmol/L) causes respiratory muscle weakness, impaired weaning from ventilator, haemolytic anaemia, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac dysfunction. Most commonly seen in refeeding syndrome.

Refeeding syndrome prevention

  • Identify high-risk patients: prolonged fasting >5 days, malnutrition, chronic alcohol, weight loss >15%
  • Start feeds at 10–20 kcal/kg/day and increase slowly
  • Give thiamine 200–300 mg/day before and during refeeding
  • Monitor PO4, K, Mg, glucose daily for first week

Hypercalcaemia (Ca >2.6 mmol/L)

Mnemonic: Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans (bone pain, renal stones, abdominal groans, psychic moans). Causes: PTH-mediated (hyperparathyroidism) or PTH-independent (malignancy, vitamin D toxicity, sarcoidosis, thiazides).

Severe hypercalcaemia (>3.5 mmol/L or symptomatic)

  • IV fluids: 0.9% NaCl 1–2L over 2–4h, then 200–300 ml/hr to maintain UO 100–150 ml/hr
  • Loop diuretic: Furosemide 40–80 mg IV after adequate hydration (do NOT give before rehydration)
  • Bisphosphonates: Zoledronic acid 4mg IV over 15 min — most potent; effect in 48–72h, lasts weeks
  • Calcitonin: 4–8 IU/kg SC q12h — fastest onset (4–6h), tachyphylaxis after 48h; use as bridge
  • Steroids: Prednisolone 40–60mg/day — effective in lymphoma, vitamin D toxicity, sarcoidosis
  • Denosumab: 120mg SC for malignancy-related hypercalcaemia refractory to bisphosphonates
  • Dialysis: Severe refractory or AKI — low-calcium dialysate
